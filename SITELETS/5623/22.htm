Faculty in Higher Education
    < b>Description/Role

Faculty in higher education are responsible for the teaching and advising college students. In addition, faculty perform research in their field of expertise as well as act as consultants to various organizations in government and to businesses and people within the community. 

It is customary for faculty to teach more than one course at the undergraduate or graduate level. Faculty may teach both graduate and undergraduate students and could teach small seminar courses or teach in large lecture halls for hundreds of students. Beyond preparation for lectures and grading papers, faculty work with students individually in the capacity as an advisor. Faculty are also responsible for the supervision of graduate teaching assistants in the classroom and oversee research in preparation for thesis or dissertation. 

A commitment to life-long learning is essential for faculty as current knowledge of a chosen field is necessary for dissemination of knowledge to students. Faculty perform research in their own discipline of expertise and contribute to the existing knowledge by collaborating with other professionals at professional conferences and the publication of research in scholarly journals. 

Beyond teaching and research, Fac ulty are also members of academic committees that update and maintain policies of the university and departmental committees, which deal directly with academic requirements and curricula. 

The division of faculty time spent on teaching, research, and other duties depends on the university and the level of attainment within the faculty hierarchy the faculty member has achieved. 

Requirements in Texas (other states may vary)

  • Ph.D.
  • Ability to communicate and relate to all students, regardless of age or background
  • Enjoy working with students and provide motivation for success
  • Be committed to the pursuit and dissemination of knowledge
  • Self-motivated
  • Work with little or no supervision

University Faculty consists of four levels of tenure track position

  • Professor
  • Associate Professor
  • Assistant Professor 
  • Instructor

Tenure can be achieved after a faculty member has served for a specific time period (usually 7 years). At the end of the specified time perio d the faculty's work is reviewed and tenure is granted or denied based on contributions to teaching and research and the general contributions to the university. Tenure is desirable for most faculty as it allows freedom to teach and conduct research without concern for repercussions because of advocacy of unpopular thoughts, ideas, or opinions.

Job Conditions

Benefits in Texas (other states may vary):

  • Insurance varies with type of institution
  • Most universities require 9 month contracts
  • Access to campus facilities
  • Tuition waivers to dependents
  • Housing and travel allowances
  • Paid sabbatical leaves

Pay Scale in Texas (other states may vary):

Earning vary according to rank of faculty, type of institution, geographical location, and field of discipline 

1999-2000 - average salaries
Professors $76,200
Associate professors $55,300
Assistant professors $45,600
Instructors $34,700

Salary for faculty in public institut ions was lower than private, but higher than religious institutions. In disciplines with high-paying nonacademic alternatives, faculty salaries were higher. In areas such as humanities and education salaries are lower.

Hours: Flexible schedules/hours; Present for classes; Established office hours; Scheduled committee meetings
