   Long-Term Follow-Up of a Mandated Program for Divorcing Parents 
    A Research Review

Kramer, L., & Kowal, A. (1998). Long-term follow-up of a court-based intervention for divorcing parents. Family and Conciliation Courts Review, 36(4), 452-465.

Introduction

In response to the clear evidence of the negative impacts of divorce on children, the development of court-mandated programs for divorcing parents has been underwa y for more than two decades in nearly all states (Clement, 1999; Geasler & Blaisure, 1999). Parent-focused, court-mandated educational programs have sought to lessen the negative impacts of divorce on children and reduce divorce litigation (Geasler & Blaisure, 1998). Few of these programs, however, have been subject to empirical and long-term evaluation. Because most of the programs are brief (2-4 hour, one-session classes) and court-mandated, some researchers have expressed concern about program effectiveness for both the short- and long-term (Emery, 1999; Hughes & Kirby, 2000).

The Children First program of Belleville, Illinois, represents one of the few educational interventions for divorcing parents that has undergone empirical, short-term, and long-range evaluation. The study is a good example of the importance of impact evaluation, the final stage in Francine Jacobs' (1988) five-tiered model of evaluation for family programs. After a three-month follow-up assessment of the program was conducted, researchers followed the original participants six years later to evaluate the long-term effectiveness of the Children First program (Kramer & Washo, 1993). The study's main purpose was to determine if the program facilitated more harmonious coparenting over time and lowered the incidence of parents' returning to the courts to relitigate decisions regarding support, visitation, and custody.

Kramer & Washo (1993) viewed reitigation rates as an indirect measure of the extent of interparental conflict that continues postdivorce, as well as a measure relevant to the interests of an overburdened court system. Evidence that an educational intervention is associated with reducing relitigation could provide strong support for communities initiating or continuing similar mandated programs.

Method

< p>The participants in the initial sample were 168 parents who filed for divorce in 1990-1991 and attended the Children First program, compared with a control group of 43 divorcing, non-attending parents from a nearby county. Two trained research teams systematically reviewed court records in both counties to determine how program participation influenced relitigation rates. Information was available for 159 of the 168 parents in the experimental group and for all 43 parents in the control gr oup. 

The original program participants and controls were also mailed a brief follow-up questionnaire in the summer of 1996. The authors reported significant difficulties in locating the participants and controls due to frequent moves and name changes. The response rate was 31% for the experimental group and 23% for the control group. The measures of interest from the self-report questionnaire were interparental conflict, triangulation of children into marital conflict, and adaptive parenting.

Results

Although the analyses were narrow in scope due to the low return of the questionnaires, several correlates of relitigation were established: Those most likely to relitigate among the program participants were those who reported that their spouse had currently engaged in more triangulating behaviors and had a lower quality coparent relations hip. The parents' perceived levels of adjustment for both themselves and their children were not found to be correlated with frequency of relitigation.

The findings provided no evidence that the rates of relitigation were reduced following the introduction of the Children First program. Furthermore, participants in the program were as likely to engage in divorce relitigation as the controls. However, when the authors correlate d relitigation rates with reported family processes, they found divergent relitigation patterns. The researchers placed the parents into subgroups based on who reported high or low levels of interparental conflict, triangulation of children in postmarital conflict, and adaptive parenting. The parents in the experimental group who relitigated less often were those who reported to have engaged in relatively higher levels of interparental conflict, triangulating their children, and l ess adaptive parenting. 

Discussion

The results of the more detailed, family-level analysis indicated that the Children First program may be particularly helpful for at risk parents. Those who reported higher levels of interparental conflict, triangulating children in postmarital conflict, and lower levels of adaptive parenting appeared to benefit the most from program attendance. The at risk parents who attended the program had lower relitigation rates over a six year period than the controls. This corroborated earlier results of Kramer and Washo (1993) that the Children First program appeared to be most beneficial to high-conflict families.
