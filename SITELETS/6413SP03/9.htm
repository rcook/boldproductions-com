   Caregiving: The Changing Face of Families
    A Research Review

Introduction

A next consequence for families is increased caregiving for older family members, thus changing family roles.

According to The Senior Source (2003), nearly one out of every four households (22.4 million households) is involved in caregiving to persons aged 50 and over (in 2000, 50% of Americans were 50 or older). Forty-one percent of older caregivers also have children. Part of the 'sandwich genera tion' (being the primary caregiver of parents and still raising children), many women will spend more years caring for a parent than they do raising a child. One out of three people will have their life affected by some form of dementia, either in themselves or a loved one and the patient will live 8 to 20 years (or more) after the first symptoms, usually in good health physically but not cognitively. This paper presents a few of the issues that caregivers, particularly those of the baby boom generation, will have to confront as their parents age. The concept of family is being redefined as medicine and technology increase the life expectancy of humans. Research reveals that the longer one lives, the better chance of developing some form of dementia. 

Caregiving Issues

In light of the above statistics, the aging baby boom generation, the med ical world, and families must address and develop plans for the issues of caregiving. While there are many facets of caregiving, this paper will focus on cultural issues, depression and gender, costs, and euthanasia/end-of-life. 

Cultural

The baby boom generation, generally recognized as comprising the birth years of 1946 to 1964, faces issues that their parents did not. Many baby boomers who waited later in life to have children are still in the process of raising them and, at the same time, have become caregivers of their aging parents, usually while working full time. This status of dual roles is known as the sandwich generation. Baby boomers' parents were less likely to have had any long-term care issues for their parents due to earlier death. 

Depression and Caregiving

Ethnic differences also show to play a part in caregiving issues. For example, Janevic & Connell (2001) reported that White caregivers tended to report greater depression and viewed caregiving as more stressful than did African American caregivers.

Depression is one of the greatest stress factors of caregiving. Neundorfer, McClendon, Smyth, Stuckey, Strauss and Patterson (2001) showed in their res earch that when there was an increase in depression in the Alzheimer patient, depression also increased in the caregiver for that patient. Gender also played a role in that other variables showed the caregiver was female, a spouse rather than a non-spouse, and was in poor health herself. An increase in the patient's dependency in the Instrumental Activities of Daily Living (ADLs) was a significant predictor of increased depression level in the caregiver. The results of this study shows a seldom-examined patient characteristic: depressive symptoms. These symptoms are called primary stressors and contribute to caregiver depression. Treatment of patient depressive symptoms may improve the quality of life for patients and for their family caregivers. The study confirmed the need for interventions for caregivers early in their family member's illness when the caregiver's depression may be high due to their family member's depression and when initial signs indica te that their family member is becoming more dependent.

Powers, Gallagher-Thompson and Kraemer (2002) examined the longitudinal relationship between depressive symptoms and coping strategies in older adult primary caregivers of non-institutionalized Alzheimer's patients. Results suggested that a strong stable component is present in caregiver coping styles. Caregiver intervention researchers may want to examine the extent to wh ich commonly used outcome measures are assessing stable caregiver traits rather than state-dependent distress. The study showed that different aspects of coping have very high stable characteristics with very little evidence that caregivers change their style of coping when trying to adapt to their family member's increased dependency on them. In other words, the caregiver's coping measures remained the same even as their family member's health and mind deteriorated with the Alzheimer's Disease (AD), pres enting the question of whether coping measures should change as the caregiving becomes more intense emotionally, physically and psychologically. This study should be considered in planning future longitudinal studies with caregivers because the stability in the coping measures of the caregiver may underestimate the potency of interventions.

According to Gallagher-Thompson, Canto, Jacob and Thompson (2001) research is needed to find out if adult daughter s demonstrate more negative interpersonal interactions than wives due to their generally higher levels of life stress as members of the 'sandwich generation' in interaction patterns with AD family members and interactions with non-AD family members. There is a need for more research on caregiving (especially with AD) in multi-generational families. Increased knowledge in these areas could have important implications for patient care and family stress.

Li eberman and Fisher (2001) showed that female family caregivers and spouses displayed greater declines in health and well-being over time, compared to other family caregivers whether institutionalization of the Alzheimer's patient was chosen or not.

Does gender play a significant role in caregiver depression? For example, a daughter being the caregiver of her father? Or a son being the caregiver of his mother? Personal caregiving (bathing, grooming, dr essing, health issues) brings about different issues when the patient and the caregiver are not of the same gender and the caregiver is not a spouse. Purchasing formal services is one option but money isn't always available. 

Costs

In research based on the National Longitudinal Caregiver Study, Moore, Zhu, and Clipp (2001) showed the annual cost of providing informal care to elderly commun ity-dwelling veterans (males) with dementia to be $18,385 per patient in 1998. Caregiving time was placed at $6,295 and caregiver's lost earnings were estimated at $10,709. Out-of-pocket expenses annually for formal care totaled almost $4,500; $4,320 was identified for other services (e.g., laundry, barber, clothes). 

All aspects of costs increased with disease severity and problem behavior. Most of the cost increase came from the increased care giving time required for the provision of physical care. This study provided on a national level costs associated with providing informal care by female caregivers to community-dwelling elderly male veterans with dementia, examining caregivers' lost income, caregivers' out-of-pocket costs of purchasing formal care, and caregivers' excess health costs. It also examined what conditions in the course of dementia lead certain caregivers to relinquish their family member to institutions. When does the caregi ving become too much for them to handle? 

Euthanasia/End-of-Life

What effect does depression have on suicide statistics? According to a study by Roscoe, Malphurs, Dragovic and Cohen (2001), depressive disorders or depressive symptoms were reported in 13% of the euthanasia cases/physician-assisted suicides performed in the state of Oregon and gender and marital status appeared to influence decisions to seek an assisted death. Of the 69 euthanasia cases examined by one physician, 72% of the patients were women and most were non-Hispanic Whites who lived at home at the time of death. Research by Roscoe, Malphurs, Dragovic and Cohen also revealed that the availability of a caregiver may differentially affect the end-of-life decisions made by both men and women. Women provide the majority of care given and women who need care themselves tend to receive less assistance fro m family members than do men and are more likely to have to pay for assistance even if married.

Conclusion

More longitudinal research is needed in this field. The studies and questions mentioned in this paper bring out even more questions and concerns about the rapidly aging population, nationally and internationally, and one cannot help but wonder who will be caring for the baby boomers in the years to come. Optimistically, since this generation has been redefining culture since birth, we can look expectantly and hopefully for the caregiving transition to a large population to be not quite as painful or bleak as many predictions.
