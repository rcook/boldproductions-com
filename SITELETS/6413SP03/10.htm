   Family Resilience
    A Research Review

Resilience has been studied for well over two decad es. Increased awareness of the concept of resilience has developed since the events of September 11, 2001. A growing body of longitudinal studies provides concrete data that indicates that many children and adolescents experiencing great adversity can develop into confident, competent, and caring adults (Werner & Smith, 1992). The Kauai Longitudinal Study and the Iowa Youth and Family Project are examples of the many longitudinal studies that follow individuals over the course of a lifespan. These studies have consistently documented that between half and two-thirds of children growing up in families with risk factors such as mental illness, substance abuse, domestic violence or economic adversity do overcome the odds and demonstrate resilience (Ganong & Coleman, 2002). 

Conger and Conger (2002) have been working on a significant longitudinal research program called the Iowa Youth and Family Project (IYFP) since 1989. The Iowa project researchers define resilience as a process by which individuals, relationships and families adapt to major adversity. In particular, the Iowa project measured economic hardship and its impact on adaptation from childhood to adolescence and their transition from adolescence to adulthood. The initial study began when th e 558 adolescent participants were in 7th grade. Findings indicate involved parents with nurturing styles promote resilience to economic stress as well as successful adjustments in the life cycle. These findings emphasize the importance of prevention programming. By increasing effective parenting styles during childhood, positive adaptations throughout adversity and life transitions are more likely (Conger & Conger, 2002).

Werner and Smith's (1989) longitudinal study researched the lives of 505 participants living on the island of Kauai, Hawaii, for more than 40 years. These individuals were studied from 1955 throughout adulthood to determine the development of resilience in the face of adversity. The Kauai Longitudinal Study specifically evaluated the impact of factors such as economic disadvantages, natural disasters, stressful life events, and protective factors on the development of a diverse ethnic group. One of every three participants was born with a high number of risk factors. According to Werner and Smith (1989) one of every three of the same at-risk youth had developed into a confident, capable, and caring young adult by age 18. Successful adult adaptation was evident in the follow-ups performed at ages 32 and 40. The adolescents who had developed coping problems in their youth demonstrated a reversal of these challenges in adulthood. The research team found that at age 40, all but two of thes e individuals were still successful. Many of these individuals outperformed children from low-risk families. A significant finding was that resiliency can be developed at any point in the life course. Learning or behavioral problems exhibited at 18 had resolved in 50% of the children by age 30. They reported holding satisfying jobs and stable relationships.

Both studies evaluate protective factors and their impact on resiliency. A family's belief in their ability as a group to solve problems and cultivate resources to deal with life challenges may be the key point in the development of protective factors. Success in coping with one situation creates the basis for the understanding that the family will survive other situations (Seccomb, 2002). Involved caring and nurturing parents, mentors, teachers and the community were of significant importance in the ultimate success of the youths followed in these studies.
