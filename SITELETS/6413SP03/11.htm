   Management and Innovation:
Key Concepts in Institutional Transformation
    A Research Review

Great managers are critical to a corporation's success. According to Buckingham and Coffman (1999), not only do managers find, empower, and retain talented employees, but they also determine how productive an employee is and how long she will stay with the company. Profit sharing, vacation opportunities, and other benefits may influence an employee's decision to work for a company—but it is the manager who has the power to influence productivity and longevity. In the words of Buc kingham and Coffman, "Managers trump companies" (p. 36).

Buckingham and Coffman (1999) utilized twenty-five years of research of the Gallup Organization to evaluate productivity, profitability, retention, and customer satisfaction of a broad range of companies, industries, and countries. Gallup surveyed over a million employees, and conducted, recorded and transcribed over eighty thousand interviews with managers. The r esult was a survey of 12 key questions that directly link performance and employee engagement. 

Great managers, reported Buckingham and Coffman (1999), simply stopped trying to make people what they were not, and instead "try to help each person become more and more of who he already is" (p. 57). Buckingham and Coffman advocated that companies "keep every manager focused on the four co re activities of the catalyst role: select a person, set expectations, motivate the person, and develop the person" (p. 65). This transformation is brought about by The Four Keys.

The first key is to Select for Talent. Buckingham and Coffman (1999) defined talents as "recurring patterns of behavior" (p. 99). Talents make up the essence of our being. A manager must know first what talents she wants for the ta sk at hand. Then striving to reject a focus on pathology, she creates an environment in which the employee's talent can flourish. 

Define the Right Outcomes, the second key outlined by Buckingham and Coffman (1999), encompasses the basic managerial responsibility, which is to "focus people toward performance" (p. 110). When each person finds her own route to the clearly defined goal, the manager and employee are aligned in harmony. Responsibility is encouraged, and the employee is able to use his talents to best accomplish the task.

In the third key, Focus on Strengths, Buckingham and Coffman (1999) elicited a critical point. Instead of trying to perfect "the other," which is a focus on weakness, great managers intentionally focus on strengths. They spend the most time with their best people. Great managers also foc us on excellence and realize that "average thinking" limits performance (p. 162).

The final key is to Find the Right Fit. The responsibility of the manager, stated Buckingham and Coffman (1999), "is to steer the employee toward roles where the employee has the greatest chance of success" (p. 177). Rather than promoting employees to their level of incompetence, great managers lif t up the prestige in every role that is performed in excellence. In addition, Buckingham and Coffman suggested "broadbanding"—defining pay ranges in broad bands, with each level overlapping the level above and below. Broadbanding "gives teeth to [great managers'] commitment that every role, performed at excellence, will be valued" (p. 190).

Gallup's study covered a 25-year span 51;but it is a study of deductive reasoning rather than inductive application. The theory is radical and potentially transformational. But does it work in practice? 

Since 1997 International Paper has refocused its management agenda utilizing Gallup's survey and theory of management to drive strategic corporative change. Although IP has encountered resistance to change they have met difficult challenges. The shift in focus is beginning to pay off. Placing the best talent in the right place at the right time is leading to "wide-range innovations and a more compelling value proposition based on employee engagement" (Ashton, 2002, p. 30).

The impact of innovative management has been tested through the use of longitudinal data collected from 1994 to 1997 at Kraft Jacob Suchard, part of the Philip Morris Corporation. Hailey (2001) reported that corporate renewal throu gh innovative human resource management is possible, although it may take several years to achieve. Innovative management techniques strengthen employee relationships which then in turn fortify the company.

Similar to these reports, Dov Eden, Avolio, and Shamir (2002), posited that "Transformational leadership has been shown to have a positive relationship with performance" (p. 735). Leaders who are transformational wor k to elicit the full potential of each follower through motivation and the satisfaction of self-actualization needs. The impact of this leadership style was tested through a longitudinal, randomized field experiment including 54 military leaders, their 90 direct followers, and 724 indirect followers. The study concluded there is a positive impact on development and performance of those who follow transformational leaders.

Finally, perhaps Gallup itself adds to discovery. Fisher (2002) claimed that Gallup "practices what it preaches to its clients" (paragraph 5). The results are an increase in total revenues of 80% in just five years; an annual turnover rate at 12% (below the consulting industry average of 20%); an increase in stock from $60 in 1997 to $300 in 2002. These are rather convincing data. Fisher quoted Gallup's CEO, Jim Clifton who said, "'Maximizing the potential of a human being is not something they teach at Harvard or Wharton. It's an alien concept. But we're betting the company on it'" (paragraph 2).

Evoke the highest potential in each person. Treat people as they desire and with respect. Match talent and need to heighten productivity. Do these radical ideas transform corporative [intentional term] life? Perhaps the most important answer is my own. "Would I be willing to faithfully give my l ife's work to a company that worked to meet these goals?" My emphatic response: "You bet I would!"
