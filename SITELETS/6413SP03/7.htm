   —Ready— for School
    A Research Review

In many quality, child-initiated early care and education prog rams, parents are increasingly concerned that their preschool-aged children are not —ready— for school. In fact, many parents are pulling their children out of these quality programs and placing them in academically oriented programs instead.

Administrators and teachers of child-initiated programs are frustrated and concerned about these withdrawals. They are even more concerned when children are enrolled in an ove rly didactic, formal instructional environment (Elkind, 1986; Zigler, 1987). Such an environment has been shown to decrease positive conduct and work habits of children, while teachers perceive children to be more distractible, less willing to follow directions, and less pro-social (Hart, Charlesworth, Burts, & DeWolk, 1993).

A recent longitudinal study reveals robust research findings of positive benefits of child-initiated environments on school su ccess for young children. In the study, Moving up the Grades: Relationship Between Preschool Model and Later School Success, Rebecca Marcon (1999) revisited the cohort of low-income, minority children who had attended two years of school (preschool and Kindergarten) prior to entering first grade. These children were in a study of three divergent preschool models within an urban school system. The three models were child-initiated preschool classroo ms, academically directed preschool classrooms, and middle-of-the-road preschool classrooms.

In this follow up study, Marcon (1999) examined the transition from the children's fifth to sixth year in school. The type of preschool experience expected to allow for more school success was the child-initiated model. This expectation was based upon earlier findings for these children (Marcon) and results of other studies (Miller & Bizzell, 1984; Schwe inhart & Weikart, 1997; Schweinhart, Weikart, & Larner, 1986). Her findings give quality programs good research for advocating a child-initiated approach to learning. 

Data from the Year 6 transition indicated that children who participated in child-initiated preschool classrooms had significantly higher overall grades while their school performance improved or held constant in all but two subject areas (music, social studies). Grades of ch ildren from academically directed preschool classrooms declined in all but one subject area (handwriting). Marcon's (1999) interpretation of the finding is that in child-initiated learning environments independence and self-initiation are encouraged and that this provides a foundation for critical thinking skills needed for school success. 

Marcon's (1999) findings support quality child-initiated programs' goals for how best to prepare young c hildren for formal schooling while reassuring parents that their children will be —ready— for school.
