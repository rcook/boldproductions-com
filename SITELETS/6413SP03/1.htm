   A Two-Factor Model for Predicting When a Couple Will Divorce
    A Research Review

Regarding the issues of marriage and divorce, specifically predicting divorce, John Gottman is the leading researcher of our time. In A two-factor model for predicting when a couple will divorce: Exploratory analyses using 14-year longitudinal data, Gottman and Levenson (2002b) created a post hoc model using Time-1 data to predict the length of time the marriage will last. This approach addressed the timing or patterns of reason for divorce rather than quality or outcome of the marriage.< /p>

Gottman introduced the "four horsemen of the apocalypse" (criticism, defensiveness, contempt, and stonewalling) as a pattern of greater negativity than positivity during a conflict discussion (Gottman, 1994; Gottman 1999; Gottman & Levenson, 1992). Using more recent data, Gottman presented revision and extension of his work, suggesting "two patterns of affect regulation in couples headed for divorce: an emotionally inexpressive pattern that is low in both n egative and positive affect, and an emotionally volatile attack-defend pattern" (Gottman & Levenson, 2002a, p. 83). The researchers said that a "different pattern of Time-1 marital interaction predicts earlier versus later divorcing" (p. 83).

The sampling frame for Gottman and Levenson's research consisted of 197 couples who responded to newspaper advertisements and then completed a demographic questionnaire and two marital satisfaction measures. The final sample narrowed to 85 couples who participated in laboratory assessments and completed a number of additional questionnaires. The initial focus and time point of measurement for this sample is called Time-1. Participants were contacted four years later, Time-2, and yearly thereafter for 10 years. The Time-1 data consisted of an oral history interview, an observed interaction session in which the couples had three 15-minute conversations based on: the events of the day, conflict-resolution discussion of a problem area, and a pleasant topic. During discussions, physiological measures were obtained and video recorded the frontal views of each partner's face and upper torso. 

From the data collected and examined post hoc, Gottman and Levenson (2002b) speculated that there are two dysfunctional modes of marital interaction, an earlier divorcing attack-defend mode between spouses during conflict, and a later divorcing emotionally disengaged mode tha t is devoid of positive affect. The data further supported that the Time-1 information is inadequate to predict divorce versus marital stability; however, a review of the data post hoc revealed the two above-mentioned patterns. Couples who divorced earlier were initially expressive and had high levels of anger and wife negativity, while later divorcing couples were higher on indices of neutral affect and high on skin conductance level, which are also used by polygraphers as an index of suppression. 

DeKay, Greeno, and Houck (2002) argued that a duration model approach would have been a more appropriate study, examining all marriages, not just those couples who divorced. Gottman and Levenson (2002b) responded that this study was not an attempt to predict which marriages will end in divorce but rather an examination of the patterns that may have contributed to couple's divorcing after examining the data post hoc. 

Intense marital conflict would likely make it difficult to remain married for long while its absence would make it easier to stay; however, the absence of any affect may eventually take its toll. Two studies from the 1980s and 1990s found supporting results. The most common reason given for divorcing was an increasing distance and isolation between partners or long-standing disillusionment and disappointment in the marriage exacerbated by a mid-life crisis (Gigy & Kelly, 1992; Steinb erg & Silverberg, 1987).
