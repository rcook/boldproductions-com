   Temperament and Time 
    A Research Review

The value of longitudinal research methodology is never more justified and apparent than when approaching the study of temperament. The very definition of temperament as “stable behavior and emotional reactions that appear early in life” (Kagan, 1994, p. 40) implies an element of time, as researchers look for consistent patterns of behavior from infancy through adulthood. Beginning with the landmark study by Thomas and Chess in the 1956 New York Longitudinal Study of Temperament (Thomas, Chess, & Birch, 1968), social scientists have sought to identify the psychological traits that ma ke up the "basic building blocks of personality" (Buss & Plomin, 1984). Over the past forty years, two major approaches to studying temperament have emerged, both using longitudinal methods to uncover the origins and developmental patterns of consistent and inconsistent personality traits. 

The questionnaire/observation approach to longitudinal temperament research

Taking their cue from Thomas, Chess, and Birch (1968), many current researchers utilize a popular methodology for studying temperament: the use of parental questionnaires and checklists. With this approach, parents (and sometimes teachers) rate children’s attributes through the use of numerous temperament behavior scales. Andersson and Sommerfelt (1999) used the Infant Characteristic Questionnaire to examine whether dimensions of infant temperament as rated by parents predicted problem behav iors in 5-year-olds, finding only a modest correlation. Another recent longitudinal study assessed temperament in 93 children from infancy through age 9 years, relying on the Colorado Childhood Temperament Inventory and the Emotionality, Activity and Sociability Questionnaire, in combination with videotaped play situations, to analyze dimensions of preschool temperament’s stability through middle childhood (Hagekull & Bohlin, 1998).

The physiological approach to longitudinal temperament research

Recent temperament research was turned in a new direction through the ongoing work of Harvard University professor Jerome Kagan and his associates who have designed numerous longitudinal temperament studies based on the physiological components of temperament. Beginning with the premise that it is the underlying structure of the brain and its reaction to stimuli that heavily contribute to differences in human personality development (Kagan, 1994), these researchers have followed over 400 infants from the age of 4 months through 12 years, using a variety of empirical testing methods. From his initial research, Kagan classified 4-month-old infants into two groups, high reactive and low reactive, based on their physical responses to novel stimuli in a laboratory setting (Kagan). Children from these two groups have been followed in a series of correlational studies using a variety of physiological, empirical measurements at selected time periods. Kagan, Reznick, and Snidman (1987) studied the infants as toddlers, using observational indexes to indicate inhibited and uninhibited behaviors. Arcus and Kagan (1995) noted the difference in the craniofacial structure between high and low reactive toddlers at 14 and 21 months. Some of these same high reactive and low reactive infants were tested again as 4-year-olds for signs of inhibited and uninhibited behavior s, using quantitative measurements that included numbers of smiles and social initiations, along with approach withdrawal behaviors in experimental settings (Kagan, Snidman, & Arcus, 1998). Most recently, the infant cohort has been studied at the age of 10 -12 years with the use of innovative brain research techniques, in which brainstem responses to stimuli were compared to behavioral indicators of inhibition (Woodward, et al., 2001). The absence of s ubjective temperament questionnaires in these studies is notable, as these researchers continue to emphasize more objective, biologically based measurements for temperament identification and most importantly, for preservation of these traits over time as indicators of adult adjustment.

Forging links for the future

Kagan has combined a longitudinal approach and sophisticated physiological meas urements to add to the understanding of the uniqueness among and between humans. Stating that, "No human quality, physical or psychological, is free of the contribution of events within and outside the organism" (Kagan, 1994, p. 37), Kagan continues to push the direction of temperament research away from the purely subjective measures that have been the accepted norm of temperament research in the past decades. While never discounting the influence of pioneers Thomas and Chess (Thomas, Chess, & Birch, 1968) or Buss and Plomin (1984), Kagan’s use of longitudinal research and biological measurements to validate perceived qualitative differences in children that remain stable over time continues to add to the knowledge of the formation of personality traits.
