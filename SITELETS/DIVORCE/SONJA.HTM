  Divorce and Child Custody:
Looking at the Issu es Through Rainbow Colored Glasses
    Divorce is complicated enough with lawyers, mediation, child custody, alimony and more. Add to that already stressful situation a difference in sexual orientation for one of the married and previously practicing heterosexual partners and the battles take on a very different front. No longer is the divorce about the marriage, it is about the sexual orientation of a partner and if there are children involved, the psychological well being of the children in relation to that orientation. Though strides have been made toward eliminating court rulings based on heterosexual biases, many judicial systems, with gay and lesbian blinders on, continue to address and make family law decisions based on unrealistic and prejudici al stereotypes (Shapiro, 1996). Due to the limitation of space, the focus of this article is gay and lesbian family structures and their effects on children's psychological well being as factors that determine child custody rulings in divorce cases. 

Several court cases during the last decade have become pivotal in portraying the on-going battle regarding homosexual parents and child custody issues (i.e., White v. Thompson, 90; Chicoine v. Chicoine, 92 ; Pleasant c. Pleasant, 93; Van Driel v. Van Driel, 94; Bottoms v. Bottoms, 95). Much of the debate lingers today. As indicated in previous literature reviews, family law courts view divorce and child custody through a different lens when homosexuality becomes part of the dynamics (Falk, 1989; Patterson, 2001). Decisions are based on common assumptions related to homosexuality and its perceived effects on the family. Gay fathers and lesbian mothers are sometimes portrayed as unfit parents because it is assu med that their sexual orientation and conduct are the focus of their being, inseparable from their role as parent (Fajer, 1992). This double standard does not apply to heterosexual parents because their sexual orientation is not automatically viewed as a factor in their ability to parent. Because homosexuality and parenting have not been viewed as separate entities, negative biases have prevailed. Those biases have typically included assumptions that gay fathers and lesbian mothers cannot provide a stable environment for children and that their sexual orientation will have negative effects on the child's psychological well being. Other assumptions have been made and tested in previous reviews of the literature (Falk, 1989; Patterson, 2001). 

The statement "stable homosexual family environment" is viewed as an oxymoron by those conservatives and traditionalists who believe there is no such thing as a stable home environment th at includes a gay father or lesbian mother. This pretence is used in divorce cases based on the assumption that homosexuals have many sexual partners and frequently move in and out of relationships. Contrary to that belief, research has indicated that between 40 to 60% of gay men and 45 to 80% of lesbian women are in steady relationships (Peplau & Cochran, 1990; Peplau, Veniegas & Campbell, 1996). On a larger scale, in 2000, there were nearly 596,000 gay and lesbian unmarried partners residing as families (US Census Bureau, 2000). Reports of relationship satisfaction were consistent with findings of heterosexual relationship quality (Flaks, Ficher, Masterpasqua & Joseph, 1995; Peplau & Cochran, 1990), revealing greater relationship satisfaction for lesbian couples with children (Koepke, Hare & Moran, 1992). Though monogamy was viewed more favorably by lesbian and heterosexual couples than by gay couples, sexual satisfaction, an indicator of relationship quality, was found to be similar among all groups (Bryant & Demian, 1994). In studies comparing relationship longevity, break up rates for gay and lesbian couples were relatively low and comparable to heterosexual couples (Blumstein & Schwartz, 1983, Kurdek, 1995). In reviewing research on types of gay and lesbian relationships, levels of satisfaction and longevity, the findings reveal relationships that are characteristic of stable heterosexual home environments.

Another assump tion made by some traditionalists is the belief that children raised in the homes of gay and lesbian parents will suffer psychologically. In an attempt to be non-biased, some courts have utilized a nexus test in child custody cases. The nexus test requires an analysis of the conduct (in this case homosexuality) and its effect on the child in an attempt to rule in the best interest of the child; however, it is not consistently or effectively applied (Shapiro, 1996). Testing this assumption of psychologica l detriment to children, researchers have pursued studies that examine children's self-concept, peer relationships, and behavioral adjustment (Flaks, et al. 1995; Patterson, 1992, 1995, 1997), reporting normal levels of development in all areas. In a study of 37 four- to nine-year-old children of lesbian mothers, children reported feeling joyful, content and comfortable with their individual selves (Patterson, 1994). However, lower self-esteem was reported for children whose fathers rejected the mother's le sbianism (Huggins, 1989). Regarding the child's adjustment to the divorce and the parent's sexual orientation, researchers found that children who learned of the parent's homosexuality earlier in childhood were more likely to have higher self-esteem (Huggins 1989). Furthermore, consistent with findings from previous decades, children of gay fathers and lesbian mothers reported normal peer relations (Patterson, 1992; Tasker & Golombok, 1995). Earlier research reviewed children's interactions with the oth er parent (most commonly the father) and adults of both genders, finding they had established satisfactory relationships (Golombok, Spencer, & Rutter, 1983). Contrary to stereotypical beliefs, research has continually found that children of homosexual parents develop normally psychologically, similar to children raised by heterosexual parents.

The dynamics of divorce and child custody issues as they relate to gay fathers and lesbian mothers are multi faceted. This article has addressed only two of the assumptions that arise in family courts regarding these parents. However, current and past research has provided a strong case against the rulings against homosexual parents made in the courtroom. Further research should be conducted in this area, including studies that use more diverse populations and that address gay fathering issues, as well.
