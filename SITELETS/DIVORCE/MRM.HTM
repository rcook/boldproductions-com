  The Importance of Parent Mediation Courses
    Today, separation and divorce have become normal life events; yet all too often, the children are caught in the crossfire. Many divorces are characterized by high levels of conflict, including property disputes, custody battles, kidnappings, physical threats, stalking, harassment, verbal fights and name calling that continue for many years after the divorce (Hopper, 2001). It is estimated that each year more than 1 million children experience parental divorce (U.S. National Center for Health Statistics, 1991).

Over the past two decades, researchers have documented that both parents and children who experience the disruption of divorce are at increased risk of having problems with health and well-being. The evidence further suggests that the effects of marital conflict and divorce on children often persist into young adulthood (Amato & Booth, 1997). It is commonly assumed that two biological parents provide the optimal environment for healthy child development and that any deviation from this structure is problematic. Even temporary disruptions in parents' physical and psychological functioning during a divorce can interfere with their ability to provide support and supervision at a time when children need them most (Heatherington, Bridges, & Insabella, 1998). Divorce is a process that begins while parents are still living together and ends long after the divorce proceedings are over. The divorce process typically sets in motion many events that children experience as stressful. These stressors can increase the risk of negative emotional, behavioral, and health outcomes for children (Booth & Amato, 2001). 

Children, however, do not all react the same to divorce, but transiti on differently according to many factors such as age, developmental stages, temperament, economic impact, ongoing conflict and how parents manage the divorce (Furstenburg & Kiernan, 2001). Stable relationships with two caring adults can help children negotiate the important and frequent changes in their own lives. The loss of these resources is a distinct disadvantage to children and may result in stress overload. In general, research suggests that divorce may be beneficial or harmful to children, de pending on whether it reduces or increases the child's exposure to stress (Booth & Amato, 2001). Although conflict between divorced couples diminishes over time, it remains higher for couples with children as they work through co-parenting relationships and economic responsibilities (Heatherington, et al, 1998). 

Given the prevalence of divorce and the potential for adjustment problems, professionals have developed support services for the se families. In the early 1980's, mediation was introduced as a way to resolve custody and visitation disputes among parents seeking a divorce (Hughes & Kirby, 2000). In the past two decades, researchers have learned much about the kinds of support that can help divorcing couples. An increasing number of states are offering (or mandating) mediation and education courses for divorcing parents, and school-based support programs for children are becoming more common (Amato, 2001) . 

In many communities, professionals have developed programs for families going through divorce. In 1996, it was estimated that approximately 17% of U.S. counties provided parent education programs to divorcing families. Two-thirds of the programs consisted of one session lasting 2 to 4 hours with most (74 per cent) mandated by the courts (Kirby, 1998). One such program "Families First" in Atlanta offers a variety of services for families transitioning through divorce or separation. This program includes a TransParenting course for parents and a Rollercoaster program for children. Courts in Virginia have mandated that both parents attend the TransParenting class to learn about handling post-divorce child issues: attendance is required before a divorce will be granted. This four-hour class addresses areas such as alienation, criticism of ex-spouse in front of the children, using the children as messengers, using child ren as spies, and the effects of such parental behavior on the child. The course also educates parents as to possible effects of divorce on children of different age groups. 

While the program does not reach parents early enough to prevent custody litigation itself, it does seek to control child-related litigation that courts find most pointless, bitter, and frustrating (Crouch, 1993). Through this type of parent mediation program, parents ca n gain insight on how their behavior impacts their child's adjustment to divorce. Role-play and other exercises help parents understand from the child's viewpoint how their negative behavior affects the child. While parent feedback from these types of mediation programs is mostly positive, there is also considerable need to understand the effects of community-based intervention efforts, in order to provide feedback to policy makers and program developers (Kirby, 1998; McKenry, Clark, & Stone, 1998). A lthough some evidence suggests that these interventions are beneficial to adults and children, more evaluation research is needed, especially studies based on randomized trials (Amato, 2001).
