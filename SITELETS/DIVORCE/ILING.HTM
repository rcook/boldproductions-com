Divorcing: The Process of Divorce 

Divorce law has been changed from at-fault divorce to no-fault divorce. In the past, divorce was granted only after finding one party at fault for having done something wrong during the marriage, such as being violent or h aving an affair with others outside marriage. Katz (1994) indicated that most states have simplified the process of ending a marriage by granting no-fault divorces. Two questions arose on simplifying the process of divorce: Have "divorced children" (Kelly, 2000) benefited from no-fault divorce because of decreasing the hostility between the parents? Has the divorce rate increased because of no-fault divorce laws?

Kelly (2000) reviewed studies by numerous researchers (e.g., Buchana, Maccoby, & Dornbusch, 1996; Hetherington, 1999; Maccoby & Mnookin, 1992; Neighbor, Forehand, & McVicar, 1993) and implied that children do benefit from post-divorce parents' cooperative parenting relationships. Cooperation can include showing respect for one another, providing appropriate emotional support, disciplining authoritatively, and maintaining age-appropriate expectations. In fact, by working together to minimize conflicts and keeping the children out of th e middle, divorced parents will diminish the negative influence on children's adjustment and development. These studies indicate that no-fault divorce without hostility does benefit the divorced children.

Katz (1994) indicated that there are needs for critical evaluations on the effect of the no-fault divorce on the divorce rate. This question has not been adequately answered by research.

Children of Divorce

Kelly (2000) reviewed over 70 studies in the past decade on children's adjustment in conflict marriage and divorce and used the term "divorced children" for efficiency. The term refers to parental divorce, not parents who divorce their children. The review indicated that divorced children, compared with children in never-divorced families, have significantly more adjustment and achievement problems. 

Vandewater & Lansford (1998) found that no gender differences could be linked to divorce (as cited in Kelly, 2000). Hetherington (1999) found that adjustment and achievement in boys and girls after divorce varied by age, time since divorce, type of parenting, and type and extent of conflict (as cited in Kelly, 2000).

Kelly (2000) pointed out that resiliency is the long-term outcome of divorce for divorced children. In the short ru n, those children are more reliant on friends and peer groups that use substances, such as alcohol, cigarettes, and drugs. They have fewer coping skills.

McLanahan & Scandefur (1994) indicated that divorce is associated with lowered academic performance and achievement test score. The factors include reduced resources and lowered parental monitoring post-divorce (as cited in Kelly, 2000).

However, an interesting finding (Nord, Brimhall, & West, 1997) addresses less decline in academic performance as fathers' involvement with the child's school and schoolwork after divorce increased (as cited in Kelly, 2000).

Divorced: Post-divorce Interventions

It becomes easy to divorce, since the divorce law has been simplified. There is need for education about divorce. Parents need the interven tion programs regarding the effects of the divorce process on children, divorce itself, and conflicts that typically emerge before, during, and after divorce. 

Divorce education programs, divorce mediation and custody programs are court-connected or community-based avenues for parents to learn about the effects of divorce on children, to reflect parental behaviors, to encourage positive parenting after divorce, to diminish conflicts and increase mut ual respect between divorced parents, and to focus on child's outcomes (Kelly, 2000).

Kelly (2000) indicated that high-conflict parents appear to benefit the most from divorce education programs early in the legal process. Empirical research (Arbuthnot & Gordon, 1996; Geasler & Blaisure, 1998) find that client satisfaction with the programs is high, even among parents who were mandated to attend (as cited in Kelly, 2000).

Kelly (2000) reported that divorcing men and women are significantly more satisfied with mediation processes compared with those using adversarial processes to settle their divorce disputes.
