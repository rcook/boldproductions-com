  Emotional Resiliency following Divorce
    Divorce in the United States is not a rare phenomenon, for it has been prevalent in ending almost half of all recent marriages. Divorced individuals experience higher levels of psychological discomfort in their daily lives, but the extent to which they adjust to it tends to vary to some degree (Wong & Amato, 2000). A question often asked is, "why do some individuals adapt more readily and easily to the effects of divorce than others?" This research brief discusses factors that help aid with diminishing the draining psychological and emotional effects of divorce.

Divorce often triggers an internal explosion of devastating stress, which makes it difficult for some individuals to successfully cope with the life transition and continue trucking down the road of life that may eventually le ad to new happiness. Those who have access to protective and supportive tools can soften the stress-related experiences following divorce. The resources required for bouncing back after divorce may reside within the individual's self-efficacy, coping skills, and social skills; interpersonal relationships that provide social support; and in structural roles and settings, such as employment, community services, supportive government policies, etc. How quickly one forms new social networks, particularly newly formed intimate relationships, plays a key role in being emotionally resilient. One's perception of divorce also serves as a moderating factor for resiliency (Amato, 2000).

According to the family stress and coping theory of McCubbin, Cauble, and Patterson (1982), an individual's adjustment to divorce varies according to the degree of constant stress build-up, coping resources for stress, and the conceptualization of their divo rce. Some examples of personal resources that may aid one with his/her divorce-related stress are education, employment, and income (Wong & Amato, 2000). Education has been referred to as a strength builder for coping, for educated people have been found to possess better problem-solving skills and more sense of control; thus aiding with the divorce transition period (Ross & Wu, 1995).

Employment has played a role in easing some of the psycholo gical stress of divorce by elevating the individual's sense of self-value and independence (Demo & Acock, 1996). Another resource for helping divorced individuals cope is their supportive social networks such as friends, relatives, and a new intimate partner. Being involved in steady dating and cohabiting relationships, or even remarrying has been related to successfully moving on and burying harsh and saddening feelings following a lost marriage. A person's perception of the divorce is also a factor i n how well he/she deals with the post-divorce events. Those who cling to negative attitudes about divorce have been found to be more likely than those who hold positive attitudes to perceive their divorce as a moral failure or catastrophe (Ross, 1995).

Education, employment, and income are positively related to psychological adjustment among individuals who have experienced divorce (Demo & Acock, 1996). For instance, job satisfaction was discovered t o help women rid themselves of negative post-divorce feelings (Wallerstein, 1986). Employment was also found to give women opportunities for broadening their social networks, and psychological stress was higher for single mothers who depended on public assistance than for employed single mothers (Amato & Partridge, 1987). Another significant predictor of adjustment after divorce is social network size (DeGarmo & Forgatch, 1999).

New intimate rela tionships tend to be most beneficial in promoting emotional resiliency following divorce, for psychological well-being has been found to be higher among those who acquire new romantic partners (Garvin, Kalter, & Hansell, 1993). Furthermore, Kitson (1992) concluded that a new relationship destroys the divorced person's preoccupation or obsession with the ex-spouse. Finally, attitudes toward divorce are significant for the adjustment process. According to Booth and Amato (1991), people with conservative attitudes toward divorce while married are more depressed after the dissolution of their marriage than those with more liberal attitudes. Individuals who perceive several problems in their marriage and who are unhappily married seem to adapt more easily and quickly immediately after their divorce (Wong & Amato, 2000). Gray and Silver (1990) found perceptions of control over the divorce process to be a positive effect on emotional resiliency following divorce. In other words, initiators of divorce were m ore likely to be less attached to their ex-spouses and therefore more content with divorce.

For individuals to successfully move on with their lives after a stressful divorce, it is necessary for them to possess internal coping skills as well as support resources and social networks, including new positive relationships. Having a positive perception of one's situation following marital dissolution and divorce is also beneficial for being emotionally resi lient during the post divorce period. Individuals' adjustment to divorce is dependent on how well they manage stress and define their divorce. Forming new significant relationships is the bridge that carries one over the rough waters of losing a spouse, and achieving new happiness and peace of mind.
