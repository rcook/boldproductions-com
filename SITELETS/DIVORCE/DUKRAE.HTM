  The Negative Impact of Divorce on Children's Social Behaviors
    Over the last decades, the most dramatic change in family life was the increase in the rate of divorce. Nearly one-half of current marriages end in divorce and more than one million children have experienced parental divorce before reaching adulthood. The rate of children who live with a single parent is increasing, a nd the children must face stressful life events, such as moving, decreased income, and the absence of one parent. 

A number of studies in the 1990s found that parental divorce had negative impacts on children's academic achievement, psychological adjustment, self-esteem, and social competence (Amato, 2000). Marital disruption can be problematic for children for two reasons. First, it involves a number of stressors including interparental conflict that challenges children's development. Second, marital dissolution can interfere with children's ability to utilize parental resources (Amato, 1993). This article will discuss social behaviors of children who have experienced parental divorce.

The family is a fundamental institution in which children experience and learn about relationships. Thus, the marital relationship is very important for the development of children's interpers onal skills with their expectation and beliefs about relationships with others (Stocker & Youngblade, 1999). Amato (1996) examined various factors that might contribute to the association between parental divorces from one generation to the next. He found that the most significant predictors of the intergenerational transmission of divorce were interpersonal behavior problems. He also suggested that if their parents have poor quality interpersonal styles, children might develop interpersonal problems.&n bsp;

Beatty (1995) examined the differential effect of age on the variety of impact of father absence on children versus adolescents. The randomly selected research sample consisted of forty children between thirteen and fourteen years old. The research results indicated that father-absent boys revealed poorer interpersonal relationships than did father-present boys because they lacked an appropriate male role model during earl y childhood.

Kunz (2001) explored the differences in interpersonal relationships, such as peer relations and relationships with siblings and parents, between children from divorced families and children from intact families. In this study, a meta-analysis of fifty-three studies concluded that divorce had negative consequences for children's relationships with father-child, mother-child, family, peer, and romantic relationships, except for relationships w ith siblings. Kunz explained these findings through parent absence and economic disadvantage perspectives. 

First, parental absence perspective suggested that two parents were better than one for children's socialization and overall development because when parents divorced, children often had less involvement with the noncustodial parent. Consequently, the children from divorced families often experienced a decrease in parental attention, assistan ce, direction, and supervision. Second, economic disadvantage might affect the development of children. Children living in poverty might be negatively labeled by society and their peer groups. 

Stocker and Youngblade (1999) also examined the association between marital conflict and children's relationships with siblings and peers in a sample of children between seven and ten years old. The data was collected from observations of marital and family interaction and family members' report. The result showed that marital conflict was related to problematic relationships with siblings and peers. Children experienced parental divorce, which might extend to the children's own romantic relationships when they become young adults. 

In 1995, Morrison and Cherlin found that there was little effect of parents' marital disruption on girls, but boys in disrupted families had behavior problems during the p arental divorce process. In addition, parental divorce affected the romance relationships when children from divorced families reached adulthood. For instance, parental divorce played a part in shaping the heterosexual relationships in young adults, but the relation was more significant for women than men. Compared with women from intact families, women from divorced families reported less trust, less satisfaction, more conflicts, and more hesitancy about in involvement (Jacquet & Surra, 2001). Amato (1996) studied intergenerational transmission of divorce, and that parental divorce was more likely to be associated with the risk of the children's divorce when both spouses experienced parental divorce.

Cookston (1999) studied single-mother families, single-father familes, and two-parent families to determine the effects of family structures and parent supervision on adolescent alcohol use, illegal behaviors, and delinquency. The research results indicated that supervision was lowest in single-father homes, a little higher in single-mother homes, and highest in two-parent families. Adolescents in single-father homes were more likely to use alcohol and drugs. Delinquency rates were also highest in single-father homes. In addition, the results suggested that the role of parent supervision could have a positive impact on preventing the rates of adolescent problem behaviors.

In summ ary, parental divorce does not affect all children equally, but it has a negative impact on children's socialization if the children from divorced families have less supervision, attention, and assistance than children from two-parent families. Children who experience parental divorce are more likely to have difficulties in relationships with others and behavioral problems, such using drug or alcohol. According to Shifflett and Cummings (1999), children from high-conflict divorce families have a greater ris k for the development of emotional and behavioral problems. As a result, there is clearly a need for prevention and intervention efforts for divorcing families.
