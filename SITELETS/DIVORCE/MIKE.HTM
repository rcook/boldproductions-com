  Use of Statistics in Reporting about Divorce
    Purpose of Review
Prior to the increasing acceptance of qualitative methodology, the professional literature was dominated by quantitative research. Publishers and writers of journals sought scientific legitimacy through statistical analyses. The popular media focus on hot button issues such as the incidence of alcohol-related deaths or the percentage of marriages ending in divorce. Because these subjects seem to sustain the morbid interest of talking heads and supermarket tabloids, we are inundated with reports of the celebrity divorce-du-jour. What gets obscured by the sensationalism, is the fact that quantitative research on divorce has produced findings on so much more than mere statistical incidence. This review will consider how researchers h ave used statistics to inform over a wide range of issues related to divorce. Readers may observe that quantitative research has not only produced statistics on the incidence of divorce itself, but covers the gamut of related issues. Due to the limitation of space, this review will provide only a sample of what was covered in the last few decades of research on divorce and statistics.

Attitudes and Risk Factors
An interest ing focus that has been subjected to statistical analysis is the risk of divorce (South, Trent & Yang, 2001). Macrostructural-opportunity theory posited that spouses' opportunities to form alternative opposite-sex relationships are significant variables in the explanation of why some couples divorce. Researchers continue to seek understanding through the measurement of attitudes and environmental factors that impact marriage and divorce patterns. Besharov (1999) reported on the National Marriage Project at Rutgers University regarding marriage rates since 1960 and postulated reasons for the growth of divorce rates. Organized religion appears to have a vested interest in the preservation of marriage. Researchers consider ways that might reverse divorce trends (Weishaus, Marston & Shieh, 1994). But they have not looked only at factors leading to divorce. Burr, McCall & Powell (1994) examined the effects of divorce on suicide rates. Some of the studies reviewed used very large samples to achieve stat istical significance. Burr, McCall & Powell used 1980 National Center for Health Statistics data for 294 standard metropolitan areas.

Consequences of Divorce
The consequences of divorce have long been the focus of research studies. Weng (1988) looked at the impact of divorce upon children in terms of academic potential and drug use in a study comparing three methods of statistical testing, measurement, and evaluation. Lach (1999) offered a demographic picture of the children of divorced parents in the 1990s along with the conclusion that they were less likely to end their own marriages than their predecessors in the early 1970s. She and others (Wolfinger, 1999 & 2000) have based similar conclusions on demographic data culled by the National Opinion Research Center at the University of Chicago since 1970, explaining that negative effects of divorce on children have been weakening. An inte resting consequence of divorce was observed from the perspective of labor force participation. Grossman (1978) reported (albeit in a dated study) that divorced women were more apt to be employed in full-time, better paying occupations than women of any other marital status. Evarts (1988) studied the relative merits of divorce litigation and concluded that the costs of litigation exceed the benefits. He further stated that mediation would reduce these costs, and ameliorate the negative consequences.

Cross-Cultural Studies
The risk factors for divorce have also come under scrutiny in cross-societal contexts as exemplified by Trent and South (1989). They analyzed data from a sample of 66 countries, focusing on four societal-level correlates of divorce rates: socioeconomic development, female labor participation, sex ratio, and dominant religion. Marital satisfaction and instability from a cross-cul tural perspective has been studied by researchers for decades (Goode, 1962; Larson & Klebe, 1966; Lloyd, 1968) and will continue to be a focus of current research (Mbosowo, 1994).

Alcoholism and Divorce Statistics
The connection between psychological/behavioral disorders and divorce has also been a popular focus of statistical studies. So much has been done in this area that researchers ha ve performed meta-studies of relevancy of previous research. Before and since Paolino, McCrady, & Diamond (1979), researchers have written overviews of the statistics on alcoholism and divorce.

Multiple Divorce and Remarriage
Redivorce and remarriage has not been as widely covered statistically as first time divorces. Day & Mackey (1981) stated that 28 then current family texts were sa mpled for their treatment of remarriage, and found that most do not even address the topic. They asserted that remarriages were less prone to divorce than primary marriages. The Day and Mackey study was followed by Counts' (1991) assertion that there is a paucity of literature on individuals who have been divorced more than once. Counts' conclusion conflicted with the previous study by Day and Mackey (1981), because in 1991, Counts asserted that as of 1986, there had been an increasing flood of individuals going through second and third divorces. 

Increasing Circles of Interest
It may be discerned from the cursory list of references that the entire social-cultural milieu is interested in the statistical analysis of divorce. Educators, mediators, caregivers, anthropologists, economists, and religious organizations seek insight through an understanding of divorce and statistics. The interest cuts across ethnic and international borders. This should alert producers and consumers of research to be careful not to play fast and loose with statistical figures for the purpose of furthering personal or political agendas.
