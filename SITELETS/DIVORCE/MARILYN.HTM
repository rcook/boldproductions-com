  The Effects of Parental Divorce on Family Relations in Later Life
    The divorce rate among middle adulthood and older adults is still lower than that of young adults; however, the rate seems to increase as the overall divorce rate increases. By middle and late adulthood, many couples who will divorce have already done so. This may be why parent al divorce has such a profound impact on adult children. For the most part, parents and adult children maintain close and supportive relationships throughout life. Adult children expect to see their parents' marriage disrupted by death and may have prepared themselves unconsciously for such an event. However, parental divorce often brings about strained relations between adult children and older parents. 

Remarriage provides an even greater impact for t here is a replacement relationship with which to deal. Many times with a parental remarriage, both generations face unexpected strain from hostility and upheaval. The adverse effects of parental divorce are especially strong for the relationships between adult children and their fathers especially if the father marries a woman with children. This is consistent with several previous studies by Aquilino and Cooney as reported by Kaufman and Uhlenberg (1998). Should we be surprised if older adults decide to di vorce and remarry? After all, the average life expectancy for men is about 72 years whereas the life expectancy for women is about 79 years of age. Better health care has allowed adults to maintain active, healthy, and self-sufficient lifestyles well into older adulthood. 

Economic Exchange
The general consensus from research has been that divorce decreases the amount of economic exchange between generations (Pezz in & Schone,1999). In the past the need for public financial assistance has been decreased by intergenerational support through shared homes, adult children's provision of informal care of parents, grandparent care of grandchildren for no or reduced pay, and intergenerational exchange of finances. Young adult children receive economic assistance from parents to help defray borrowing constraints (Pezzin & Schone). As the older generation ages, caregiving and financial assistance is provided, in turn , by adult children. If divorce and/or remarriage results in decreases of intergenerational economic exchange, greater demands may be placed on public assistance such as Medicare home health. As a result, this erosion of family support has become a concern of policy makers (Pezzin & Schone). Not only has this erosion of family support affected the United States, the concern is worldwide as evidenced in research findings of Christine Millward (1997) conducted in her homeland of Australia. 
In her article "Divorce and Family Relations in Later Life," Millward notes three main demographic changes to be taken into consideration. First, the population itself is aging, which will result in increased need of services by the older adult segment and for an increased number of years if the financial needs are not met by the next generation of children. Second, Millward points out that the decreasing fertility rate means a smaller number of future taxpayers to support government services to the growing population of the aged. Quoting research by de Vaus (1997), Millward states that the pool of people with divorced parents is increasing. Added together, these factors mean fewer resources for an increasing number of older adults in coming generations. She found that older men were particularly impacted, especially if they had remarried women with children of their own. Studies by Pezzin & Schone support these findings that later-li fe divorce and/or remarriage are changing the traditional role of the family support network and economic exchange. These findings also support implications for increased demands on public programs that will be financed by a smaller segment of population. 

Gender 
Both men and women whose parents' marriages ended are more likely to experience a deterioration of their relationships with their fathers than adu lt children whose parents remained married to each other. This substantiates earlier findings by Furstenberg and reported by Kaufman and Uhlenberg (1998). It seems that fathers are more likely than mothers to be blamed for the divorce. Second, the gender of the child may be a determining factor in the intergenerational relationship, as well. The relationships between adult children and divorced parents may also be gender specific. Kaufman reports that studies by both Aquilino and Cooney found that, after t he initial adjustment period, negative effects are stronger for daughter-father relationships than for son-father relationships. Her study also found that divorce reduces the closeness for sons to mothers, but not sons to fathers. This is in contrast to findings of Aquilino and Cooney who found that sons may feel additional stress if they are expected to take up some of their father's roles in the family. A study by Linda Nielsen(1999) found that college-aged adults' management of parental divorce hinges o n several factors: their relationship with their fathers, their beliefs about why their parents divorced, finances, feelings of self-reliance, and their memories about their past. However, as with older adults, daughters usually fare better than sons. 

Legacy
Amato and Booth (2001) provide evidence that marital quality is transmitted from one generation to the next; therefore, marital discord may be self-perpetuat ing. This study found that adult children bring to their own marriage any "negative behavioral predispositions" learned in the family of origin. The hypothesis is made, therefore, that marital quality is transmitted across generations. With each younger generation, the acceptance of divorce is more common. Sixty per cent of college students have divorced parents. Most do not suffer long-term affects on their academics, careers, social, and mental health. (Nielsen,1999).

In closing, most of the research on the effects of parental divorce has focused on young children. However, there does not seem to be much research on adult children whose parents divorced when offspring were 18 or older. Considering the economic impact this may have on our society as a whole, further research should be considered.
