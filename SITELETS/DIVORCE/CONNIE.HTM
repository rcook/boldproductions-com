  Choosing to Leave: The Initiator's Journey
    Divorce is a complex, multifaceted, and painful experience. Each member in the family unit facing divorce has their own emotional journey and timeline through the divorce processes. The timeline and intensity of the grief process varies with each family member.

< font size="2">Kubler-Ross's model of stages of the grief process applies to a physical death, but can also be applied to any loss process in life, including the loss of a marriage. When something is lost in life, it is normal and natural to go through a grief process as a result of the loss. The Kubler-Ross model is a valuable tool to help family members externalize their emotions, providing a label for each emotion, and assisting family members in understanding what is happening to them. The ultimate goal it to aid individual participants in the process to realize that the emotions they are experiencing are normal, indeed natural.

The Kubler-Ross model is often presented as clear cut stages or steps, culminating with eventual acceptance of the situation. As I have observed and experienced the stages of grief, I find the stages to be not so clear-cut. The grief process actually vacillates back and forth between stages and eventually moves into some level o f acceptance. The specific grief process is not easily forgotten, often remaining for a lifetime. The pain will diminish with time, but the feelings have the potential to resurface years later as a result of a reminder of a similar situation. Therefore, instead of clear cut steps or stages of grief, the process is more like an ocean wave that rises and peaks only to be hit with some reminder that will jag the grief process back to an earlier stage of the emotional process, only to go forth again moving clo ser to accepting the end result.

All family members are on their own timeline in dealing with their grief journey through their divorce process. The complexity and multifaceted nature of the process of the family in totality is too large of a scope to address in this article. Therefore, this work will focus specifically on the initiator of the divorce from an abusive relationship and the journey through the initiator's grief process.

In observing the initiator, one may mistakenly perceive that the initiator has no pain since it was the initiator's choice to leave the marriage in the first place. This is not true. The pain for the initiator is commonly earlier in the divorce process as the initiator works through the decision to leave the marriage, even though it may not be what the initiator really wants to happen. The initiator experiences a high level of distress and grief while the marriage is still intact, indeed, experiencing years of mourning a marriage that never was. By the time the separation occurs, the initiator has already finished mourning the end of the marriage and appears to be better adjusted than other family members (Wang & Amato, 2000). 

The initiator's painful grief process can be compared to a wood sliver under the skin. At first, it is easy to deny that the sticker is there at all. With t ime, the pressure and pain mounts as the infection builds. The initiator could attempt to bargain the pain away…It's only a matter of time and the pain will go away. Anger enters the picture, blaming someone for not taking care of the board with the wood sliver sticking out…The initiator might even feel anger at themselves for having not been more careful. Eventually, the sore spot must be dealt with. This reality is a depressing thought. The initiator must decide how to deal with the removal of the sticker and infection. Accepting and dealing with the sore will then allow the skin to grow healthy again. A more detailed look at the initiator's grief stages (based on the Kubler-Ross model) and some of the emotions the initiator experiences are detailed below. 

Denial
Initiators can spend years in denial, thinking that their marriage will get better. Abuse, physical or psychological, may be discounted by the initiator in an attempt to self-convince that the abuse happened only once or twice. The initiator may have distorted thinking: acknowledging that their partner was physically or psychologically abusive, but accepting the partner's apology and promise that the abuse wouldn't happen again. Sometimes, the initiator is not even aware that they have a choice of staying or leaving.

Bargaining
Initiators in this stage want to believe that the relationship or marriage will improve with time. They spend time and energy thinking such thoughts as, "If only the marriage or relationship would be OK." "If only I made more money." "If only I was a better parent." " If only I were a better spouse." "If only I was better looking or would lose weight." Some women believe that if they improve their behavior, their partners will ultimately stop abusing them (Turner & Shapiro, 1986).

Anger
Anger in this stage is directed outward: 1) anger at the spouse for not working with the initiator, 2) anger at God for not intervening and making the relationship better, 3) anger at friends and family for not being more supportive financially or emotionally, 4) anger at the children for complicating the marriage process by misbehaving.& nbsp;

Depression
Depression is commonly anger turned inward, and in this instance it may produce a trapped feeling: "I wish it weren't true (my marriage is breaking up), but it is true, so now I'm forced to make a decision that I don't want to make…to remain in the abusive marriage or leave." Regret and fear are overriding emotions in this thought process.

Accep tance
Acceptance is characterized by a firm feeling that the initiator has made the right decision and is able to discuss his or her life by looking beyond the abusive relationship (Turner & Sharpiro, l986). It is a resolve that the decision has to be made and the initiator is willing to move ahead with the process. This may not be the decision hoped for, but based on the circumstances, this is the best decision for the initiator at this time. Once at this stage, the ini tiator can begin the physical move out of the relationship.

Conclusion
Divorce is an extremely painful, complex process for all family members. All family members will be on their own timeline in dealing with their journey through their divorce and grief processes. The Kubler-Ross stages of grief model is a tool to externalize the emotions involved and make some sense of how the emotions all fit together. For each fam ily member, part of the ease of dealing with the divorce is to make some kind of sense out of what is happening. The Kubler-Ross model helps all members, and specifically the initiator to accomplish this difficult life task with an understanding that is helpful as the process moves forward.
