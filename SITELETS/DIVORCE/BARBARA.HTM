  Adjusting to Divorce: Crisis or Chronic Strain?
    Adversity can strike families in many forms and fashions. Poverty, physical illness and disabilities, and violence are adversities that threaten the fulfillment of basic human needs and can interfere with the achievement of developmental tasks (Sandler, 2001). Divorce is also one of those adverse life events that can have a profound impact on family functioning. Research has shown that parental divorce is associated with problem outcomes in children, such as academic problems, substance abuse, mental health problems, and teenage pregnancy (Amato, 1993; Zill, Morrison, & Coiro, 1993). Divorce adjustment depends largely on the perspective of the family members and how they cope with the stress produced by the divorce process.

Divorce adjustment c an be viewed through the lens of stress and coping theory. Lazarus (1984) examined stress through a cognitive appraisal process, in which a person evaluates whether an event is important or threatening to his or her well-being. The individual conducts a primary appraisal in which he determines the degree of the threat, and then takes a broader perspective, evaluating the coping options available and the likelihood that they will be effective. Lazarus posited that it is not the nature of the stressor, but the person's cognitive appraisal of the stressor that determines how he will respond and adjust.

In relating this stress and coping model to divorce adjustment, Wang and Amato (2000) reported that these factors were important: the perception of the stressor, the resources available for dealing with the stressors, and the build-up of stressors. Amato (2001) discussed the process of divorce adjustment from a divorce-stress-adjustment perspective, in whi ch divorce is viewed as a process beginning before the actual separation of the marriage partners and ends long after the divorce papers are signed. This process includes numerous events that can be perceived as taxing to the family members and can result in both positive and negative outcomes.

The divorce-stress-adjustment perspective proposes two models: the crisis model and the chronic strain model (Amato, 2001). The crisis model represents a view that divorce is a situational crisis that results in a state of disequalibrium. The "crisis" means that previous coping mechanisms are no longer effective. How quickly the crisis resolves depends upon the person's perceptions of the event, the support systems available, and the ability to find effective coping skills (Aguilera, 1994). The crisis model assumes that with enough time, most people return to the same level of functioning as they had prior to the crisis. Indeed, some studies have shown that pr oblems experienced earlier in the divorce process, such as unhappiness, depression, and health problems, were no longer present two or three years after the separation (Booth & Amato, 1991; Lorenz et al., 1997).

The second model within the divorce-stress-adjustment perspective is a chronic strain model (Amato, 2001). This model assumes that divorce involves ongoing and persistent strains upon the family, and that there may never be a return to a sta te of well-being or pre-divorce functioning. Chronic strain may occur as a result of ongoing financial hardships, changes in roles within the family and social networks, and other stressors that continually pull at the family. These stressors may not ease with the passage of time, and for some people, there is no improvement in functioning after the divorce, unless there is a remarriage (Amato, 2001; DeGarmo & Kitson, 1996; Wang & Amato, 2000).

It is likely that both of the models are appropriate in different families. Amato (2001) states that much depends upon the types of stressors involved, such as continuing conflict with ex-spouse, economic concerns for the parent, loss of contact with a parent, and children's observation of continuing parental conflict. Additionally, protective factors, such as perceptions of the meaning of the divorce to the family members as well as personal and social resources, play a role in divorce adjustment (Wang & Amato, 2000). Future research efforts should continue to focus on factors relating to divorce adjustment and the long-term consequences for families.
