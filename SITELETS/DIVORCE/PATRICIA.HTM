  Advantages of Shared Parenting
    As a counselor working with children and their families who are dealing with divorce trauma, I have concluded that while the parents may choose to be married no more, their family, supported by a shared parenting arrangement, may survive. Recent research supports this thesis. The purpose of this review is not to argue for proposed shared parenting legislation. Nor is to review the ways in which shared parenting arrangements may be planned and executed. This paper concentrates on some of the research that examines why shared parenting is gaining as the custody arrangement of choice.

In shared parenting or joint physical custody arrangements the child actually lives, not just visits, with each parent from 30% to 50% of the time. Donnelly an d Finkelhor (1993) find that 12% of divorced families have joint physical custody arrangements. But Clarke (1995) reports that percentage has risen to 15.7% of custody cases, in which physical custody time has been court ordered to be at least 30% of the time. And in 1997, according to the U.S. Census Current Population Survey, National Center for Health Statistics, divorced children now live in joint physical custody arrangements in about 20% of the cases.

Glover & Steele (1989) conducted research on post-divorce parenting arrangements in terms of how such arrangements affected the children of families of divorce in terms of locus of control, self-concept, and family relationships. Children from shared custody, maternal custody, and intact families were studied. The study concludes that a child fares better under shared parenting than under sole custody. Bender (1994) finds that joint custody should be a rebuttable pr esumption of the court since the cases of joint custody studied show that the children fare better physically, mentally, and economically under that arrangement. Rebuttable presumption entails the view that joint custody is advisable unless domestic violence or other problems presented by a parent might be detrimental to the child.

How a child adjusts after divorce has been found to hinge on several variables, including the am ount and quality of contact with noncustodial parents, custodial parents' psychological adjustment and parenting skills, level of parental conflict in the divorce, degree of economic hardship, and the number of additional stressful life events (Amato, 1994). Arditti (1992) finds that a child's economic needs are as well as or better provided for in joint custody as compared with sole custody. A number of research projects have found that children living in joint custody situations fare better in most ways (Buchannan, Maccoby & Dornbush, 1991).

Research findings from studies conducted by Arditti (1992) and Buchannan et al. (1991) support the idea that conflict between parents is reduced or is the same with joint custody of their children as it is in sole custody. This reduction in conflict between parents is not only beneficial for their children but for the parents themselves. Ehrenberg (1996) f inds that divorced couples that cooperate are more satisfied with their lives than conflicted divorced couples. 

Fathers are the big losers in cases where sole custody is awarded. In 1997 it was reported that only 9% of fathers were awarded custody, whereas 69% of mothers were awarded sole custody (NCHS). But the children are also the losers: Amato & Gilbreth (1999) report that feelings of closeness to the father an d authoritative parenting were positively associated with children's academic success. Gardner (1999) finds that in child-custody disputes evaluators who fail to assess the impact of parental bonding in their research and findings, weaken the value of the assessment. Gardner also points out that the evaluators who do take bonding to parent into consideration do not cite objective criteria. And in many of these cases, the child is separated from the father.

For those who would like a more in-depth and comprehensive report on the shared parenting advantages to children of divorce, I direct the readers to the following book-length resources:

(1)Adolescents After Divorce (Buchanan, Maccoby, & Dornbusch, 1996). This book is the report of an empirical longitudinal study of social and emotional adjustments of 5 22 adolescents in 365 divorced families.

(2) Joint custody and Shared Parenting (Folberg, J., Ed., 1991), a series of research articles and findings on the subject.

As research continues on shared parenting and its effects, no doubt more now at-risk children of divorce will exper ience happier aftermaths of their parents' separations. As a counselor of these children and their families, I look forward to this eventuality.
