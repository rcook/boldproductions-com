  Maintaining Family Rituals During Divorce
    Family routines, rituals, and traditions are fundamental factors in the development of healthy, resilient families (Curran, 1983). Research shows us that these times, events, and activities bind a family as a cohesive, meaningful group; create invaluable memories; insure future togetherness; provide stability and continuity; create and transmit families values (Bossard & Boll, 1950); and create a sense of true belonging (Bennett, Wolin, & McAvity, 1988). "Traditions are treasured rituals we carry from childhood into adulthood" (Galinksy, 1999). Bossard and Boll (1950) postulated that family rituals exemplify the very core of the family. So, what happens when the marital relationship dissolves? If family routinized activities defin ed the family and their values, should they be dissolved with the marriage or maintained as a way to communicate that family values are unchanged?

Family routinization has been positively related to healthy resolutions in divorcing families. Divorcing families tend to be more resilient when rituals remain unscathed. "That is, when a family is experiencing stress, either normative or non-normative, family rituals may serve to protect family members f rom the negative effects associated with the stressor" (Fiese, 1995, p. 276). When a marriage dissolves, the family is often uprooted, disrupted, and life can seem uncertain. Maintaining family activities may help to create a sense of normality in the face of unfamiliar circumstances. Knowing that rituals, routines and traditions are unchanged may help children to comprehend that life goes on and that certain aspects of life will remain predictable and unaffected. It can also give the custodial parent a template for carrying on after the divorce. While the family structure may have changed, the family methodology can remain the same. Even if the custodial parent finds it difficult to continue activities that used to be shared with a former spouse, discontinuing them may be interpreted by the child as discounting the parent and the activities the family found important.

"Divorce is a disruptive process that begins in the fail ing marriage and continues for many years following the divorce" (Pett, Lang & Gander, 1992). The transition is even more difficult when children are involved. Several researchers have found that preserving family activities is a helpful tactic to ease the tumult of divorce. 

After interviewing 50 parent-grandmother dyads in which the parent was divorced, Johnson (1988) concluded that when family rituals were maintained du ring and after divorce, the transition tended to be easier. Children's positive image of their family was maintained. Rituals created a buffer-zone that controlled interactions and eased stress. Eliminating role and relationship ambiguities and conflicts moderated effects of the divorce process. It seemed that family rituals also affirmed family relationships after the family experienced divorce.

Pett, Lang, and Gander (1992 ) interviewed 111 older divorced parents and 115 of their adult children to determine the impact of divorce on family rituals. The study found that one of the effects of divorce was loss of family identity and traditions. The biggest difference reported was the way the families celebrated Thanksgiving and Christmas after divorce. The children considered this a negative factor of the divorce. 

In her 25-year longitudinal study of the effects of divorce, Judith Wallerstein (2000) found that in some families routines were neglected after divorce. After the change in family composition, which may lead to changing work status and financial stability, families may be struggling to simply survive. Rituals and routines may be neglected for lack of time, energy, and financial resources. One child in Wallerstein's study reported that after her parents divorced she virtually forgot that her mother used to rejoice and be involved i n all the aspects in her life. The rituals they had enjoyed while the marriage was intact were replaced by a hectic schedule that lacked the rituals and routines that made life predictable and stable. 

William Doherty (1997) said that it is natural for rituals and routines to weaken in the weeks and months after a divorce, and that the custodial parent may be too overwhelmed to provide more than basic child care. The absent parent may also be struggling to reorganize his/her lifestyle. Without these stabilizing forces the family is "adrift" (p.167). Doherty suggested that recently divorced families need to maintain the rituals that were working well before the divorce, modify the rituals that no longer work, and create new rituals for the newly formed family (pp. 170-171). This may help the family to create the equilibrium needed to raise healthy children.

In the ir book, Raising Resilient Children, Brooks and Goldstein (2001) listed creating traditions and special times as one of six ways to make a child feel loved, special and appreciated. When the family is in upheaval and one parent is becoming less involved, it is important that these activities remain intact. The child may interpret this dedication to tradition as an expression of devotion to the relationship. Covey (1997) clai med that traditions can help family members feel supported and understood, as well as demonstrate loyalty. Continuing family traditions "renews the emotional energy and bonding of the past" (p. 280) which can be even more meaningful in the process of divorce when relationships can seem ambiguous.

By maintaining family rituals, routines, and traditions during divorce, parents can demonstrate that family bonds, while changing, are still important and strong. Judith Wallerstein concluded "Parents need to know that it's extremely important to restore routines as quickly as possible after divorce" (2000, p. 167).
