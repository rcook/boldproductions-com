  Fathers and Shared Custody
    When the term "Fathers' Rights" is mentioned in mixed gender company, it is often met with a curious grimace or a not very well hidden eye roll and often accompanied by a statement basically saying that if fathers want to have "rights" then they should pay child support or should not have left the family in the first place. Still, we read about the ills of a "fatherless" society and how a child with two parents generally does better than a child with an absent parent. 

In fact, Goldberg (1997) states that children without involved fathers have higher rates of criminal activity, substance abuse, suicide and running away during their teen years than do children with involved fathers. Goldberg also speaks to the child support argument by addressing the possibility that fathers who are not emotionally involved with their children due to lack of or extremely limited visitation are less likely to be financially involved. Young (2000) supports this by stating in her article that "...the vast majority of divorced men who are steadily employed pay all or most of the child support they owe" (You ng, 2000 p. 42). One of the primary examples of fathers rights that Young discusses is the relatively recent case of Elian Gonzalez, the young Cuban child who came to Florida by boat. Young maintains, and I agree, that if it had been his mother that was still in Cuba and his father had been the one that died, that the case would never have become the issue that it ultimately produced.  

Young (2000) goes on to attack the other argument mentioned above concerning fathers leaving the family via divorce. She states that two thirds of all divorces involving children are initiated by the mother. These are not cases that are just filed officially by the mother but actually asked for by the mother in the absence of extenuating circumstances such as adultery or abuse. She goes on to state that in surveys, the fathers seem more willing to continue with a questionable marriage for the sake of the children, polling 14% higher than women in this regard. Still, in the United States, only 14 states and the District of Columbia have the presumption of joint custody, assuming both parties agree. It is still more common for courts to adhere to the "tender years doctrine," which assumes that children are better off with their mothers because mothers are able to form a stronger and more lasting bond with the children than those formed by fathers (Goldberg, 1997). 

Even when joint custody is established, up to half of all fathers report that they have their visitation rights interfered with, though this is not explicitly described, and up to 40 percent of mothers admit to denying the fathers visitation rights as a punishment for a real or imagined transgression (Young, 2000). With this short article, I am not advocating that all fathers should have sole custody of the children after a divorce. How ever, I am advocating that fathers be given the consideration of obtaining at least joint custody. 

Goldberg (1997) cites a woman named Joan Entmacher who states that the decision needs to be based on what is in the best interest of the children, but then speaks out against fathers by saying that joint custody is just a trapping of man to keep the woman under his control even after the divorce. She also points to the child support issue and says that, based on those that do not pay their child support, fathers are inconsistent at best about caring for their children (Goldberg, 1997). To me, this sounds as though she has already decided that what is in the best interest of the children is that the custody should go to the mothers by default. I can not support that stance and I feel that it should be looked at more closely. If the current literature continues to state that the role of the father and role of the two parent household is increasingly important to the welfare of the children, then minimally the presumption of joint custody should spread to more states. 

References

Goldberg, S. B. (1997). Make room for daddy. ABA Journal, 83, 48-53. 

Young, C. (2000). The sadness of the American father. American 
Spectator, 33(5), 40-45.
