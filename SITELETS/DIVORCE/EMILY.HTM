  Remarriage
    Introduction

According to Furstenberg (1996), the societal ideal of the nuclear family came to the forefront in the United States in the 1950's, during a period when there was a great debate over the decline of the extended family, which had been the prior ideal and norm for family configuration. The re was a concern at the time that the nuclear family would not be able to withstand the pressures and demands of intimacy and companionship placed upon it, along with the changing opportunities and demands of the workforce, the division of household labor and gender role issues (Furstenberg). Indeed, there has been an increase in the divorce rate in this country since the 1950's (Furstenberg). This paper will explore one of several options available to divorced parents - remarriage- and some of its effects on families. 

Parents and Remarriage

Thomson, Mosely, Hanson, and McLanahan (2001) examined the relationship between mothering behavior and whether the mother was single, in a remarriage or in cohabitation with a partner. The findings of the study suggested that single mothers provide more harsh discipline than do mothers who are in a stable cohabitating relationship or remarried. However, findings also suggested that supervision of children in the home is higher when mothers are single than when they remarry or become involved in a new relationship.

Warshak (2001) discussed Parental Alienation Syndrome (PAS), or children's "estrangement from the parent being criticized…when…divorcing parents…try to undermine their children's affection for the other parent" (p.229). He stated that behaviors of all of the parents (remarried, step, and othe r) as well as the child may encourage the existence of this syndrome. PAS, according to Warshak, arises from:

…jealousy, narcissistic injury, desire for revenge, the wish to erase the exspouse from the child's life in order to 'make room' for the stepparent, competitive feelings between the exspouse and the stepparent, the new couple's attempt to unite around a common enemy and avoid recognition of conflicts in the marriage, the child's attempt to resolve inner conflict, and parent-child boundary violations (p.229).

Warshak (2001) recommended that early intervention occur with newly-formed stepfamilies to address these issues right away before unhealthy patterns of relating become enmeshed within the family structure.

Children and Remarriage

Axinn and Thornton (1996) conducted a study examining the impact of family dissolution on attitudes held by children regarding the formation of marriage. Findings of the study suggested that children are highly influenced by their mother's attitudes towards marriage, cohabitation, divorce and remarriage. Children whose mothers have remarried tend to demonstrate "approval of premarital sex, and cohabitation and …tolerance of divorce" (p.71).

Lye, Klepinger, and Hyle (1995) investigated how a child's living arrangements post divorce affect his/her relationship as an adult with his/her parents. Findings indicated that the bond between the non-custodial parent and the children living with a remarried custodial parent are likely to be severely weakened. However, findings also indicated that the remarriage of the custodial parent seems to ameliorate the relationship between the custodial parent and the child.

Adolescents and Remarriage

McCurdy and Scherman (1996), in a study about how family structure affects the individuation-separation process of young people ages 18-24 attending college, found that those subjects whose parents were remarried reported less attachment to their fathers than did those subjects whose parents had intact marriages. The findings of the study suggested that "adolescents who have experienced pa rental divorce and/or remarriage may be at risk for conflictual dependencies on their noncustodial fathers" (p.310).

Sprujit and Goede (1997) examined the effect of changes in the structure of families on the well-being of adolescents. Their study investigated adolescents from intact families, intact families with conflict, single-headed families after divorce, and stepfamilies after the remarriage of the custodial parent. The study's findings suggested that adolescents who are in the remarriage category fare almost as well as those in the intact families in regard to their well-being. Adolescents from intact families with conflict or in single-headed families after divorce seem to experience lower levels of well-being when compared to those from intact families or remarried families.

Conclusion

Taken together, these findings suggest that bein g part of an intact family is the most beneficial for adolescents and children. However, perhaps due to the pressures for intimacy and companionship placed on the nuclear family- and evolving work and household labor and gender role issues- that ideal is not possible for most people to obtain and maintain. The resulting divorce provides parents with several options, one of which is remarriage. Children are learning that divorce and remarriage are viable alternatives to marriage in this present-day society.

While remarriage is a transition for families, it seems that it may be a valuable lifestyle choice versus living in a conflicted intact or single-headed family. Perhaps remarriage and the formation of stepfamilies is a new way for families to recreate the ideal image of the extended family of the olden days and increase the social and economic resources available to them. The choices made by parents after divorce and during remarriage do appear to impac t their relationships with children. This seems to be especially true for non-custodial parents, who are typically the fathers of the children. Given the trend toward divorce and remarriage, it may be helpful for family scientists to assist newly remarried families with parenting, co-parenting and communication skills, and for policy makers to write supportive policy to make the transition a positive one for all concerned.
