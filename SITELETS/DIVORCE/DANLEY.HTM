  Dissolution of Long-term Marriages
   

"Divorce, whether it occurs early or late in the life cycle, is a disruptive process that begins in the failing marriage and continues for many years following the divorce." (Pett, Lang, & Gander, 1992, p. 526)

Trends & Causes

Prior to the change in attitude of American society in the mid 1960's about marriage and divorce, when marriage was still considered a moral and social obligation, spouses who were not personally satisfied with their marriage, but who experienced limited open conflict, tended to stay together. But in 1969, California instituted the no-fault divorce.

Forty-six other states enacted such provisions by 1974, and the remainder of the states followed suit by 1985. The immediate result of this new, easy exit from the bonds of matrimony was an astronomical increase in the divorce rate in this country. The effect was especially remarkable for older couples who were ending long-term marriages (Galston, 1996). No-fault divorce faci litated the phenomenon. It probably was not the reason for it. Lanza (1996) offered several reasons for the increase in the proportion of divorces among older couples, such as the stress of debilitating illnesses and more second or subsequent marriages.

Hiedemann (1998) found disruption in long-term marriages to be dependent on both the duration of the marriage and on various earlier family transactions, such as age at marriage , age at the onset of childbearing, and the timing both of children's entering adulthood and their leaving home. Hammond & Miller (1992) recalled the additional suggestions of other researchers of a greater acceptance of divorce among cohorts: increased economic independence of women and an increase in the numbers of mid-life and pre-retirement divorces.

Effects

The effects of divorce on older individuals appear to be numerous and varied. These effects are physical, behavioral, psychological, financial, emotional/social, and familial. They are not necessarily negative. Certain of the effects appear to be associated with gender and/or age, while others are similar for most ex-spouses.

The likelihood of a divorced man dying of heart disease, stroke, hypertension, a nd cancer is twice that of a married man. Too, various forms of cancer are two to three times as likely to be fatal in divorced women. (Galston, 1996) In a study of stress on mature individuals, Perreira and Sloan (2001) found that for problem drinkers of both genders, divorce was associated with a reduction in their drinking.

An older woman who divorces faces both the normal aging experience and the major life transitions requi red by the divorce at the same time. She may have difficulty with deciding who she is and what she can do (Lanza, 1996). Compared to younger divorcees, older women reportedly find themselves with more leisure time, but with more limited career opportunities (Hammond et al., 1992). Older divorcees may find themselves worse off after a divorce, especially if they have spent most of their married lives as homemakers, rather than in the outside work force. This is due to the fact that divorce settlements today are likely to divide financial resources equally between the divorcee and the ex-husband, who often has the greater earning capacity, rather than awarding the bulk of the couple's property to the ex-wife, as was typical in settlements in earlier decades (Galston, 1996).

Hammond et al. (1992) did find, however, that older women reported better housing situations than did older men; a better life in the categories of social life, being a parent, and overall happiness; and (in contrast to other studies) no significant differences in the finance category.

"The loss of a partner is either accompanied by feelings of failure—in the case of divorce—or by feelings of grief about the loss of a unique relationship" (Peters & Liefbroer, 1997, p. 696). Possibly the most salient effect of the loss of a spouse is loneliness, whether the loss was due to divorce or to death. Indeed, Peters et al. (1997) found no difference in the levels of loneliness for divorced and widowed respondents. Loneliness did decrease with time passed since the last dissolution for both men and women, but the tendency was not found to be linear.

Older adults who had lost at least one partner were still more lonely than those who had never had a partner. Further, older men and women who had experienced multiple dissolutions were found to be lonelier than those who had experienced only one such loss. The oldest old are less lonely than the young-old, perhaps because they have lower expectations of companionship (Peters et al., 1997).

Older women are more likely than older men to have friends. And older women tend to turn to others for emotional help more often than do older men. Specifically, older women are more likely to turn to family members, friends, and religious leaders ( Hammond et al., 1992). This may be one reason why men who have no partner, but who have had one in the past, are more lonely than their female counterparts (Peters et al., 1997).

Finally, older divorced persons tend to experience changes in their relationships with their extended families. Divorce often results in family conflict, tension in family relations, and divided loyalties among adult children, with family members sometimes feeling less of a sens e of obligation toward a divorced woman than they would toward a widow (Lanza, 1996). There is often a loss of contact time between divorced older persons and their grandchildren, which generally causes disappointment in the adult children. Also, the keeping of family traditions can prove extremely difficult, if not impossible, especially immediately following parental divorce, due to feelings of sadness, hurt, anger, and loss among the family members. A positive side to the disruption of established tradit ions may be that some family members enjoy a new sense of freedom to try and build new family traditions (Pett et al., 1992).

Choices

Many older divorced individuals choose not to remain without a partner after the dissolution of their marriage. Many remarry, while a growing percentage simply cohabit. In a study of the question of who remarries among individuals who divorce at mid-life or later, Sweeney (1997) found that for both men and women, a younger age of first marriage and a younger age at separation were positively associated with the likelihood of remarriage. This study found that the single predictor of older women remarrying was their having a good socioeconomic prospect, that is a good occupational status, perhaps because of an increased ability to attract a mate. The study found socioeconomic status not to be a predictor of remarriage for men.

Although the reasons for cohabitation among the elderly have not been clearly defined, Chevan (1996) addresses certain tendencies of this growing practice. He states that elderly people who have been divorced or separated are probably more likely to cohabit than the widowed. He also found that men who were never married are significantly less likely to cohabit than males of any other marital status, while this is not true of women who have never-married. Too, po or health is a deterrent to cohabitation for older men, but not for women.
