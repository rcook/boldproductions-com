  Predictors of Divorce
    Current Marital Trends

Contemporary research reports that American marriages are in a state of change. Since the 1940s, the rate of first marriages has steadily declined while the rate of divorce has steadily risen (Teachman, Tedrow, & Crowder, 2000). It wasn' t until the 1990s that divorce rates in America began declining. According to Bradbury, Fincham, and Beach (2000), this decline is due to a large amount of people waiting until they are older to get married. Regardless, it is reported that "until death do us part" pertains to only about 50% of couples taking the vows (Knoester & Booth, 2000). Knoester and Booth (2000) claim that between 1960 to 1980, "the annual divorce rate nearly tripled, and it has remained at a relatively high level ever since" (p. 78).

Ethnic differences can be found in the current marital trends. For example, according to Teachman, et al., there was a decline in marriage for White and African American women, ages 20 to 24, between the 1970s and 1980s. This decline was more drastic for African Americans, however, because their "prevalence of marriage … was already substantially lower" than Whites. Conversely, Hispanic women's tendencies to marry ha d changed only moderately and they were "more likely than either Whites or African Americans" to marry (p. 455).

Modern couples have different expectations of marriage than compared to their predecessors. Unlike the past, divorce has become a viable option for many couples in times of marital distress. In addition, the propensity for White women and African American women to remarry has decreased through the years while Hispanic women's tendency rose (Teachman, et al., 2000).When couples remarry, they enter the relationship intending to "do it right" this time. However, according to research, remarriages are just as likely to end in divorce as the first time around (Ceglian & Gardner, 1999). Why are marriages so unstable? Below are some theories attempting to account for this trend.

Relationship Theories

Up to the 1980s, marital quality research focused on cognition and on "microanalytic studies of sequential patterns in behavior." In the 1990s, research left this trend and evolved toward studying higher levels of interaction (Bradbury, et al., 2000, p. 966). Until now, marital satisfaction was believed to follow a "U-shaped function," with high levels of happiness in the early stage of marriage, declining during child rearing years, and rising again in later life (Bradbury, et al., 2000, p. 964). However, Bradbury, et al., contradict this theory reporting that marital satisfaction instead follows a constant downward path through the years. There are many theories in the current literature attempting to explain such reported trends in marital satisfaction and subsequent divorce. 

One such theory is the demand/withdrawal model. In this model, one spouse, typically the wife, "criticize s and nags" the other spouse, who, typically the husband, "avoids the discussion and disengages." As this pattern repeats, marital satisfaction decreases and divorce is likely (Bradbury, et al., 2000, p. 966). While some research has supported such gendered associations with marital unhappiness, this model has often been criticized for gender stereotyping (Gottman & Levenson, 1999; Roberts, 2000). For example, studies have found such communication patterns not to be gender related but rather related to the "nature of the discussion" (Roberts, 2000, p. 702). 

Another theory is the skills-deficit model. According to Burleson and Denton (1997), this theory proposes that marital unhappiness results from a lack of communication skills or constructive conflict management by the spouses. Negative interactions are exacerbated, which hinder marital satisfaction. Developing communication skills is thought to improve conflict manage ment and therefore the relationship. However, research has stated that improving communication skills has "proven to be less than effective and lasting methods of enhancing marital satisfaction" and has been "assumed much more frequently than it has been shown" (Burleson & Denton, 1997, p. 885-886).

A third model is the social exchange theory. In this theory, people aim at maximizing rewards and minimizing costs in their rel ationships. The partner "over-benefiting" from the exchange will view the relationship positively whereas the partner "under-benefiting" will perceive it negatively (Wright & Aquilino, 1998, p. 195). Thus, according to Knoester and Booth (2000), the "commitment of the relationship depends on" its attractiveness, which depends on the perceived rewards and costs (p. 79). The model also proposes that the "partner with greater personal resources . . . should have greater power" in the relationship, which ha s been empirically supported (Patterson, 2000, p. 272). However, past research on the theory's factors has been shown them to be obscured when not accounting for "the presence of alternatives and barriers to divorce" (Knoester & Booth, 2000, pp. 79-80). The above mentioned theories are not exhaustive yet they offer a lens through which to view many contributing factors leading to divorce.

Factors of Divorce

Founded in the rich empirical and theoretical foundations of the 1980s, the 1990s expanded research on factors of marital dissatisfaction and divorce. One often cited factor is the presence of children in the marriage. According to Gottman and Notarius (2000), once children are present marital quality declines, conflict increases, sexual activity decreases, and the spouses return to stereotypical gender roles. 

Economic factors are also a consideration in marital dissatisfaction. Whereas women merely entering the workforce do not affect marital stability, their "level of employment and earnings" do when greater than that of their spouses' (Heckert, Nowak, & Snyder, 1998, p. 701). Heckert, Nowak, and Snyder (1998) also report that "income dependency is an important determinant of marital disruption" (p. 701). In addition, worrying about money decreases m arital satisfaction (White & Rogers, 2000, p. 262-263). 

Poor conflict resolution and communication skills are often cited as major reasons for marital dissatisfaction (Greeff & De Bruyne, 2000; Kurdek, 1995; Marchand, 2000; Ono, 1998). However, according to Gottman, Mordechai, and Levenson (2000), the reason for divorce is not negativity and arguing but couples simply growing apart and not feeling close, loved, or appreciated by their partners. For example, Scott and Sprecher (2000) report that sexual satisfaction is an important predictor of marital dissatisfaction. However, other types of intimacy, such as expressing affection, are better predictors of relationship quality than sexual satisfaction (Scott & Sprecher, 2000).

Currently, younger, White couples are vastly over represented in empirical study. Olde r couples and cultural differences are poorly represented. According to Gottman and Notarius (2000), older couples experience more affection and less negative emotions when resolving conflict than do younger couples. However, elderly couples also experience greater stressors, such as ailing health, financial strains, and caretaking stressors, which negatively impact their relationships (Wright & Aquilino, 1998). In addition, McLoyd, Cauce, Takeuchi, and Wilson (2000) report that "there is little evidenc e to suggest that major predictors of marital quality and marital conflict differ across racial and ethnic groups" (p. 297).
