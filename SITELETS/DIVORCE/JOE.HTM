  Marital Dissolution and Child Adjustment
    Current research in Family Studies has a lot to say about divorce. Much of the research focuses on children and the effects of divorce on children. Michael Stevenson and Kathryn Black published a research review of this literature in 1996. Stevenson and Black cite three reasons to account for differences between c hildren of divorced and never divorced families, calling the reasons interrelated and difficult to distinguish empirically. 

First, differences are accounted for traditionally by the absence of the father. This idea is supported by the research of Amato and Keith (1991). Second, differences are attributed to various forms of family dysfunction, which may include parental conflict and difficulty transitioning from living with two parents to livi ng with one parent. McLanahan and Booth (1989) emphasize parental values, childbearing practices, lower parental attachment, less supervision, more favorable attitudes toward divorce, greater stress, and problematic child-parent relationships among some of the family dysfunctions. The third reason is economic difficulty. This difficulty is associated with working more hours, parents spending less time with children, less parental supervision, lower parenting skills, and increased likelihood of children dr opping out of school (McLanahan and Booth, 1989).

Stevenson and Black (1996) indicate that there may be other factors to account for the differences; however, these are the main ideas currently researched. They conclude that parental conflict and economic difficulty are the most stressful and have the greatest effect on child adjustment. Stevenson and Black also suggest that the legal process of marriage may have the greatest impact on these two f actors. Stevenson and Black cite Miller, who suggests that the legal process of divorce is influenced by untested assumptions and unsubstantiated generalizations. Negative effects of divorce are greater for boys compared to girls, and the need for parental role models are examples of these assumptions. Stevenson and Black state that policies encouraging early interventions from informed lawyers, mental health professionals or family life educators may encourage optimal family functioning and decrease par ental conflict.

Stevenson and Black (1996) cite Wallerstein (1991) concerning future research directions. Long-term studies examining the psychological effects resulting from economic hardships of children of divorce are needed. We need more studies detailing ethnic and racial variations of the effects of parental conflict and economic difficulty. 

The Handbook of Texas Family Law (200 0) provides the Texas Family Code and explains the provisions of the code. The seven grounds for divorce listed in the Texas Family Code (insupportability, cruelty, adultery, conviction of a felony, abandonment, living apart, and confinement in mental hospital) may be an excellent resource for the court, lawyers, or mediators. Future research could examine the relationship between the two factors above, offered by Stevenson and Black (1996), and the seven grounds for divorce. These grounds for divorce ma y serve as a warning to the legal community and to social policy makers, and allow them to respond in ways that are least detrimental to families going through divorce. The needs of the family and the potential welfare of the child could be better understood by the legal system, which could be the largest contributory and influential factor to parental conflict and economic difficulty.
