  And unto the Fourth Generation:

Family Victimization from Within
    Victimization is most often associated with individuals; however, an entire family may also be victimized. Since the family continues to be the basic unit of society and it is within the family that the primary socialization of children occurs, it can be seen that the effects of family victimization are long reaching (National Network for Family Resiliency, 2002).

Some researchers believe family abuse to be the most damaging of all forms because the supposed source of comfort is also the source of pain (Boss, 2002). Neglect of a child, especially her emotional need for love and support, is a form of abuse that may carry the greatest risk of serious social and psychological problems (Kinard, Strauss, & Williams, 1995). 

Families that can be taught coping strategies for stressor events and skills to meet individual needs within the family may find increased capacity to survive long-term stress and crisis (National Network for Family Resiliency, 2002). Families sometimes find that overcoming and surviving a crisis makes them a stronger unit than they were previously.

Finally, it is possible that each person is born with an innate capacity for re silience in the face of difficulties. This resilience may enable the development of skills such as problem-solving, social competence, critical thinking and a sense of purpose. The possibility of teaching these skills to high-risk families may be able to alter or even reverse negative outcomes of family victimization.
