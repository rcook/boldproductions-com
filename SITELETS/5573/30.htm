  The Process of Resilience
    Family theorist Pauline Boss (2002) sees resilience as a rela tional process influenced by internal and external contextual factors. Other family scientists, Hamilton McCubbin, Jo Futrell, Elizabeth Thompson, and Anne Thompson (1995), agree and identify three internal factors: 
  • Harmony in the individual's mind, body, and spirit
  • Interdependence between family members
  • H olistic interrelationships within the family

External factors are those mutual interactions between formal and informal networks. Relatives, friends, and neighbors within the community provide the needed support (Pitzer, 1997). 

Boss asks how cultural values and beliefs fit with family resilience. McCubbin and colleagues (1995) address this question and give exampl es. To these researchers, cultural values and beliefs represent protective tools families can use to survive stress. Cultural identity is believed to promote self-esteem, pride and well being among family members. And cultural values motivate the family unit to take action moving toward positive change. These researchers suggest that family rules and coping patterns passed down through the generations help build family resilience (McCubbin et al., 1995).

However, Boss (2002) warns about faulty beliefs and values that can aid negative patterns of coping and resilience within the family. For example, the wife "who tolerates abuse and then prides herself on being resilient enough to take the abuse" may cause her family to remain in an unhealthy place (p. 83). 

In order for resilience to be deemed healthy, a family must have the courage to challenge the source of stress and to embrace change.
