  12-Step Recovery Programs:
Mastery Orientation or Fatalism?
    When individuals follow a 12-step recovery program are they taking control of the situation or leaving it in the hands of a Higher Power? 

Pauline Boss (2002) explains an individual or family with a mastery orientation may believe that they can change a situation by taking action against the problem. Family members who practice fatalism believe that they cannot change the situation but that change, if it is meant to be, comes from a Higher Power.

Often addiction recovery revolves around The Twelve Step Program. Adapted from the Bible, the Twelve Steps were applied to alcohol addiction recovery by Bill W. in 1935 and have been applied to many recovery programs since then (Alcoholics Anonymous).

The steps outline a plan of action for the addict to master the psychological and physical dependence of the addition. These programs usually involve week ly meetings, mentors and support groups. The steps focus on personal reflection under the guidance of a Higher Power. 

The Twelve Steps of Alcoholics Anonymous begin as follows:

1. Admit you are powerless over alcohol-that your life had become unmanageable.

2. Believe that a Power greater than yourself could restore you to sanity.

3. Make a decision to turn your will and your life over to the care of God, as you understand Him.

Do the Twelve Steps advocate mastery, acceptance or both?
