  Strengthening Resilience in Clergy
    Boss’s (2002) definition of family can be applied to churches, school systems, businesses, governments, and to groups of clergy. Thus, if family and stress theories are relevant to families then they may also be relevant to groups of clergy.

David Papero (1996) uses Bowen systems theory to connect families and organizations. Papero emphasizes the interdependence of families at the physiological, mental and behavioral levels and suggests that this same interdependence influences the functioning of o rganizations and groups. An individual problem may reflect a similar problem in the larger group. 

Steven and Sybil Wolin ( as cited in “How to Survive,” 1992) promote nurturing resilience in families. Resilience is the capacity to rise above adversity and forge new strength – a shift in focus, a reframing of the issue. Seven attributes complement resilient behavior: insight, independence, relationsh ips, initiative, humor, creativity, and morality.

It is time to think of and describe clergy collegial relationships as “family,” shifting focus from pathology to health. If clergy purposefully teach “family” members how to live in healthy relationships, resilience will be fostered. Our lives can be transformed from individuals who are crisis-focused to pastors who are mobilized, energized, empowered, resilient, and who thrive in the face of adversity. W e can focus on health! As theorist Froma Walsh (1999) says, “In strengthening resilience, we inspire people to believe in their own possibilities for regeneration.”
