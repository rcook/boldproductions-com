  Chronic Family Stress and Fatalism
   

When referring to long-t erm, chronic, family stress, Pauline Boss (2002) states, "the value orientation of fatalism may be dysfunctional in that it prevents systemic change" (p.147).
With a fatalistic view, the family either is in denial of the situation or there is nothing that they can do to help the situation. The family's inactivity or passivity in dealing with the chronic illness causes the family to live with a high level of stress.

After seve ral years of progressive medical care, exceptional rehabilitation treatment, and special adaptations made to his home, a quadriplegic father could still not take charge of his life. Opportunities came to him in the way of advanced education, part-time work, and volunteering with those who were new to the world of quadriplegia. But he refused all opportunities. Days turned into months with the father never leaving his home. When asked why he would not try one of these opportunities to move forward with his l ife, he explained that he was doing all that he could do, that he had nothing more to give. 

His passivity left the rest of the family in limbo, living with a lot of stress. This family unit could not change and grow because of the father's fatalistic value system. The fatalistic orientation is a barrier to coping because it encourages inactivity instead of action. 

Reference

Boss, P. (2002). Family stress management: A contextual approach (2nd ed.). Thousand Oaks, CA: Sage.
