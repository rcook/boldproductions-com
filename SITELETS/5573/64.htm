  Gender Issues and Culture
    According to Pauline Boss (1988), boys and girls are rais ed differently. Males are socialized to be strong, handle family problems and never show signs of weakness (p. 114). Females, on the other hand, are socialized to be ultra feminine. They caregive for many generations of families.

Cultures often send negative messages such as:

Big boys don't cry.

You'r e the man of the house.

Girls don't get dirty.

Girls are not good at math and science.

Society promotes the idea that males are superior to females. This contributes to the high rate of suicide among older males after retirement in later life. Both genders are surprisingly faced with adve rsity and equality at this point.

Girls see fictional fairy tale characters as "rescuers." This notion continues today. As adults, some women continue searching for a "knight in shining armor, riding a swayed back horse." This rescue mentality creates stress and encourages unhealthy relationships within families. Divorced, single or widowed women may wait for help from males the rest of their adult lives.

Polly Eisendrath (1996), a psychologist, says overt and covert messages to females about their strength undermine resilience (p. 97).

Several organizations have promoted efforts to increase self-esteem, career exploration, science and math involvement among girls. Girl Scouts of America's motto is "be prepared."
