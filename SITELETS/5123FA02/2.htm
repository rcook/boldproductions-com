   Systems Theory
    Boundaries

Boundaries define who is in and who is out of a particular system. They serve to regulate the flow of information and feedback with other regulated systems. Some boundaries are physical and psychological in nature. A physical boundary is a family's house. A psychological boundary is shared values or feelings such as dating rituals and religion.

Boundaries are also open and closed. Cl osed boundaries minimize interaction with the environment or other systems. There is high conformity and low conflict. Open boundaries allow group consensus, individual expression and balance interaction with the environment and other systems. Conflict and conformity are variable.

    C ybernetic System

Weiner (1948) wrote that the word cybernetics is taken from the Greek kybernetes, meaning steersman. From the same Greek word, through the Latin corruption gebernator, came the term governor, which has been used for a long time to designate a certain type of control mechanism. The basic concept that the investigators of cybernetics are describing is that of a feedback mechanism, which is well represented by the steering engine of a ship (Becvar & Becvar, 2000). The cybernetic system or control mechanism in relation to family could be the dictates of what the particular society or culture mandates is acceptable. As long as these dictates are followed there is equilibrium or a steady state.

"Cybernetics is the art of creating equilibrium in a world of possibilities and constraints" (Von Glasersfeld, 1996).

    Differentiation of Self

The ability to separate one's emotional and intellectual functioning. According to Bowen, the higher an individual's differentiation of self, the less prone the individual is to dysfunction. The lower the level of one's differentiation of self, the greater the influence others have on one's functioning, and the more an individual tries to control the functioning of others. 

Bowen contends that an individual's differentiation of self develops during childhood and adolescence, and rarely changes without extreme motivation to change.

    Emotional Cutoff

Emotional cutoff is one way a family system can manage unresolved emotional issues. It occurs when a member reduces or eliminates emotional contact with other members by cutting off all contact, physically moving away and visiting infrequently, or remaining in contact but evading unresolved issues.

Relationships appear improved; however, the unresolved issues are dormant and can produce imbalances in other relationships. If the family remains in contact, periods of interaction may be tainted with underlying emotions, leaving members exhausted, anxious, or sick. Other encounters may produce yelling or blaming episodes. Less differentiated people usually have more unresolved issues.

    Equifinality

Different beginnings following different paths will result in the same end. Thus, if my family becomes closer because of a sudden catastrophe and another family becomes closer because of therapy, the two families have achieved the same end by different paths. "The same origin may lead to different outcomes, and the same outcomes may result from different origins." Just because families follow a different path does not mean the outcome cannot be the same. One family can be happy living a lifestyle that another happy family would consider chaotic (Gladding, 2000, p.305).

Gladding, S. T. (2000). Counseling: A comprehensive profession (4th ed.). Upper Saddle 
River, N. J.: Merrill.

    Equipotentiality

Equipotentiality is starting w ith the same original conditions and changing or going different ways. Family system members can share a very similar upbringing, but turn out to be very unlike each other.

Twins can eventually take different roads, grow into individuals with their own insights, values, habits and preferences. Consciousness guarantees that what we choose to make of our original conditions is more important than the conditions themselves. An example is a survivor of abuse . The individual who owned the pain moves on. The one who doesn't becomes a chronic victim and will probably have a succession of revictimizing situations.

    Family Projection Process

The primary process by which parents transmit emotional problem s and immaturities to their children. This process follows three steps. First, the parent focuses on the child out of fear that the child has some type of problem. The parent then interprets the child's behavior as confirming the fear. Consequently, the child is treated as though the
problem actually does exist. The projection process is a self-fulfilling prophecy, because while the parent attempts to correct the "problem," the child begins to embody the problem.

    Feedback

Feedback is defined as information that comes in and goes back to its origin after completing the loop. Feedback allows for balance and represents a concept of homeostasis. Information that is feed into a system is fed back into the system to maintain a constant level or to regulate actions. Feedback arrangements are used widely in technology to allow for stabilization and are also used in human systems.

    Hierarchy

Hierachy is the "layering" of systems of increasing complexity. It includes subsystems, systems and suprasystems. Subsystems are smaller parts of the same system. Systems are the system itself. Suprasystems are larger systems such as economic and political systems (Boss, 1993).

There are two major concepts of hierarchy of organizations. They are hierarchical constraints and emergent properties. The higher levels include and are more complex than lower levels. 

Emergent properties are cultural and social phenomena that emerge from the interactions and exchanges of the members of a social system. This includes group roles, norms, goals, shared experiences, shared vocabulary and viewpoints.

Boss, P. G. (1993). Sourcebook of family theories and methods: A contextual approach.

    Holism

Holism is defined as looking at a system as a whole that cannot be taken apart. Every whole can be understood in the context of the larger whole containing it. Wholes have emergent properties that come together as a whole and are not subject to further isolation from each other. Higher wholes emerge from lower wholes. Holism look s at the interaction between families and the environments without separating the family from a much larger complex society.

Stein, I. (1974). System theory, science, and social work. Metuchen, NJ: Scarecrow 
Press.

© 2002 Wendy Fitts

    Homeostasis

Our bodies have an automatic tendency to keep bodily functions on an even keel or steady state. "In family terms, homeostasis refers to those internal, ongoing, sustaining, dynamically interactional processes that take place within a family and help assure internal balance" (Goldenberg & Goldenberg, 1991). An example is when parents are arguing and they realize their small children are listening: they will decide to settle their differences after the children have gone out to play or been put to bed for the night. Thus homeostasis has been restored (p. 38).

Goldenberg, I., & Goldenberg, H. (1991). Family therapy: A n overview (3rd ed.). 
California: Brooks/Cole.

    Interdependence

Interdependence is the degree to which each element of a system influences and is influenced by the other elements (Meade, 2002). Interdependent components are the people and roles they play that make up the system. As roles are assumed by family members, their functions are assigned, too. The actions of each member affect the actions of other components, producing mutual influence. Do the group members rely on one another to achieve shared goals? Or do group members attempt to achieve shared goals individually?

Family system members succeed when they are able to count on each other to accomplish daily goals.

    Morphogenesis

"The ability of the family to modify its functioning to meet the changing demands of internal and external factors is known as morphogenesis" (Gladding, 2000). Morphogenesis usually requires a second order chang e (making an entirely new response) rather than a first order change (continuing to do more of the same things that have worked previously). Instead of just talking, family members may need to try new ways of behaving (p. 305). For example, when children reach their teen years the problems become different, requiring different methods to solve.

    Multigenerational Transmission Process

The process by which minor differences in the levels of differentiation between parents and children evolve to pronounced differences in differentiation among multigenerational family members. The information that produces these changes is transmitted via relationships. The transmission is complex and multidimensional involving both conscious and unconscious processes as well as emotions and behaviors. This process in teracts with genetically transmitted data to mold an individual's self. Replication of this process continues over family lines with differentiation becoming more pronounced with each generation. Thus, the origins of many human conditions, whether positive or negative, have endured for generations.

    Nuclear Family Emotional System

Four relationship patterns direct family experiences during increased and prolonged tension: 

Marital Conflict - Spouses become hypersensitive to each other's flaws; each attempts to control the other and both resist.

Dysfunction in one spouse - An over accommodating spouse relinquishes so much self-control that the resulting anxiety produces dysfunctional ailments.

Emotional distancing - Individuals distance themselves from others to reduce the tension associated with the relationship.

Impairment with one or more children - When parents project anxiety on a child, the child becomes more sensitive to parental expectations, needs, and attitudes. This undermines the child's differentiation from the family.

   < /td> Positive and Negative Feedback

Positive and negative feedback operate constantly and together. They allow the system to change or maintain depending on what kind of information it can integrate through the feedback mechanism. Negative feedback serves to maintain the status quo. It keeps the system within established parameters to maintain a steady state. Positive feedback serves to change the system by amplifying the deviation and at times destroy the steady state. The positive feedback may change the system but also allows for new growth in the system.

Stein, I. (1974). System theory, science, and social work. Metuchen, NJ: Scarecrow 
Press.

    Self-Reflexive

Self-reflexive is defined as the ability humans have to examine and explain the self and its behavior. Self-reflex ive ability is what makes human unique from other systems. Humans, unlike nonhuman systems, have self-awareness. This allows humans to reflect on past and current events and think abstractly to set or change goals. Self-reflexive ability allows interaction and communication with others to send and receive messages of activities, behaviors and symbolic content.

    Sibling Position

Bowenian concept that suggests that people who grow up in the same sibling position predictably exhibit some important common characteristics. For example, oldest children tend to be leaders and youngest children tend to be followers. 

Bowenian theorists maintain that these characteristics carry over into adult relationships. If people marry spouses that complement their sibling position, their relationship is more likely.

    Social E motional Process

In addition to the emotional system acting as a force on relationships in a family, it also acts as a force within society. The societal emotional process promotes both progressive and regressive periods within a society. It also impacts how well it adapts to the challenges it faces. During periods of regression, the increased anxiety results in more decisions focused on the short-term in an effort to relieve the anxiety as opposed to long-term principled decision-making. The focus on easy solutions, along with looser standards within the society, makes raising children difficult, especially for less differentiated parents.

    Triangles

According to Bowen, the triangle is the smallest, stable relationship system. A two-person system will inevitably bring in another person in order to relieve anxiety and stabilize the system. The tension can then be shifted around three relationships. This may stabilize the system, but there is no real resolution.
