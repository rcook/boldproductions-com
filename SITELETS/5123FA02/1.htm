   Symbolic Interactionism
    Symbolic Interactionis m

Symbolic interactionism is what Nye and Berardo (1966) called a conceptual framework, rather than a theory (Longmore, 1998). This framework emphasizes interaction among individuals and incorporates definitions of meaning, self, identity, and their relationships to behavior. 

Its philosophical roots are traced back to the Scottish Enlightenment and American pragmatism around the early 1900s. It is based on the work of Cooley, James, Mead and Blumer, who coined the term symbolic interactionism. Social interaction influences the way human beings attach meanings to symbols and language, shape their reality, and examine and modify their behavior.

Crooks, D. L. (2001, January). The importance of symbolic interaction in grounded theory research on women's health [Electronic version]. Health Care for Women International, 22, 11-28. 

Longmore, M. A. (1998). Symbolic interactionism and the study of sexuality [Electronic version]. Journal of Sex Research, 35, 44-58.

    Symbols

Symbolization highlights the process through which events, situations and objects in the environment take on meanings and elicit feelings and actions. In the context of symbolic interactionism, language, gestures and rituals are symbols; and through social interaction and relationships, symbols attain their meanings and are essential to expressions of thought.

Crooks, D. L. (2001, January). The importance of symbolic interactionism in grounded theory research on women's health [Electronic version]. Health Care for Women International, 22.

Snow, D. (2001). Extending and broadening Blumer's conceptualization of symbolic interactionism [Electronic version]. Psycholog y & Behavioral Sciences Collection, 24(3), 367-377.

    Scripting

A script is a set of rules and exp ectations associated with particular roles each of us play in society. According to Longmore (1998), the use of scripting could have emerged out of the symbolic interactionist framework. The actors obtain meaning and direction in their behavior via language and relationships in society.

Longmore, M. A. (1998, February). Symbolic interactionism and the study of sexuality [Electronic version]. Journal of Sex Research, 35, 44-58.

    Family

The best definition of family from a symbolic interactionist's perspective comes from Ernest Burgess, as he defines the family in the title of his 1926 article, "The Family as a Unity of Interacting Personalities" (LaRossa & Reitzes, 1993, p. 141). This defines family roles as being dynamic. It also illustrates that a change in one member's behavior will affe ct the rest of the family's behavior and how they interact with one another. As families are social groups, culture and society influence them, as well. 

LaRossa, R., & Reitzes, D. C. (1993). Symbolic interactionism and family studies. In P. Boss, W. J. Doherty, R. LaRossa, W. R. Schumm, & S. K. Steinmetz (Eds.), Sourcebook of family theories and methods: A contextual approach (pp. 140-163). N ew York: Plenum Press.

© 2002 Karen Kinman, RN, MS
    Self

For symbolic interactionists, clearly the most important concept is that of the self. Made up of two components: object and process. Basically it is both an answer to the question "Who am I?" and the internal process by which this answer is derived. The self can become an object to itself.

Stephens, R. C. (1991). The secret addict role: A theory of heroine addiction. Albany: State University of New York Press.

© Corey D. Allan, M.Ed., NCC, LPCI

    Identities

Self-meanings that are shifting and dynamic within a role. One's identity does not merely emerge from society but the actor and his or her interactions within their social context create it. Situations often indicate or limit one's identity or identities. Therefore, identities are usually organized into an identity salience hierarchy, which is the probability of an identity being used in a given situation or situations.

Cassidy, C., & Trew, K. (2001). Assessing identity change: A longitudinal study of the transition from school to college [Electronic version]. Group Processes & Intergroup Relations, 4(1), 49-61.

Herda-Rapp, A. (2000). Gender identity expansion and negotiation in the toxic waste movement [Electronic version]. Sociological Quarterly, 41, 431-443.

© 2002 Corey D. Allan, M.Ed., NCC, LPCI

    Role

Simply stated, a role is the behavioral expectations of the occupant of the role, or the actor. These expectations must be defined from the actor's viewpoint and the "goal is not solely to understand interaction processes but rather to understand the general and regularly predictable behavior of a group of individuals defined by their roles" (Stephens, 1991, p. 23).

Stephens, R. C. (1991). The secret addict role: A theory of heroine addiction. Albany: State University of New York Press.

© 2002 Corey D. Allan, M.Ed., NCC, LPCI

    Definition of the situation

Also known as the Thomas theorem or axiom, developed by William Isaac Thomas and Dorothy Swaine Thomas. Simply stated, "if men define situations as real, then they are real in their consequ ences" (Smith, 1995, p. 12). Therefore human action cannot be understood apart from the subjective interpretations that go along with the situation. For example, if a parent interprets an infant's crying as malicious, then that interpretation will have direct implications on how the parent will interact with the child. This interpretation would increase the likelihood of the child being hit by the parent.

Boss, P. G., Doherty, W. J., LaRossa, R., Schumm, W., & Steinmetz, S. K. (Eds.). (1993). Sourcebook of family theories and methods: A contextual approach. New York: Plenum Press.

Smith, R. S. (1995). Giving credit where credit is due: Dorothy Swaine Thomas and the 'Thomas Theorem' [Electronic version]. American Sociologist, 26(4), 9-29.

© 2002 Corey D. Allan, M.Ed., NCC, LPCI

    Boundary Ambiguity

In simple terms, this terminology means not knowing who is in and who is out of the family, not knowing who does what task and not knowing what rules the family will use to solve problems when stress occurs. An example would be a parent is an alcoholic. The parent is physically there, but not emotionally available to the family system. Families are systems. Systems must maintain their boundaries if they are to survive and not collapse under pressure. In order for a family to maintain its boundaries, the family must know who is in and who is outside the family.

Boss, P. (1997). Ambiguity: A factor in family stress management. Retrieved October 26,2002, from University of Minnesota, Extension Service Web site: http://www. extension.umn.edu/distribution/familydevelopment/DE2469.html

    Social Construct

The word construct means to make or to build. The word societal is from the root word society. It means of or relating to society. Thus societal construct would be those procedures or ideas made up or built by society. An example of this might be the notion of society to separate special children from the so-called "normal" children for teaching or social purposes.

Webster's new collegiate dictionary (8th ed.). (1981). Springfield, MA: G. & C. Merriam.

    Social Interaction

When members of a group, that is two or more people, share information and are with each other on a regular basis, there is interaction (verbal and non-verbal). This interaction greatly influences the attitudes and behavior of the individuals within this group.

    Physica lly Present/Psychologically Absent

A situation in which a person's body is physically present, but his/her mind is not available to hear, understand, care, nurture, assimilate or process what is happening in his/her environment or setting. An example: In the case of the Alzheimer's patient, caregivers often perceive their mate as physically present, but psychologically absent. (Boss, 1993, p. 164).

Boss, P. G. (1993). The reconstruction of family life with Alzheimer's disease: Generating theory to lower family stress from ambiguous loss. In P. Boss, W. J. Doherty, R. LaRossa, W. R. Schumm, & S. K. Steinmetz (Eds.), Sourcebook of family theories and methods: A contextual approach (pp. 163-166). New York: Plenum Press.

    Symbolic Reconstruction

We all use sounds, movements, gestures, dialogue, objects - all forms of communication and interaction, part of the process of symbolic reconstruction (Wilson, 2001) that we engage in to develop our view of the world we live in.

That is a very post-positivist statement. However, it draws upon the very concepts that are at the core of symbolic interactionism, a social theory that was developed in the early 20th century. 

The generalized, socially accepted symbolic reconstructions that people subscribe to can be as varied as the very number of societies or social configurations that exist in the world (Papastephanou, 2001).

Papastephanou, M. (2001). Reformulating a reason for philosophy of education. Educational Theory, 51(3), 293-305.

Wilson, B. (2001). Arts magnets and the transformation of schools and schooling. Education & Urban Society, 33(4), 366-388.

© 2002 Gary Gardner

    Cooley's Looking Glass

Drawing upon the Pragmatist philosophical legacy of the turn of the 20th century, Charles Horton Cooley proposed that an individual's self develops through interactions with others. The self is a social construction.

Each to each a looking-glass
reflects the other that doth pass.
   
   &# 160;          —Charles Horton Cooley

That little couplet succinctly defines his view. He further elaborated that the process occurs in three ways: we develop an idea of self based upon how we think others see us, we also perceive how others judge us, as they see us, and then each of us has some inner feeling, such as pride or mortification that colors our image of ourselves.

Coser, L. A. (1977). Masters of sociological thought: Ideas in historical and social context. International Thomson Publishing, 305-307, from http://www2.pfeiffer.edu/ ~lridener/DSS/Cooley/COOLWRK.HTML

    Play Stage

Elaborating upon Cooley's concept of the 'Looking-glass Self,' George Mead described the formation of a person's self as taking on, or making, a role. Role taking and role making, according to Mead, is a process requiring four stages: the preparatory stage, the play stage, the game stage, and the refere nce group stage.

In the play stage, a child takes on roles that he or she sees in others. Taking such roles is taking the perspective of other people—seeing the world from their perspective. The child observes gestures, objects, and language—all a part of communication. The play stage is thus essentially an imitation stage.

    Game Stage

The game stage follows upon the play stage, in George Mead's explication of the process whereby one's self is formed through the taking and making of roles. Where the play stage involves role taking, the game stage involves more and more the making of roles. 

In the game stage, the child is mature enough to now start to construct a self-image and a role, based both upon the learning he or she has experienced in the play stage, and upon his or her own innate abilities to conceptualize and form abstractions.

The game stage is still very much a social process.

    Role Taking

Role taking is defined as putting self in the role of the other person and trying to understand what they are thinking and how they will behave. Another way to put it is simply putting oneself in someone else's shoes (LaRossa & Reitzes, 1993, p. 148).

    Role Making

Role making is the personalization of a role; creating and modifying roles so as to make them more explicit. Role making is a key mechanism of interaction, for all situations and roles are inherently ambiguous, thus requiring us to create those situations and roles to some extent before we can act (LaRossa & Reitzes, 1993, p. 148).

    Negotiations

Negotiation refers to the many ways of "getting things accomplished" and includes activities such as bargaining, compromising, and engaging in collusion. Negotiation creates temporary, social constructed relations that remai n in constant flux, despite relative stability in the basic framework governing those relations (LaRossa & Reitzes, 1993, p. 152).

    Interactions

It is through social interaction that individuals apply broad shared symbols and actively create the specific meanings of self, others, and situations. The presentation of self in everyday life entails using vocabulary and nonverbal c lues to convey and announce one's role and identity while at the same time making inferences about others and the way they are responding to the presentation (LaRossa & Reitzes, 1993, p. 149).
