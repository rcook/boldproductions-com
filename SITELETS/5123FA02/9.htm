   Feminist Theory
    Moderate Feminism

A lso "women's-rights" feminism, it is the least radical feminist stance. Moderates focus on the principle that all people deserve equal opportunity. They recognize that, historically, women have not benefited from this due to socialization, legal inequality, employment discrimination, and lack of resources. Oppression and sexism are seen as institutional rather than methods of benefiting men. Distribution of power is seen as pluralist, as many groups compete for resources. Women's status can be improved in t he current system through education and working with men, yet women are responsible for leading the fight. Ideal society includes androgyny and social justice.

Deckard, B. S. (1975). The women's movement: Political, socioeconomic, and psychological issues. New York: Harper & Row.

© 2002 Molly Kuzmich, MS, LPC

    Socialist Feminism

Oppression of women is seen as stemming from class systems and economic systems. Capitalism benefits from sexism, and as housewives in capitalist society, women's work is valueless. Women 's position is affected by sexual and reproductive freedom, and by the role of raising children. A socialist feminist revolution would include equal employment, socializing housework and childcare, and re-education of men. Socialist feminists demand equal pay for equal work, equal educational and employment opportunity, free abortion, and free childcare.

Deckard, B. S. (1975). The women's movement: Political, soc ioeconomic, and psychological issues. New York: Harper & Row.

© 2002 Molly Kuzmich, MS, LPC

    Radical Feminism

Patriarchy, male domination and oppression of women, defines society. Sexism is seen as psychological rather than economic, and men derive self-esteem from domination of women. The sex class system relegates women to the role of breeder. Women are biologically bound to childbearing (prior to reliable birth control) and dependent on men for physical survival. Radical feminists see the need for revolution rather than reform. Revolution includes destroying the family as it is known, abolishment of sex roles, equal responsibility in child rearing, a socialist economy, integration of women and children into larger society, and sexual freedom for women and children.

Deckard, B. S. (1975). The women's movement: Political, socioeconomic, and psychological issues. New York: Harper & Row.

© 2002 Molly Kuzmich, MS, LPC

    Patriarchal Terrorism

A pattern of escalating violence perpetrated by a husband on a wife in attempt to gain and maintain control. The methods of control include, but are not li mited to, physical violence, isolation, threats, and economic dependence. Common characteristics of patriarchal terrorism include: wives are more often victims of violence by men than vice versa, the average number of annual incidents of violence is extremely high, and the violence escalates to more and more severe forms. Data regarding patriarchal terrorism tends to be gathered when the woman seeks help from shelters, emergency rooms, etc.

Thompson, L., & Walker, A. J. (1995). The place of feminism in family studies. Journal of Marriage and the Family, 57(4), 847-865.

© 2002 Molly Kuzmich, MS, LPC

    Common Couple Violence

This form of violence in relationships occurs on rare occasions, as minor incidents, and is perpetrated by both husbands and wives in response to common conflicts. Common characteristics of common couple violence include: wives and husbands are equally likely to hit the other, few annual incidents occur within a given relationship, and incidents remain of minor severity and may de-escalate over time. Incidents of common couple violence tend to be reported in national surveys.

Thompson, L., & Walker, A. J. (1995). The place of feminism in family studies. Journal of Marriage and the Family, 57(4), 847-865.

© 2002 Molly Kuzmich, MS, LPC

    Mutual Empowerment

Mutual empowerment involves promoting the development and power of others within a relationship. It is a process by which two or more individuals enhance the other's abilities and actions. It requires each pe rson's capacity to view reality and circumstances from the side of the other and to take the other person's situation into account.

Goodrich, T. J. (Ed.). (1991). Women and power: Perspectives for family therapy. New York: W.W. Norton.

© 2002 Molly Kuzmich, MS, LPC

    Separate Sphere

The private realm (family) carries different rules and relations than the public realm. Women have equal/complementary power inside the boundaries of the family/private realm and power that men hold in the public realm dissolves. The rules of public hierarchy and power difference do not carry over to the private realm.

Ferree, M. M. (1990). Beyond separate spheres: Feminism and family research. Journal of Marriage & Family, 52, 866-885.

© 2002 Maureen Platkowski

    Gender Role

Characteristics (behaviors, attitudes, personality) attributed by the culture or society to each biological sex and viewed as "normal."

Zuker, K. J. (2001). Biological influences on psychosexual differentiation. In R. K. Unger (Ed.), Handbook of the psychology of women and gender. New York: John Wiley & Sons.

© 2002 Maureen Platkowski

    Second shift

Household/family responsibilities in a dual income family that in the majority of cases is disproportioned creating a heavier load for the wife than the husband; thus, creating a second shift.

Blaisure, K. R., & Allen, K. R. (1995). Feminists and the ideology and practice of martial equality. Journal of Marriage & Family, 57, 5-20.

© 2002 Maureen Platkowski

  &nbs p; Bottom Up

Viewpoint taken by feminist restating the question of power distribution, asking who is without power and why are those persons without it. "Bottom up" also raises the complex issue of class difference; that is to say, if there is a bottom, who is at the top?

Boss, P. G., Dohe rty, W. J., LaRossa, R., Schumm, W., & Steinmetz, S. K. (Eds.). (1993). Sourcebook of family theories and methods: A contextual approach. New York: Plenum Press.

    Family

A group of persons who claim relation to one another, with or without claiming residency in the same household, in a variety of parental models (Stacey, 1990, pp. 269-270). In feminism, family is important because it marks the relationships between children and caregivers as primary. These relationships form significant emotional bonds and nurture "non-oppressive dimensions" such as equ ality, fairness, and egalitarianism (hooks, 2000, pp. 37-42).

hooks, b. (2000; 1984). Feminist theory: From margin to center. Cambridge, MA: South End Press.

Stacey, J. (1990). Brave new families: Stories of domestic upheaval in the late twentieth century America. New York: Basic Books.

© 2002 Andrew C. Stoker

    Liberation

The movement of an underprivileged group to gain equal opportunity to resources and societal recognition. Liberation is also freedom and prot ection from injustice in the forms of use and abuse of people for another person's physical, financial, or psychological gain. An example of a liberative action is the United States' Civil Rights movement for women and people of color. Rights to vote, to employment, to legal equality were granted through a long and difficult period in the United States; remnants of the movement still linger in marriage and other cultural norms (Blaisure, 1995). 

Blaisure, K. R., & Allen, K. R. (1995). Feminists and the ideology and practice of martial equality. Journal of Marriage & Family, 57, 5-20.

hooks, b. (2000). Feminism is for everybody: Passionate politics. Cambridge, MA: South End Press.

© 2002 Andrew C. Stoker

    Marriage

The legal union of two mutually equal persons, which may or may not produce, adopt or care for children. The impartiality of the partnership, according to feminist theory, is the most vital component of the union (h ooks, 2000, pp. 78-84). The traditional, patriarchal views of marriage are anathema to a more contemporary view of marriage between a man and a woman, between two men, or between two women. Diversity in marital unions today offers a challenge to the cultural and religious status quo; however, this diversity brings about awareness for equal opportunity for women altogether (Stacey, 1996, pp. 27-33, 126-127).

hooks, b. (2000). Feminism is for everybody: Passionate politics. Cambridge, MA: South End Press.

Stacey, J. (1996). In the name of the family: Rethinking family values in the postmodern age. Boston: Beacon Press.

© 2002 Andrew C. Stoker
    Motherhood

The act of continual parental care and concern engendered by women. Within feminist theory, motherhood has been both valued and devalued by theorists (hooks, 134-135). Toward the end of the twentieth century and the beginning of the twenty-first century, feminists point to the continual redefinition of "mother hood" as it pertains to the continual change of societal norms and values in both the public and private spheres (Baca Zinn, 2000; Freedman, 2002, pp. 305-325; Stacey, 1990).

Baca Zinn, M. (2000). Feminism and family studies for a new century. Annals of the American Academy of Political and Social Science, 571, 42-56.

Freedman, E. B. ( 2002). No turning back: The history of feminism and the future of women. New York: Ballantine Books.

hooks, b. (2000; 1984). Feminist theory: From margin to center. Cambridge, MA: South End Press.

Stacey, J. (1990). Brave new families: Stories of domestic upheaval in the late twentieth century America. New York: Basic Books.

    Patriarchy

A traditional, hierarchical system in which males control power. Throughout Western history, males have maintained power through an intr icate, layered system through, but not restricted to, politics, education, class and religion (Millet, 2000, pp. 24-58). Within the institutional and legal injustices of this male-dominated structure other forms of injustice were created wittingly and, less often, unwittingly, such as value on biological differentiation and governmental regulations on women's bodies (Millet, 2000). These injustices lead to further psychological imagined power of males to violence against women and children, including rape a nd incest (Adams, 1994a; Adams, 1994b; Brownmiller, 1993; Millet, 2000; Okin, 1989).

Adams, C. (1994). Woman-battering. Minneapolis, MN: Fortress Press.

Adams, C. (1994). Neither man nor beast: Feminism and the defense of animals. New York: Continuum.

Brownmiller, S. (1993;1975). Against our will: Men, women and rape. New York: Fawcett Columbine.

Millet, K. (2000; 1969). Sexual politics. Urbana, IL: University of Illinois Press.

Okin, S. M. (1989). Justice, gender, and the family. New York: Basic Books.

© 2002 Andrew C. Stoker

    Power

The display of force, physical, mental, emotional, and/or psychological, against another. In feminist theory, power of defined by the abuse of males against females (see also "patriarchy"). According to Kate Millet (2000), power posits differently in several w ays, including sadistic pornography (Dworkin, 1987; Dworkin, 1981) and misogynistic literature. These forms of power lead to violence that further degrades equivalent relationships between men and women.

Dworkin, A. (1981). Pornography: Men possessing women. New York: Perigee Books.

Dworkin, A. (1987). Intercourse. New York: Free Press.

Millet, K. (2000). Sexual politics. Urbana, IL: University of Illinois Press.

© 2002 Andrew C. Stoker

    Violence

Behavior in which one person, with evident or latent power, hinders, harms, or hurts another person who is without evident or latent power. Framing violence in this way, coupled with power, supposes that power is used and abused against those without the same power; therefore, violence is a behavior that is exerted in an unjust situation. For example, rape is an act that is unjust and unjustified. Rape vehemently harms and hurts a person physically, mentally, emo tionally, and psychologically (Millet, 2000, pp. 44-46). Rape, "as an exercise in power," expedites "hierarchical, authoritarian" structures, which is patriarchy (Brownmiller, 1993, p. 256).

Brownmiller, S. (1993;1975). Against our will: Men, women and rape. New York: Fawcett Columbine.

Millet, K. (2000; 1969). Sexual politics. Urbana, IL: University of Illinois Press.
