   Structural Functionalism
    Orderly Functioning

The process by which a system is organized to be self-maintaining based on a common pattern of norms and values that guarantee reciprocal interdependence of its parts and the subsequent integration of the entire system. The values and norms of the system serve as regulators of conduct for the individuals in the system, providing them with a common set of criteria with which to gage their everyday actions.

Filme r, P., Phillippson, M., Silverman, D., & Walsh, D. (1972). New directions in sociological theory. Cambridge, MA: Collier-Macmillan.

Johnson, B. (1975). Functionalism in modern sociology: Understanding Talcott Parsons. Morristown, NJ: General Learning Press.

    Latency

The latent pattern maintenance-tension management (i.e., latency) is the first function of Talcott Parsons' four-function paradigm for a stable and effective system. The latency component serves to define and maintain a set of common values that guide actions within a system. In additi on, the latency component ensures that the individuals have the required motivation to move the system towards goals by completing the appropriate activities. An example of this function is an educational, religious, or family institution.

    Goal Attainment

Goal attainment is the second function of Talcott Parsons' four-functi on paradigm. Its function is to define the concrete goals within a system's framework based on common values and norms. It is motivated to achieve only those goals that are necessary for the system's functioning and thus maintains a state of equilibrium. An example of this function is a political institution.

    Adaptation

Adaptation is the third function of Talcott Parsons' four-function paradigm. This function serves to provide the facilities needed for goal attainment. In addition, adaptation acts to rationally organize the object world to meet and attain the ends of the system. This includes prioritizing the multiple goals of the system and allowing only those that are supported by the resources to be achieved. An example of this function is an economical institution.

&nb sp;   Integration

Integration is the fourth function of Talcott Parson's four-function paradigm. This function serves to coordinate the comprising units of the system. It also regulates the information passed between the goal-attainment and latency functions, ensuring that the exchanges between the units of the system are smooth and orderly. An example of this function is a legal institution.

    Family Stress

Family stress is a non-routine experience that disrupts a life pattern of a family (a 
group of people living together and functioning as a single household). A change in life 
pattern, whether it is positive or negative, puts pressure on a family and the interplay of 
several variables determines the outcome of the family situation. These variables may 
include the hardship associated with the situation, how it is perceived by the family, the 
coping capacities and the external resources that can be mobilized to manage the stressful 
event.

    Equilibrium

Structural functionalists believe that societies are generally in a normal state of affairs, with the different parts functioning smoothly to contribute to the operation of society. There may be outside disturbances to the normal state causing adjustments to be made within the system tha t return that system to a state of balance. Changes from within the system itself or disturbances from without are compensated for, which causes the maintenance of a stable condition.

    Social Structure

Structural functionalists look on society as a system of social structures. Individual parts of society are interdependent and this interdependency imposes structure on its members. Interdependency exists within and among these structures, and groups and individuals are constrained by them. They are observable uniformities in terms of which action takes place and are functional in that they help society to operate. The notion of social structure implies tha t human beings are not completely free in their choices and actions but are instead constrained by the social world they inhabit and the social relations they form with one another.

    Interdependence

Structural Functionalism was supported in the United States by sociologist Talcott Parsons, who claimed that there were stable structural categories that made up the interdependent system of a society. People, institutions and organizations of society are mutually dependent elements, r elying on mutual assistance, support and cooperation. A change in one part affects other parts. This requires the parts to become aware of the changes, modify actions, and adapt as necessary.

    Expressive and Instrumental Roles

Often identified with the female role in the family, the expressive characteristics are people, roles and actions concerned with the care of common tasks, as well as managing family tensions and conflicts. The instrumental characteristics are associated with people, roles and actions in problem solving and accomplishing tasks and is often attributed to the male role in the family. Talcott Parsons claimed the necessity of these roles in 1955 when he stated that the "bearing and early nursing of children establishes a presumption that the man, who is exempted from these biological functions , should specialize in the alternative instrumental direction" (Boss, 1993, p. 197).

    Anomie

Anomie is to describe personal feelings of distress when feelings do not conform to the dominant values and norms. It involves a disjunction or lack of integration between cultural goals and the legitimate means for attaining them.

    Conformity 

Conformity is the nondeviance category in Merton's typology where one accepts and obtains culture goals and the cultural means to achieve that goal. Conformity may also be defined as a tendency for people to adopt behavior, values, and attitudes of other members of a reference group. There are two major types of conformity: normative conformity and informative conformity. Normative is caused by the desire to be liked an d accepted. In addition, people want to avoid embarrassment and humiliation from other members of the group. This desire leads to the basis of informational conformity in which people conform because they look to others whom they believe to be correct to give them information. Conformity results from internal controls or restraints developed during socialization and is induced through external mechanism.

    Ritualism

    Rebellion 

A category in Merton's typology of deviance where the one rejects both
the culturally approved goals and the means to obtain them while simultaneously they seek to br ing into being a new, greatly modified social structure. Old goals are substituted for new goals and means.

    Deviance

Deviance is nonconforming behavior that has degrees of dysfunction for organization. Causes of deviance lie in the contradictions betwe en the emphasis place on cultural goals and the lack of opportunities for some people to use legitimate means to reach these goals. It does not conform to norms or meet with group or societal expectations.
