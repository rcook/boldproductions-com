   Family Development Theory
    Determinism

The view every event is the inevitable result of antecedent causes. Individuals have no choice in regulating their actions. Instead, their actions may be accurately predicted from the external stimuli that triggers them. 

Although currently rejected, determinism is part of the original assumptions upon which Family Development Theory is based. In relation to family development, determinism assumes a direct cause-effect link between environmental conditions and family status.

The Columbia Encyclopedia, 6th ed. (2001). Retrieved October 19, 2002, from http://www.bartleby.com/65/de/determinism/html

    Dynamic Concepts

The group of properties that govern what goes on between stages. These concepts are involved with what happens to the family system over time! These concepts include transitions, timing, age, and event and stage sequences. From the structure-function and interactional approaches, these and other terms were b orrowed as a system of interacting features. These concepts govern how long stages last and assume all families go through stages as part of the developmental process. For example, transitions are both stage-dependent and duration-dependent.

King, R. J. R. (1969). Toward a theory of family development. In Family relations: Concepts and theories (pp.142-146). Berkeley, California: The Glendessary Press. (Original work published in 1964) 

White, J. M. (1991). Dynamics of family development: A theoretical perspective. New York: The Guilford Press.

    Event

Qualitative change that occurs at a specific point in time. One would not ordinarily use the term "event" to describe a gradual change in some quantitative variable. The change must consist of a relatively sharp distinction between what precedes and what follows. For example, the most useful indicator of family stages is the experience of a family transition "event." A family event or (d yadic event) is experienced and reported by an individual; however, a family event is necessarily an indication of something that happened in the family, although experienced and reported by an individual.

Allison, P. D. (1984). Event history analysis: Regression for longitudinal event data (Sage University Paper Series on Quantitative Applications in the Social Sciences, No. 07-046). Beverly Hills, CA: Sage. 

Featherman, D. L. (1985). Individual development and aging as a population process. In J. Nesselroade & A. von Eye (Eds.), Individual development and social change: Exploratory analysis (pp. 213 - 241). New York: Academic Press.

    Family

A family is a social group of persons, usually related by marriage, birth, or adoption that influences one another's moral and social values and contribute to the emotional/psychological make-up of its individual members. A family can be seen as a system of interrelated and interdependent parts forming a whole. It includes not only the family membe rs, but also relationships, communication patterns with one another, and interactions with the environment and with people who share common goals, resources and commitment to each other over time.

Walsh, F. (1993). Normal family processes. New York: Guilford Press.

White, J. M. (1991). Dynamics of family development: A theoretical perspective. New York: Guilford Press.

    Norms

Norms are a representative standard or value for a given group. An ongoing process tha t develops the attitudes, feelings, and expectations that define acceptable behaviors, they serve to govern the behavior of individual members and the family system as a whole. A norm may be a single or a range of values expressing the typical performance to which any individual can be compared. Norms function to bind the family in a common culture, specifying right and wrong behaviors, and also serve as a mechanism for social control.

Hildebrand, V., Phenice, L. A., & Gray, M. M. (1996). Knowing and serving diverse families. Prentice-Hall: Simon & Schuster.

    Position

The location of an actor or a class of actors in a system of social relationships. It is an analytic point in a social structure. A position may exist independently of a role. As a concept, "position" is similar to "point" in geometry. It can be defined for any abstract social structure, regardless of the real existence of that social structure in any society. Positions must be defined independently of roles in order to make cross-cultural research possible. For example a "husband" is a position defined as a "married man."

Gross, N., Mason, W. S., & McEachern, A. W. (1958). Explorations in role analysis. New York: Wiley. 

White, J. M. (1991). Dynamics of family development: A theoretical perspective. New York: Guilford Press.

    Roles

Roles are defined as a person's organized patterns of actions and attitudes, shaped by the position and function he/she is carrying in relation to one or more oth er person's. It is a cluster of reciprocal behaviors in which certain obligations and expectations of oneself and others are held to be inherent.

Three levels of Roles represented by a family system:

  • Individual roles, which are performed by family members,
  • Subsystem roles, which are carried out by various units within the family (i.e., sibling subsystem and parent subsystem), 
  • Family roles, which are fulfilled by family units for their environments (i.e., social control of its members.)

Perlman, H. S. (1961). Family diagnosis in cases of illness and disability. In Freeman, D.S., Techniques of family th erapy (pp. 55-56). New York: Jason Aronson.

    Stage

A division within the lifetime of a family that is distinctive enough, in its cluster of interaction patte rns from those that precede and follow it to constitute a separate period. Every family experiences stages of development. For example, family formation begins with the event of the first child's birth. The "launching" stage is the period between the departure of the first child and last child from the home. Unlike the use of stage in connection with the maturation of individuals, its use in connection with the family does not connote an idea of progression.

Aldous, J. (1996). Family careers: Rethinking the developmental perspective. Thousand Oaks, CA: Sage. 

White, J. M. (1991). Dynamics of family development: A theoretical perspective. New York: Guilford Press.

    Static Concepts

Those groups of properties that determine what occurs inside a certain stage. These concepts have lasted over time. An example of a static concept is the term norm that describes those actions or behaviors that are expected or typical. An example of a norm follows. Parents of a teenager are concerned because their daughter is extre mely smart and independent. They are afraid she will become an old maid. The parents decide that the mother must "have a talk with her." This example shows that eventually even gender norms can change. Other static concepts include stage, position, and role.

King, R. J. R. (1969). Toward a theory of family development. In Family relations: Concepts and theories (pp.142-146). Berkeley , CA: The Glendessary Press. (Original work published in 1964)

    Structural levels

Strata of family composition within which transitions occur. Family Development theory posits four structural levels:

Institutional level - includes the cultural constructs of marriage and family as well as broadly recognized enterprises like education and career.

Group level - structures like immediate and extended family that are embedded in institutional levels.

Relationship level - in teractions taking place between members of a group, often characterized by dyads or triads. Examples include husband-wife, sibling-sibling, and grandparent-grandchild relationships.

Individual level - focus on a specific person affecting or affected by transition. Investigation at this level may involve individual perceptions.

Rogers, R. H., White, J. M. (1993). Family development theory. In P. G. Bos s, W. J. Doherty, R. LaRossa, W. R. Schumm, & S. K. Steinmetz (Eds.), Sourcebook of family theories and methods a contextual approach (pp. 245-248). New York: Plenum Press.

    Tele ology

Explanation by reference to ends, purposes, or function. Teleology is dismissed in social science work, because it often presupposes an intelligent design.

One of the faulty assumptions upon which the theory of Family Development is based, teleology seeks to explain the existence of family features by analyzing the function of such characteristics. Take for example, early theorists view of the " normal" family. In this family structure, the father is the head of household because his earning potential supports the stability of the family. However, this does not necessarily explain why the husband works.

    Timing

A human construct that determines the way we define reality. Time is a base component of the sociocultural order we take for granted and serves as t he foundation for decisions and actions. Where a family is in time, or in other words, where they are in their life cycle, within an era of social change, in a particular season, day, or hour all play an important role with the individuals in the family unit as well as the unit as a whole! Time historically is used to see how present-day families are different from past families.

Daly, K. J. (1996). Families and time: Keeping pace in a hurried culture. London: Sage.

    Transition

To move from one stage in family development to another. The occurrence of a transition is partially dependent on the duration of the previous stage and the nature of the preceding stage. All families have developmental histories marked by active moments and relative calms. These transitions include points in a day, week, or season. An example of a transition is the shift from children in the home with mother to daycare, as a result of the changing roles of women in today's society. Also, women ar e now allowed to own land and hold more professional positions.

Daly, K. J. (1996). Families and time: Keeping pace in a hurried culture. London: Sage.

Duvall, E. M. (1967). The family life cycle. Family Development, 5, 3. 

Goode, J. W. (1971). World revolution and family p atterns. Family in Transition, 65, 57.
