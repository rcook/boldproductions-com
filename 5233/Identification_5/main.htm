Identifying Researchers & Theorists

[Click on a passage below to learn the source.]

  1. She worked with Buller on the Interpersonal Deception Theory: while humans are not very good liars, they are even worse lie detectors. A strong bias for the truth makes most people assume they are hearing the truth. When a speaker is concerned about being believed, nervousness may emerge and related behavior may make a listener suspect a lie (when there is none). This is most obvious when the listener considers himself/herself a good judge of lying but that is another deception, a self-deception. Even when on guard for lying, listeners mis-judge more than half the time.
  2. This small group theorist at the University of Minnesota proposed Symbolic Convergence Theory, also known as Fantasy Theme Analysis, in 1992. A group's cohesion is increased when members agree to discuss outside issues, producing a "fantasy chain reaction" and "social convergence" (essentially, cohesion). The word fantasy refers to the creative and imaginative interpretations that group members share after they learn more about one another. For example, they may develop shared meanings of words and symbols that produce understanding, laughter when uttered.
  3. This Georgetown researcher's books in the 1990s included Gender and Discourse (1994) and The Argument Culture: Stopping America's War of Words (1999). Published since the 1980s, she has influenced how language and conversation are regarded by lay audiences as well as scholarly ones.
  4. Her 1990 publication on Critical Mass Theory suggested that the natural science concept of critical mass can be applied to interactive media. She said that when the critical mass is reached, an organization can assume universal access by its members. While critics maintain that access to technology can never be assumed to be universal, the theory nevertheless demonstrates that technology adoption shifts analysis (and attention) from the individual to the group.
  5. His research into CMC (computer-mediated communication) includes such assertions as the empirical case for electronic forms of communication being equal to or even superior to face to face (F2F) communication.
  6. This developmental psychologist has specialized in emotional development and expression by children and adolescents. She writes frequently on topics such
as how children come to understand emotion or emotional competence.
  7. In attempts to identify universal expressions of emotion, his international research has included showing pictures of various facial expressions. Debate continues over the relative impact of biology and culture in producing smiles or looks of fear, for example.
