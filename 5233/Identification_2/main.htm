Identifying Researchers & Theorists

[Click on a passage below to learn the source.]

  1. Despite wide criticism of theory particulars, his theory of proxemics from 1966 (The Hidden Dimension) remains the classic literature base for the vocabulary and general concepts for personal space as a part of communication. Regardless of whether the measure of 18 inches is correct, the theorist posited that in the most intimate range, two people's "thermal spheres" may include mutual chemical impact, producing connection of emotions.
  2. He gave us the medium is the message and the world is a global village. This academic became a household word by the late 1960s, when television and magazines invoked his name and his theories in examination of the influence of mass media.
  3. His theory of Pragmatics, also sometimes called the Interactional View, posited that communication has two components: content and relational. When the components are not aligned, miscommunication results. He also proposed that a person cannot not communicate. Some interactional theorists and practitioners agree that members of a family system can change their communication—but only with the help of an outsider.
  4. This world-famous semanticist was dubbed the Samurai Scholar when, in 1968, he met SF State College protestors on their ground (the roof of a van) and pulled the plug on their loud speakers. Despite continuing criticism that the semanticist used his administrator's power rather than persuasion to quiet his campus, he won popular approval on the campus, across the State, and into the U.S. Senate.
  5. His X-Y Theory was introduced in 1960 in The Human Side of Enterprise. Revolutionary at the time, the decades since have seen many versions of his basic theory of management, motivation, and communication with employees.
