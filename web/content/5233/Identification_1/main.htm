1940s & 1950s

Identifying Researchers & Theorists

[Click on a passage below to learn the source.]

  1. Channel and channel capacity (both the  terms and concepts) emerged from the work of this Bell Laboratories scientist. His mathematical theory of communication, published in the 1940s, served as the basis for modern Information theory.
  2. His introduction of kinesics in 1952 spurred the use of film for analysis of body movement. He said that all movement had the potential for meaning but that there were no universal meanings of movement.
  3. His relatively short career (he died at age 44) produced relative semantics and the notion that one's language is tied to one's culture. Less accepted today is his apparent belief that one's language determines one's thinking. This insurance agent turned academic did not document direct observation or research of the populations he wrote about, leading to serious errors about Hopi Indians (based on his conversation with one Hopi member who lived in New York City). Still, his works (along with teacher Sapir's) were quickly institutionalized in communication studies.
  4. His universal grammar essentially reversed all prior thinking about how humans acquire language. Announcing that language skills are innate, he began a new movement of language study as well as a career that has spanned politics, communications, and more. According to a 1992 analysis of more than a decade of coverage in the Arts and Humanities Citation Index, he is the most quoted scholar alive and the 8th most quoted in the history of quoting.
  5. His 1954 model of communication was circular and he warned of thinking of communication as "starting somewhere and ending somewhere." His leadership in the U. S. Office of War Information in the 1940s and in the founding of the first doctoral degree in communications led to his being called the founder of communications as a field of study.
