Identifying Researchers & Theorists

[Click on a passage below to learn the source.]

  1. Their Social Penetration Theory said that communication develops from shallow, non-intimate levels to deep, personal levels. The change comes with increasing self-disclosure over the course of the relationship. Their 1987 publication came after earlier work on self-disclosure.
  2. Calling humans "storytelling animals," this theorist claimed in 1982 that all communication is storytelling. The Narrative Paradigm also considers the listener, who decides what validity to ascribe to the story. That decision is based on how well the story fits the listener's belief system or paradigm.
  3. Relational Dialectics addresses specific conditions (not generalizable) in terms of connectedness / separateness, certainty / uncertainty, and openness / closedness. These theorists thus influenced how we look at behavioral "poles" between communicators in intimate relationships. Their "knots of contradiction" explained how growing closer increases opportunity for conflict. With continued book publications, the theory has become a staple of communication theory in the 1990s.>
  4. Her cross-cultural theory of Face Negotiation addresses communication in a conflict context, making it an attractive choice for study by competitive international businesses. Introduced in 1985, the theory identifies Asian societies as high-context settings and American society as low-context.
  5. From 1982 forward, this theorist questioned the "old guys" like Shannon and Weaver. Instead of accepting communication as transfer of information, this new Critical Theory approach posited that communication (as least as information in the workplace) is the means by which management seeks to maintain dominance and control. His theory promotes negotiation of power and a view of employees as stakeholders whose consent and participation should be sought by management.
