   Family Violence: A Simple Phrase?
    A Research Review

Family violence is a simple phrase, but it encompasses a horrifying list of abusive behaviors, both physical and psychological, inflicted by one family member on another…. The list is endless. There is seemingly no end to the horrors some human beings can inflict on those whom this society calls their 'loved ones.'

—The American Medical News, January 6, 1992 

This seemingly simple phrase, "family violence," (interchangeable with domestic violence, abuse, partner violence, and spouse abuse) may appear simple, but it encompasses an extreme complexity. How is family violence defined? What does it include and exclude? How is it measured in research? What challenges emerge for law enforcement investigating family violence when there are two conflicting stories? How was the violence perpetrated, in what context? These issues and more plague those studying, servicing, protecting, prosecuting, teaching, researching, and legislating in the field of family violence. Scholarly work in this field ranges from immediate tasks such as making arrests (Davis, 1998) to national longitudinal studies (Halpern, et al., 2001).

Violence is a term that is socially constructed; therefore, no one universal definition of family violenc e exists. Ultimately, disciplinarians utilize a multiplicity of definitions, depending on the context of their work. Family violence definitions that are broad and diverse create ambiguity and result in confusion for those working within the field. In contrast, a narrow definition puts limits on what constitutes violence and in turn may minimize or trivialize moderate or minimal violence. The definition one receives depends on who one asks (Barnett, Miller-Perrin, & Perrin 1997; Muehlenhard & Kime s, 1999).

This ambiguous aspect of family violence presents challenges for researchers as Barnett et al. (1997) explains, not only in the criteria used in defining it but also in measuring, sampling, establishing cause and effect relationships, and making comparisons across studies. An example involving measurement is the use of the Conflict Tactics Scale. It fails to account for the motives, consequences and context of the violence (Barnett et. al., 199 7; Halpern, et al., 2001; Johnson & Ferraro, 2000; Muehlenhard & Kimes, 1999 ). 

Investigating family violence by law enforcement officers continues to evolve with the enactment of new legislation, policies and requirements. Pro-arrest and mandatory arrest policies now implemented in most states require officers to arrest someone in almost all domestic violence incidents (Davis, 1998). Law enforcement officers who lack proper training and un derstanding of the dynamics of family violence can create liabilities for both their departments and themselves. Two examples include making a false arrest and making no arrest at all. The most recent state legislation requires that officers receive family violence training in "preventing dual arrest whenever possible and conducting a thorough investigation to determine which person is the predominant aggressor when allegations of family violence from two or more persons are received arising from the same i ncident" (Law Enforcement Training, 2001). The importance of this training and understanding is critical for officers responding to family violence incidents. The following statement by McCarty (2000) only reinforces this need: "Abusers are master manipulators. I have yet to meet a batterer that didn't think he was the victim." 

Recent research by Johnson (as cited in Johnson & Ferraro, 2000) makes the distinction of four major patterns involvin g partner violence: common couple violence (CCV), intimate terrorism (IT), violent resistance (VR), and mutual violent control (MVC). The behaviors and characteristics of intimate terrorism and violent resistance parallel findings concerning violence perpetrated by males and violence perpetrated by females. Reports indicate males' use of violence may be "instrumental - designed to accomplish some goal," i.e., control, while violence perpetrated by females generally involves feelings of entrapment and helpl essness or reactions to being abused (Kimmel, 2000; "National Annual Report," 2002). Studying, researching, teaching, investigating, prosecuting, and intervening family violence in this context should increase clarification and awareness at all levels. 

That seemingly simple phrase called "family violence" has proven to have an elusive meaning. Huge strides have been made in the complex issues surrounding this national crisis, yet much more still ne eds to be accomplished.
