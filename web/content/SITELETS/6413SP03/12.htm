   Academic Achievement and Social Skills:
A Look at Two Longitudinal Studies
    A Research Review

Researchers continue to explore the connection between social skills and academic achievement. Social networks (including friendships and peer relations), positive parent relationships, positive relationships with teachers, prosocial skills, emotionality and social acceptance are some of the social and emotional factors that have been studied as correlates with academic achievement. Various studies have looked at the reciprocal relationship of these variabl es beginning in preschool, over time in elementary and middle school, and during adolescence. (Chen, Rubin, & Lee, 1997; Fletcher & Newsome, 2001; Gumora & Arsenio, 2002; O-Neil, Welsh, Parke, Wang, & Strand, 1997; Vitaro, Tremblay, Gagnon, & Boivan, 1992; Wasik, Wasik & Frank, 1993; Welsh, Parke, & O'Neil, 2001) One of the more complicated research quests in the field of child development is defining the relationship between academic achievement and social skills in children. S pecifically, does academic achievement cause children to have better social skills, or do social skills cause children to excel academically? Two longitudinal studies explored this relationship. 

The first study, A Longitudinal Assessment of the Academic Correlates of Early Peer Acceptance and Rejection (O'Neil, Welsh, Parke, Wang, & Strand, 1997), assessed 540 children from eight elementary schools in a southern California school district once a year during kindergarten, first grade, and second grade. Observations included peer-based measures of children's social acceptance, children's academic performance and classroom adjustment. A primary focus of this study was to address whether peer rejection was predictive of early academic difficulties. 

Low social acceptance in kindergarten was linked to deficits in social skills, work habits, and lower academic performance in first and second grades. Children who were rejected by their peers in kindergarten showed poorer classroom behaviors and math skills in first grade. By second grade, the rejected children were at a significant disadvantage in the areas of social skills, work skills, and academic success. However, peer rejection was more systematically related to problematic classroom behaviors than to actual academic performance outcomes. 

The second longitudinal study, Linkages Between Children's Social and Academic Competence, A Longitudinal Analysis (Welsh, Parke, Widaman, & O'Neil, 2001) explored the question: does social competence influence later academic competence or does academic competence influence later social competence? Expanding on the findings of other research, this study followed a moderately large sample over a three-year span. The final data sample was comprised of 163 students from eight schools in a Southern California school district with comple te data recorded from first to third grade.

Measures of academic competence included report cards and teacher ratings; measurement of social competence included peer ratings of social competence, peer ratings of behavioral characteristics, and teacher ratings of social competence and behavioral characteristics. Results from this study included significant main effects related to gender. Females were rated as more prosocial than males; males were rat ed as being more aggressive than females; and females were rated as having better work habits than males. 

Other findings from this study indicated that for fairly well adjusted school-age children, positive social behaviors were directly related to later academic competence. Lower academic competence was related to social competence consistently over time. Poor academic performance as early as first grade correlated to poor social performance in later grades. Oddly, evidence of a reciprocal relationship was not apparent, which seems to be in contradiction of positive social adjustment effects reported above. 

The major difference between these two longitudinal studies was how each one explored the relationship between social and academic success. The first study by O'Neil et al. (1997), looked at peer rejection and its effect on academic failure, or deficits in the classroom, and ac tually found that patterns of behavior were more indicative of academic success or failure rather than peer acceptance or rejection. The second study by Welsh, Parke, Widaman, and O'Neil (2001) took a more positive approach and focused on positive social behaviors linked to academic competence rather than negative social behaviors and their consequences on academic performance. Sex differences were not significant in any measure in the first study, with both girls and boys seeming to be at risk for academic difficulties; the second study did report sex differences in social/emotional behavior. 

The relationships between academic success, peer acceptance, and classroom behaviors appear to be interrelated, and this suggests that further research is needed in the areas of communication styles in the classroom, especially as it relates to cooperative learning and curriculum designed to enhance peer learning opportunities. Reported differences in male and female social behaviors call for scrutiny of current programs and how they address sex differences. Finally, with the current push for early learning and academic success in American society today, it is essential to incorporate foci on social/emotional skills, including positive behavior management skills, and pro-social building skills in classroom curriculum. The evidence from these studies links academic performance and social abilities, suggesting that success is dependent on both variables. If educators focus on the three R's without focusing on social/emotional aspects of childhood, they will not achieve their desired goals.
