   Adolescent Alcohol Consumption
    A Research Review

According to Brown, Parks, and Zimmerman (2001), research regarding adolescent alcohol consumption has consistently shown racial differences. Although the findings of racial differences are supported by multiple studies, "relatively few compelling explanations have been offered to explain these differences" (Brown et al., Racial differences in adolescent alcohol use section, paragraph 2). 

A factor that is being explored as an explanation related to the racial differences in ado lescent alcohol consumption is religiosity or religious proscriptiveness as found in Benda (1997); Brown et al. (2001); Cochran (1993); and Park, Ashton, Causey (1998). Brown et al. and Benda regarded religiosity in a broad scope by evaluating the degree of participation, or religious fervor, in any religion. Park et al. and Chochran focused more closely on Jewish and Christian denominations.

The question for clarification that is asked by Brown et al. (2001) is: can the link between religiosity and alcohol explain the racial differences found for adolescent alcohol consumption? Brown et al. cited numerous studies to show that adolescent Blacks tend to consume less alcohol than Whites.

The adolescents interviewed for the study were from 10 high schools in Kentucky and 7 high schools in northern Ohio. Data were gathered from students at the beginning and end of 9th grade, the end of 10th grade, and the end of 11th grade through self-administered comprehensive surveys. The majority of the students were either 14 or 15 years old.

The results from this study showed that the greater an adolescent’s religiosity, as related to fundamentalist religious belief, the less likely an adolescent was to consume alcohol. “The most important finding of this study, however, was that different dimensions of religiosity si gnificantly predicted alcohol use and problem drinking for black and white adolescents” (Brown et al., 2001, Discussion section, paragraph 2).

This study showed Black adolescents to be more religious than White adolescents in each of the four questions related to religiosity. However, the link between religiosity and alcohol consumption for adolescents does not appear to be clearly related to race because the religiosity and alcohol consumption link hold s true for both Black and White adolescents. “There are likely other cultural factors that, if examined systematically, might shed additional light on why some adolescents drink and others do not” (Brown et al., 2001, Discussion section, paragraph 7).

Concerning the religiosity of Black adolescents being greater than that of White adolescents, one might explore the possibility that the White adolescents w ith greater religiosity are not in the public school system. Perhaps there are fewer White adolescents with high religiosity available to be interviewed because they are attending private, Christian schools or are being home schooled precisely because of the greater religiosity of their families. Thus, there may be an unrecognized bias to the sample of adolescents surveyed.

Another issue to be explored is the question regarding the number of days in the last month that the adolescent consumed alcohol. For a Roman Catholic adolescent who is very religious and attends mass very often, the question results in a “false positive,” so to speak. The questions related to the number of drinks consumed and the questions related to problem drinking do help address the issue; however, studies that differentiate between denominations and religious uses of alcohol are helpful (Cochran, 1993; Park et al., 1998). 

Park et al. (1998) utilized data for African-American and Caucasian 12th grade students from the National Education Longitudinal Study (NELS:88). The findings from that study concluded that while religious proscriptiveness does have a negative impact on adolescent alcohol consumption, the negative impact disappears for binge drinking. Another area for further exploration of this phenomenon might start with the results from Brown et al. (2001), which found that there was a high relationship between frequent prayer and problem drinking for Black adolescent males. Brown et al. examined problem drinking with related behavioral issues, such as fighting rather than binge drinking that focuses on the number of drinks. One suggestion for the relationship between frequent prayer and problem drinking is that both serve as coping mechanisms.
