   LONGSCAN: Longitudinal studies of child abuse and neglect
    A Research Review

This study, reported by Kotch (2001) began in University of North Carolina Chapel Hill with a systemic sample of 842 mother-infant pairs born between 1985 -1987 in the study of child abuse and neglect in the Carolinas. None of the subjects had been previously reported for child maltreatment. Chosen on the basis of risk criteria, the subjects were likely to be at risk for abuse. Child maltreatment reporting is compulsory in both North and South Carolina.

The study enables various state and local child protective agencies in America to learn how to interpret reports of maltreatment. These reports are used to determine children's eligibility for services and also to report data. Runyan et al. (2001), addresses relations between National Incidence Study (NIS2) and Maltreatment Coding Scheme (MCS) and how they define alleged, substantiated and unsubstantiated maltreatment.

Each cohort is reviewed and followed two times a year. Review of central registry (CR) of child abuse and neglect allegation is made. The researchers' sample for review did not include 54 as substantiated but would have preferred to include them. 

For the first four years the only source of child maltreatment outcome measure was the official report to Child Protective Services (CPS). A model based on ecology of Child Abuse and Neglect etiology was developed.

Logistic regression and general estimation equations (GEE) were used. Analysis hoped for simple categorical outcome that always takes the form of "report, yes or no." GEE was employed because it could handle multicollinearity problems tha t are inherent in longitudinal studies because variables can have values at two or more points in time. 

Child maltreatment definitions of the 2nd National Incidence Study (NIS2, USDHHS, 1988) are more detailed than the yes/no answer and the somewhere-in-between nature of CR. Coding of 27 subtypes of abuses are adapted include severity, chronicity and frequency, and age of the perpetrator.

As data is coded, 10 of 50 cases are transmitted for validity. LONGSCAN uses 545 subjects who had no problem of maltreatment between ages 0-8. Comparisons of data are made between maltreatment coding scheme, N1S2, and official DSS reports. Operations are divided into 5 sites. The Northern site looks at neglect while the Western site looks at severity. The Northwest site looks at chronicity. Any special analysis could draw its data from any of the Longscan sites, as mentioned above. 

States and local child protective agencies in the North Carolina site explore whether substantiated maltreatment predicts dire behavioral and emotional consequences better than unsubstantiated reports. Bi-variate analysis compares statistical relations between behavioral and emotional outcomes at age 8. Between 4 and 8 years of age, reports are substantiated. 

Among reports of high-risk children, there are no differences to show maltreatment, substantiated or not substantiated. Variables evaluated in the study are anger, anxiety, depression, dislocation and stress. 

After reducing 27 sub-systems into 6 types, the question becomes which system, MCS, NIS2 or DSS, predicts child's emotion and behavior better? Which of them captures the experiences of the child whether substantiated, suspected and unsubstantiated? Suspected cases are more likely to be qualitatively alike or similar to the unsubstantiated. 

Maltreatment from the child's point of view must be considered. At age 12, the interview cycle included audio-assi sted computer self-interview. This asked the child to report experiences of abuse in the past year and earlier in his life. The child's report of severe physical abuse was similar to the child's parent's report. About 9% did not agree with each other (Hussey, 2000).

There was variance between the child's report and official CR report. This was from a pool of 180 12- year-olds in which 53% them report severe physical abuse to social services for maltreatm ent. 29% report a history of maltreatment to social services themselves. According to Kotch, Brown, and Ringwalt (1995), unsubstantiated reports are more like the substantiated report. This seems to be the case in the report by Leiter, Myers, and Zingraff (1994). Researchers suggest that this area needs further study. 

Measurement of child's experiences in maltreatment is in the areas of physical, emotional, behavioral, acad emics, and social. Maltreatment manifests into unhappy outcomes for the child. How the child perceives maltreatment is more important. Caring and loving go hand in hand, and each child can recognize one when he sees it. Parents can influence happy outcomes if they are serious and diligent in the skill of caring and parenting. It is also the job of parents to watch out for uncaring attitudes when dealing with their own children.

H ow well a child does in academics is a function of how well or how badly the child is treated at home. Emotionally, the child has to be comforted at all times. Parents must provide the basic necessity for the child. Parenting skills must be encouraged.
