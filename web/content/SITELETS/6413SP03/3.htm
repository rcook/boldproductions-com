   Resilience in Families 
    A Research Review

Since 1989, Rand D. and Katherine J. Con ger (2002) have been part of a team conducting a longitudinal study concerning Midwestern families in the United States. The original name of the 5-year project was the Iowa Youth and Families Project. In this report this study will be termed the "Iowa project" for the sake of clarity. At the start, the study population was comprised of 451 White families who had a child in the 7th grade and another child within 4 years of that sibling's age. To diversify the sample, more participants were added in 1991: 10 7 ninth graders who had siblings within 4 years of their age, and who were from single parent, mother-headed homes. This brought the total sample population to 558 focal adolescents and their families. 

The purpose of the Iowa project was to investigate factors involved in protecting children from adverse effects during their transitions from childhood to adolescence. The project received further funding that allowed following the focal child to age 27. This permitted the investigators to determine how successful the young people made the transition to young adulthood, and even further, to that of becoming parents themselves. During the years since the beginning of the project, the sample has remained at 90% of the original total.

The primary adversity was the economic downturn of their rural community beginning in the 1980s. Iowa is mainly a farming area and was severely affected by this event. Th e researchers studied the effect of the event in the lives of the parents, the focal children, and the siblings. 

The project's theoretical base is the family stress model of economic hardship. This theory proposes that the family's per capita income and negative economic life events, such as a job loss or cutback, places economic pressure on the family. This, in turn, leads to emotional pressures, both internally (i.e., depression), and externally (i.e., hostility, or problems in relationships with others) (Conger, et al., 2002). The researchers used a longitudinal and multi-information research strategy (Conger & Conger, 2002). They interviewed parents and teachers of the focal children, made video recordings of home observations, and examined school records of the focal children to assess their academic progress. In the yearly assessments since 1989, attention has shifted from the focal children's families of origin to the families they are cre ating.

Findings from the project confirmed several hypotheses by the authors. First, parents who used nurturant and involved parenting equipped their children to weather the financial economy more successfully than did parents who used low-nurturant, low-involved parenting. Nurturant parenting consisted of parenting that was supportive, that provided problem solving skills, and that provided effective management in a child's life (Conger & Conger, 2002). Second, children were more likely to be affected only indirectly by the economy. They felt the impact more or less, based on how their parents handled economic loss and their parents' emotions with each other. If the parents continued to feel competent to handle the financial pressure, the children fared better. Third, nurturant and involved parenting was found to be more effective in helping children navigate the transition from childhood to adolescence and later to young adu lthood than was low nurturant parenting. Fourth, during adolescent years, the interactions between teens and parents affected both generations, whether positively or negatively. 

Finally, findings from the project suggested that parents who had low-nurturant, low-involved or hostile parenting impaired their children's opportunity to make successful necessary life transitions. Children in this group tended to have more difficulty in school, and later on, in relationships with others. The latter is a hallmark of the passage to young adulthood. This group, the researchers found, also tended to have difficulty in their own role as parents with their children. In other words, hostile parenting appears to perpetuate itself into the next generation. 

A cross-sectional study of African Americans living in Iowa and Georgia was recently conducted in an effort to replicate the Iowa project (Conger, et al ., 2002). To prevent confusion, this replication study is termed the "Georgia study" in this paper. The purpose of the Georgia study was to determine whether the implications of the Iowa project were also valid for African Americans, since the Iowa project focused only on European families. 

Aside from the racial component being different, the Georgia study was conducted in both urban and rural settings. The Georgia study focused on a population of 422 two-caregiver families. While the majority were romantic partners, a sizable minority were relatives and extended family of the mother. The Georgia study supported hypotheses similar to the findings discussed earlier. The family stress model of economic hardship was a good fit for this sample, specifically because, as a group, African American families suffer greater economic hardship than do European American families. The emotional impact on children is also greater. Still, nurturant and involved pare nting provided protective factors for the children, as occurred in the Iowa project sample (Conger, et al., 2002). The authors conceded, however, that further study of a longitudinal and multi- informational nature needs to be conducted with this population in order to generalize the findings. 

The Iowa study was a logical extension of the children of the depression study launched by Glen Elder (2002). While the children of the depression study exam ined the effect of the depression primarily on the children, the present study examined the interactions between parents and children and how those interactions affected the family system.
