   Wonder Woman, Working Girl, Working Mommy:
Oh, How Does Your Baby Grow?
    A Research Review

The increased number of working mothers, especially in regards to early maternal employment since the latter part of the 20th century, has made a significant impact not only on the marital relationship, but on the parent-child relationship as well. Among unanswered questions about how children and adolescents fare when their mothers work, one that is frequently asked is, "what is the impact on infants whose mothers work full-time?" Current research suggests although there is some influence on the youngsters, the results are not long term and are not completely negative; however, there are some concerns.

Early maternal employment has become the norm in the United States. According to the U.S. Bureau of the Census (1999), dramatic changes for married-couple families with young children occurred from 1 975 to 1998: the labor force participation rate of married women with children under age of 3 rose from 22% to almost 32%, depending on the woman's ethnicity. These percentages represent a major change in how children are cared for in early childhood. Waldfogel, Hans, and Brooks-Gunn (2002) found several questions to be explored: home environment, whether mother breast-fed, and what type of childcare was selected. According to two studies by the NICHD Ear ly Child Care Research Network (1998 and 2000), childcare arrangements have been found to moderate the effects of having a mother who worked the first year.

Home—Where Does Baby Want To Be?

The general consensus from the research has been children whose mothers work outside the home spend a great deal of time in out-of-home childcare. Bates, Marvinney, Dodge, Bennett, and Pettit (1994) found children who spend time in out-of-home care during infancy, as well as children who spend more time in out-of-home care during preschool years, evidenced more negative and less positive adjustment during kindergarten. Colwell, Pettit, and Meece (2001) discussed the children's risk for later problems, reporting the number of non-parental care in infancy places relates directly to some children being at an increased risk for later behavior problems.

In regards to exploring the children being out of the home, Waldfogel et al. (2002) explored the link between early maternal employment and child outcomes, with the question of whether women who return to work earlier may provide a less nurturing or stimulating home environment for their children. There may be increased stress and fatigue associated with employment; consequently, mothers may have less warmth and energy to invest in their interactions with their children.

Nature and Nurture—What Does Baby Eat?

The American Academy of Pediatrics (1997) and U.S. Department of Health and Human Services (1999) both agreed that mothers who worked during their children's infancy were less likely to breast-feed and this might mean less time spent with the child and less closeness with the child. The premise is breast-fee ding provides opportunities for maternal bonding with the child and it is an important maternal behavior. Due to the hours spent at work and commuting time, it sometimes is not possible for the working mother to nurse her child during working hours.

Counting the Costs—Where Has Baby Been?

Both Colwell et al. (2001) and Waldfogel et al. (2002) said another significant question to explore relates to the type of childcare in which the child is placed during the first year of life. Both teams of researchers explored the effects of non-maternal childcare, which is much less commonly used in the first year of life for children whose mothers are not working. In the 2001 study, Han, Waldfogel, and Brooks-Gunn provided the first evidence of persistent negative effects of early maternal employment on c hildren's later cognitive outcomes. In Ask The Children, Ellen Galinksky asked children of working parents what they wanted from their parents. The answers surprised many; and the results verified that children just want to be loved and raised well, that they want the time spent with them be focused, and that they are proud of their parents.

Where Do Mommy and Baby Go From Here?

The working mommy is not leaving. This phenomenon is accompanied by issues of mother balancing employment with her relationship with her child, experiencing "mommy guilt" at leaving her child in out-of-home childcare, making difficult choices about breast-feeding, and not having all the energy she needs to have an ideal home environment.

Waldfogel et al. (2002) found differences among subgroups suggesting some children may be more vulnerable to the effects of early maternal employment and may be more affected than others. The research suggested further exploration into the difficulties of maternal working conditions, poor-quality childcare, the effect of frequent changes in childcare arrangements. Colwell et al. (2001) suggested exploring family background charac teristics as well as the family and peer relationships as children develop and adapt to having been in childcare placements while their mothers worked.

In closing, most of the research on the effects of early maternal employment has suggested some negative impact, and yet, there is much needing exploration. The research is ongoing and needs to explore these children in later life to determine the long term effects of early maternal employment or just ma ternal employment in general. There needs to be research on how to assist the working mother and the child to adjust so that family scientists can help limit the negative impact and improve the mother-child relationship because chances are that baby is going to grow up to either become a working mommy or know one.
