    Exchange Theory
   

Exchange Theory

Exchange or social exchange theory is a semi-economic model suggesting that individuals seek individuals who can provide rewards greater than or equal to the costs encountered. 

The formula capturing the essence of exch ange theory is Rewards – Costs = Outcome. This formula is used subconsciously to decide whether relating with another individual is a good deal or not based on whether the outcome is positive or negative.

Adler, R. B., & Towne, N. (2002). Looking out looking in (10th ed.). Orlando, FL: Harcourt Brace College Publishers.
   

Cost

Cost is the reward forgone; a reward that could have gained from another exchange instead of the chosen exchange. Costs can be three distinct types: 

1)     Investment costs – represents time and effort committed toward acquiring the skills to provide to another.

2)     Direct costs – represents resources one gives to another in an exchange.

3)     Opportunity costs – the rewards foregone from other possible exchanges missed by selecting chosen exchange.

Roloff, M. E. (1981). Interpersonal communication: The social exchange approach (Vol. 6). Beverly Hills, CA: Sage.
   

Rewards

Rewards are preferred resources or pleasures, satisfactions, and gratifications inc luding statuses, relationships, interactions, experiences, and feelings that increase the probability of repeated behaviors. Rewards can be broken down into six categories:

1)     < /span>Personal attraction

2)     Social acceptance

3)     Social approval

4)     Instrumental services

5)     Respect/prestige

6)     Compliance/power

Roloff, M. E. (1981). Interpersonal communication: The social exchange approach (Vol. 6). Beverly Hills, CA: Sage.

Nye, F. I. (1982). Family relationships: Rewards and costs. Beverly Hills, CA: Sage.
   

Particularism

Particularism is the degree of value that a person receives as a reward, which is derived from a particular resource of a valued individual.

Sabatelli, R. M., & Sheham, C. L. (1993). Exchange and resources theories. In P. G. Boss, W. J. Doherty, R. LaRossa, W. R. Schumm, & S. K. Steinmetz (Eds.), Sourcebook of family theories and methods: A contextual approach (pp. 385-411). New York: Plenum Press.
   

Fair Exchange

Fair exchange is the expectation of rewards equivalent to the cost in relationships. It also sets the stage for negative sanctions when expected reward is not granted.

Blau, P. (1964). Exchange and power in social life. New York: Wiley Press.  
   

Outcome-Interdependence Theory

The rewards and costs derived from the relationship seen through comparison levels and comparison levels for alternatives.

Sprecher, S. (1998). Social exchange theories and sexuality. The Journal of Sex Research, 35,(1), 32-43.
   

Outcome (benefits, profits, satisfaction)

Also known as benefits, profits, and satisfaction; it is the rewards minu s the costs in the relationship. 

Sabatelli, R. M. (1988). Exploring relationship satisfaction: A social exchange perspective on the interdependence between theory, research, and practice. Family Relations, 37, 217-222.

If outcome is defined as profits the results can be obtained through bargaining.

Rank, M. R., & LeCroy, C. W. (1983). Toward a multiple perspective in family theory and practice: The case of social exchange theory, symbolic interactionism, and conflict theory.  Family Relations, 32, 441-448.
   

Total outcome

Total outcome is the rewards minus the punishments.

Sprecher, S. (1998). Social exchange theories and sexuality. The Journal of Sex Research , 35,(1), 32-43.
   

Expectation

What one has compared to what is obtainable in the relationship. This expectation may change during the relationship, depending on the investments.

Sabatelli, R. M. (1988). Exploring relationship satisfaction: A social exchange perspective on the interdependence between theory, research, and practice. Family Relations, 37, 217-222. 
   

Equity versus Equality

  • Equity Theory

Equity theory is the degree of perceived balance, the principle of justice and fairness be tween the inputs and the outcomes.

Sprecher, S. (1998). Social exchange theories and sexuality. The Journal of Sex Research, 35,(1), 32-43.

  • Equality

Similar to equity, except it focuses on the balance between the partners’ outcome, equal if both partners are receiving the same outcomes regardless of what they contributed. In addition, the exchange may be one type of resource for another.

Sprecher, S. (1998). Social exchange theories and sexuality. The Journal of Sex Research, 35,(1), 32-43.
   

Comparison Level (CL)

The expectation of what one deserves in a relationship; based on past relationships.

Sprecher, S. (1998). Social exchange theories and sexuality. The Journal of Sex Research, 35,(1), 32-43.

The expectati on is based on normative relationships within society and cognitive relationships from personal experiences. When the outcome is above the standard, the individual is satisfied and does not seek alternatives.

Sabatelli, R. M. (1988). Exploring relationship satisfaction: A social exchange perspective on the interdependence between theory, research, and practice. Family Relations, 37, 217-222.

  • Normative Orientations

Normative orientations are the relationship norms within society.

Sabatelli, R. M. (1988). Exploring relationship satisfaction: A social exchange perspective on the interdependence between theory, research, and practice. Family Relations, 37, 217-222.

  • Cognitive Orientations

Individualistic expectations derived from personal experiences.

Sabatelli, R. M. (1988). Exploring relationship satisfaction: A social exchange perspective on the interdependence between theory, research, and practice. Family Relations, 37, 217-222.
   

Comparison Level for Alternatives (CLALT)

Comparison level for alternatives is what one expects to receive from an alternative, available relationship.

Sprecher, S. (1998). Social exchange theories and sexuality. The Journal of Sex Research, 35,(1 ), 32-43.
   

Commitment

Commitment is the willingness to participate in the relationship over a prolonged period.

Sabatelli, R. M., & Sheham, C. L. (1993). Exchange and resources theories. In P. G. Boss, W. J. Doherty, R. LaRossa, W. R. Schumm, & S. K. Steinmetz (Eds.), Sourcebook of family theories and methods: A contextual approach (pp. 385-411). New York: Plenum.

Commitment is increased by greater satisfaction, investment, and comparison level for alternatives.

Sprecher, S. (1998). Social exchange theories and sexuality. The Journal of Sex Research, 35,(1), 32-43.
   

Po wer

Power is the ability to extract compliance in an exchange relationship by controlling valued rewards and resources; to improve one’s ability to reward and by reducing another individual’s ability to provide rewards and also by controlling the other’s valued resources. 

Sabatelli, R. M., & Sheham, C. L. (1993). Exchange and resources theories. In P. G. Boss, W. J. Doherty, R. LaRossa, W. R. Schumm, & S. K. Steinmetz (Eds.), Sourcebook of family theories and methods: A contextual approach (pp. 385-411). New York: Plenum Press.
   

Reciprocity

Reciprocity is the spirit of mutuality - considering others when making choices. “It is based on the assumption that people should help, not hurt others especially those who have helped them in the past” (Zimmerman, p.192). Individuals take into account their costs and rewards for others as well as what others might expect from them.

Zimmerman, S. L. (1995). Understanding family policy (2nd ed.). London: Sage. 
   

Positive and Negative Reinforcement

Reinforcement is the process whereby a particular behavior is strengthened (positively) or lessened (negatively), therefore, making it more or less likely for the behavior to continue or to discontinue respectively.

Berger, K. S. (2001). The developing person through the life span (5th ed.). New York: Worth.

   

Trust

 “…a belief in the reliability and integrity of a person” (Strong, DeVault, Sayad, & Yarber, p. 257); that one partner will not take unfair advantage of the other. 

Strong, B., DeVault, C., Sayad, B. W., & Yarber, W. L. (2002). Human sexuality: Diversity in contemporary America (4th ed.). New York: McGraw-Hill.   
   

Resources

Resources are commodities, which can be exchanged through interpersonal behavior.

1)     Love – communication of affection, comfort, and warmth

2)     Status – expression of regard, prestige, or esteem

3)     Services – activities related to a person’s belongings

4)     Goods – tangible products

5)     Information – advice, opinions, or instructions

6)     Money – coin or currency with inherent value

Resources can also be particular or concrete, which refers to the degree of value associated with whom is providing it and the specific form the resource is in during the exchange.      

Roloff, M. E. (1981). Interpersonal communication: The social exchange approach (Vol. 6). Beverly Hills, CA: Sage.  
   

Collectivism

Collectivism is the argument that the behaviors of individuals in social relationships emerge in response to the demands and needs of the social system. The behavior of individuals is influenced by societal de mands.

Sabatelli, R. M., & Sheham, C. L. (1993). Exchange and resources theories. In P. G. Boss, W. J. Doherty, R. LaRossa, W. R. Schumm, & S. K. Steinmetz (Eds.), Sourcebook of family theories and methods: A contextual approach (pp. 385- 411). New York: Plenum Press. 
   

Individualism

Individualism is the belief that social structures emerge as a result of an individual’s attempts to fulfill their own needs and social norms are created because they provide a way for individual to meet their own needs.

Sabatelli, R. M., & Sheham, C. L. (1993). Exchange and resources theories. In P. G. Boss, W. J. Doherty, R. LaRossa, W. R. Schumm, & S. K. Steinmetz (Eds.), Sourcebook of family theories and methods: A contextual approach (pp. 385-411). New York: Plenum Press.

Theorists assert certain virtues to individualism such as self-actualization, self-interest, and self-reliance, but also assert that in pursuit of individualism, there are certain vices such as self-absorption or narcissism, unscrupulous competition, and alienation.

Waterman, A. S. (1984). The psychology of ind ividualism. New York: Praeger.
