    Action< /font>

Action is defined as not only the person's conduct but also the subjective meaning that person attaches to it. There is an expectation that a person moves toward an end or destination. This indicates that the person, his/her life, energy and the meaning attached to all of these aspects becomes a part of an d integrates into who the person is (Adams, 1999). At the same time, the person, his/her consciousness and awareness become a part of the person's world (Wilson, 2002).

Adams, W. W. (1999). Exploring essential phenomenon of human existence: The interpermeating communion of self and world.&nb sp;Journal of Phenomenological Psychology, 30, 39-67.

    Construction of Reality

Through socialization with the family, a person first learns to see the world and internalize a social reality that is deemed as an object reality (Russ, 1981). The first family in an individual's life that serves this function is the family of origin. After marriage, one's family of procreation usually fulfills this function (Burger & Kellner, 1964).

Burger, P. L., & Kellner, H. (1964). Marriage and the co nstruction of reality. Diogeges, 45, 1-25.

Russ, D. (1981). The family's construction of reality. Cambridge: Harvard University Press.

© 2002 Patricia Mullins

    Cultural belief system

Cultural belief system is defined by the concepts and categories that a person uses to make sense of their everyday life in relation to the larger world around them. A cultural belief system is one of seven sources of socialization according to Arnett (1995). (The other six are family, peers, school/work, community, media, and legal.) A broad cultural belief system promotes independence, individualism and self-expression. A narrow system holds obedience and conformity as their highest values (Gubrium & Holstein , 1993).

Arnett, J. J. (1995). Family socialization in its cultural context. Journal of Marriage and the Family, 57, 617-628.

Gubrium, J. F., & Holstein, J. A. (1993). Phenomenology, ethnomethodology, and family discourse. In P. G. Boss, W. J. D oherty, R. LaRossa, W. R. Schumm, & S. K. Steinmetz (Eds.), Sourcebook of family theories and methods: A contextual approach (pp. 651-672). New York: Plenum Press.

    Ethnomethodology

Ethnomethodology is the sociological perspective founded by Harold Garfinkel in the early 1960s to study the ways in which people make sense of their social world. In effect, people create their own reality from whatever information, impressions, or experiences that are present. Garfinkel (1967) suggests a person selects facts or experiences from a social situation and then attempts to fit them into a pattern which is familiar to them. Once the pattern has been established, it becomes a framework for interpreting any new fact or experience.

Garfinkel, H. (1967). Studies in ethnomethodology. Englewood Cliffs, NJ: Prentice-Hall.

    Family Construct 

Family construct is the way in which a family reacts to worldly issues within family. The uniqueness pertaining to family construct is the way in which each family approaches the problems or concerns, because each family responds differ ently although there are shared characteristics (Gubrium & Holstein, 1993).

Gubrium, J. F., & Holstein, J. A. (1993). Phenomenology, ethnomethodology, and family discourse. In P. G. Boss, W. J. Doherty, R. LaRossa, W. R. Schumm, & S. K. Steinmetz (Eds.), Sourcebook of family theories and methods: A contextual approach (pp. 651-672). New York: Plenum Press.

    Family Discourse 

Traditionally family is defined in legal and biological kinship terms, however the non-traditional sociology of the family is called family discourse (Gubrium & Holstein, 1990). Family discourse is a cultural resource that actors use to make sense of their situations (Miller, 1991). Family is conceptualized as a “socially constructed, situationally contingent cluster of meanings”, and a collection of ideas or images used to assign meaning to everyday life (Gubrium & Holstein, 1995). Weber defined family as the process of attaching or detaching meani ng to relationships. For ethnomethodologists that process is accomplished through language and practical reasoning (Gubrium & Holstein, 1993). 

Gubrium, J. F., & Holstein, J. A. (1990). What is family? Mountain View, CA: Mayfield.

Gubrium, J. F., & Holstein, J. A. (1993). Phenomenology, ethnomethodology, and family discourse. In P. G. Boss, W. J. Doherty, R. LaRossa, W. R. Schumm, & S. K. Steinmetz (Eds.), Sourcebook of family theories and methods: A contextual approach (pp. 651-672). New York: Plenum Press.

    Fictive Kinship 

[Of, relating to, o r being fiction; fictional (Dictionary.com)]. Fictive kinship is a socially recognized link between individuals, created as an expedient for dealing with special circumstances, such as the bond between a godmother and her godchild. Fictive kinship bonds are based on friendship and other personal relationships rather than marriage and descent. A fictive relative is a person who is treated like a relative but who is not related by blood or marriage (Chatters, Jayakody, & Taylor, 1994).

Chatters, L. M., Jayakody, R., & Taylor, R. J. (1994). Fictive kinship relations in black extended families. Journal of Comparative Family Studies, 25, 297-312. Retrieved October 26, 2002 from http://www.cicred.ined.fr/rdr/rdr_a/revues/revue79-80/14-79-80_a.html

    Life World 

Life world is the pragmatic world humans consider insignificant. It is a world humans presume has a past tense, a present, and will follow into the future. Things that are among the life world are human dwellings, people, family and things of similar magnitude they we take for granted until we misplace them (Gubrium & Holstein, 1993).

    Organizationally Embedded

Organizational embeddedness is an important aspect of family discourse research (Gubrium & Holstein, 1993). In their research of family discourse, Gubrium and Holstein (1990) have detected family as an important feature of almost every organizational setting they have studied. In these settings, such as nursing homes and courtrooms, family is talked about as an organizing feature of relationships between particular entities to specify interpersonal rights and obligations. Family can be a source of, or remedy for, individual troubles. As a result family is embedded in human service organizations and an important object of organizational processes (Miller, 1991).

Gubrium, J. F., & Holstein, J. A. (1990). What is family? Mountain View, CA: Mayfield.

    Phenomenology

Phenomenological sociology is derived from Schutz’s study of Husserl’s philosophy and Weber’s sociology (Gubrium & Holstein, 1993). Husserl’s goal was to develop a method that was void of all presuppositions and illus trate phenomena by focusing upon that phenomena, to the exclusion of the underlying origins and the status outside the act of consciousness (Phenomenology, 2002). Weber conceptualized sociology as the science that tries “to understand social action interpretively and explain it causally in its course and affects” (Schutz, 1970). Schutz was able to successfully bridge the ideologies of Husserl and Weber together and the phenomenological approach was launched. 

Gubrium, J. F., & Holstein, J. A. (1993). Phenomenology, ethnomethodology, and family discourse. In P. G. Boss, W. J. Doherty, R. LaRossa, W. R. Schumm, & amp; S. K. Steinmetz (Eds.), Sourcebook of family theories and methods: A contextual approach (pp. 651-672). New York: Plenum Press.

Shortly after the “Washington, D.C.     Social Constructionist Approach

Durkheim has been called the "architect" of the social constructionist approach. He sees society as a "force" which inflicts itself upon humans. As a result, society is defined and transformed by the emotional reactions that occur in gatherings and assemblies (Fisher & Chon, 1989). Social constructionists maintain that there is great flexibility in human emotion, emotions are disconnected from biology and emotions are determined by social norms or "feeling rules." Social constructionists follow the symbolic interactionist model and posit that actors have to defin e situations before emotions can be experienced (Kemper, 1981).

Fisher, G. A., & Chon, K. K. (1989). Durkheim and the social construction of emotions. Social Psychology Quarterly, 52, 1-9.

Kemper, T. D. (1981). Social constructionist and positivis t approaches to the sociology of emotions. American Journal of Sociology, 87, 336-362.

    Social facts

Social facts represent collective beliefs, tendencies and practices dictating usages, habits, and mores, assumed to be the appropriate response to typical situations (Gurwitsch, 1974). Social tides motivate individuals towards activities such as marriage, suicide, and higher or l ower birth rates. A product of shared existence, it is a group condition repeated in individuals as actions and reactions are transmitted between individuals’ consciousnesses. It is any way of acting, whether fixed or not, capable of exerting an external constraint over individuals. It is a general societal response independent of its individual manifestations (Durkheim, 1982).

Durkheim, E. (1982). What is a social fact? In S. Lukes (Ed.) The rules of the sociological method (pp. 50-59). New York: Free Press. Retrieved November 9, 2002 from http://www2.pfeiffer.edu/~lridener/DSS/Durkheim/SOCF ACT.HTML

    Social Order

Social order is an ongoing human production, both in its origins, as the result of past human activity and in its existence at any point in time. Social order exists only as human activity continues to produce it, an d only as a product of human activity in the course of his or her externalization. It is not derived from any biological data in its empirical manifestations and it is not given in man's natural environment. It is not part of the "nature of things," and it cannot be derived from the "laws of nature" (Berger & Luckmann, 1966).

Berger, P. L., & Luckmann, T. (1966). The social construction of reality: A treatise in the sociology of knowledge. (pp. 51-61). Garden City, NY: Anchor Books. Retrieved November 9, 2002 from http://www.brainwashed.com/h3 o/Dislocation/reality.html

    Stock of Knowledge

St ock of knowledge is comprised of all interpretation of this world formed by a frame of reference, our own and that handed down to us, that guides our daily experience for dealing with things, coping with situations, and coming to terms with fellow human beings (Natanson, 1974). Language, meanings attached to language, recipes of all sorts, rules for handling things, modes of conduct, behavior and action – all create what Schutz calls the “stock of knowledge at hand.” One’s stock of knowledge is socially derived and grows throughout life (Gurwitsch, 1974).

Gurwitsch, A. (1974). Phenomenology and the theory of science. Evanston, IL: Northwestern University Press.

Natanson, M. (1974). Phenomenology, role, and reason: Essays on the coherence and deformation of social reality. Springfield, IL: Charles C. Thomas.

    Subjectivity (in human affairs)

The sphere of the subject and his consciousness, Husserl called subjectivity pure ego and pure consciousness, “the wonder of a ll wonders.” He considered subjectivity the central mystery - a being that is aware of its own being and of other beings (Spiefelberg, 1982). Subjectivity focuses attention on the meaning an act has for the actor who performs that act. It is a domain readily accessible to the inspection of any investigator properly equipped (Natanson, 1974). The meaning an action has as conveyed between actors, subjectivity is a common practice of everyday experience (Gurwitsch, 1974).

Gurwitsch, A. (1974). Phenomenology and the theory of science. Evanston, IL: Northwestern University Press.

    Verstehen or Understanding 

Verstehen origina ted from Max Weber’s concept of subjective interpretation (Schutz, 1967). For Weber the goal of verstehen is to find the meaning that an actor places on her or his actions and the contrasted meaning that this action has for the actor's partner. For other social scientists verstehen is understanding the motivation behind a person's acti on. This motivation comes from the person's private value system and intuition. Verstehen is the method of dealing with human affairs. It is the way that “common-sense thinking finds its bearing within the social world and comes to terms with it” (Schutz, 1954).

Schutz, A. (1954). Concept and theory formation in the social sciences. The Journal of Philosophy, 51, 257-273.

Schutz, A. (1967). The Phenomenology of the Social World. Evanston, IL: Northwestern University Press.
