   Conflict Theory
    Avoidance

Avoidance is a situation in which one or more of the parties go out of their way not to overtly recognize or deal with the problem. Sometimes this occurs by not being in the same physical proximity or by refusing to discuss the issue.

Avoidance is one means of preventing escalation of a conflict and can be a substitute for resolution of a dispute. Individuals may even trick themselves into believing that there is no problem by using avoidance techniques. Dudley Weeks (1992) proposes, “Avoidance denies parties in conflict the opportunity of using their differences to clarify [improve] their relationship” (p. 22).

Watkins, M. (2002). Breakthrough business negotiation: A toolbox for managers. Somerset, NJ: John Wiley & Sons.

Weeks, D. (1992). The eight essential steps to conflict resolution. New York: Penguin Putnam.

    Class Struggle

[Associated with German social thinker Karl Marx (1818-1883)] Marx theorized that relations of economic productio n were at the center of any society. Classes are groups of individuals having the same relationship with the process of economic production in any given society, primarily producers (workers) and non-producers (owners). This relationship sets up conditions for exploitation of the worker class. While interests of the classes are in conflict, they are mutually dependent for survival in their present environment. Individuals who recognize their class position can develop a class culture and act in unison with others to reach political and social objectives.

    Compromise

Compromise is defined by Oxford University Press as, “an intermediate state between conflicting opinions, actions, etc., reached by mutu al concession or modification.” Bargaining can be used to adjust opposing principles or systems in which each side gives up something in return for something offered by the other side. In this way a settlement can be reached which generally lies somewhere in the middle in terms of quality, effect, desires, etc. By basing the compromise on underlying issues rather than positions, often value can be maximized to produce a win-win solution in which each party receives what they most want.

All government, indeed every human benefit and enjoyment, every virtue, and every prudent act, is founded on compromise and barter. Edmund Burke (1729 - 1797), Br itish politician

    Conflict Functionalism

Social conflict means a struggle over values and claims to scarce status, power and resources in which the aims of the opponents are to neutralize, injure or eliminate their rivals. The functions are with those consequences of social conflict that make for an increase rather than a decrease in the adaptation or adjustment of particular social relationships or groups. It also contributes to the maintenance of group boundaries and prevents the withdrawal of members from a group.

Coser, L. A. (1965). The functions of social conflict. New York: The Free Press.

    Conflict Resolution

Conflict resolution in simplistic terms means to find the answ er to a problem. Desired outcomes may determine the method used pursuant to conclude who is more powerful, to determine who is right, or to reconcile underlying interests.

Traditional resolution methods have promoted a win-lose perspective through utilizing fight, force, or coercion. Flight or avoidance have been used as alter natives to force; including accommodation, compromise, decision-making by an outside party, chance, and counseling.

New conflict resolution methods employ collaboration and interest-based bargaining. The parties act as partners working together against the problem in order to create a win-win solution.

Kovach, K. K. (2000). Mediation: Principles and practice (2nd ed.). St. Paul, MN: West Group.

    Conf lict Underground

Another term might be internal conflict. Internal conflicts which concern goals, values or interests that do not contradict the basic assumptions upon which the relationship is founded tend to be positively functional for the social structure. Such conflicts tend to make possible the r eadjustment of norms and power relations within groups in accordance with the felt needs of its individual members or subgroups. A flexible society benefits from conflict because such behavior either internal (underground) or external (above ground), by helping to create and modify norms, assures its continuance under changed conditions - the potential for balancing the power.

Coser, L. A. (1965). The functions of social conflict. New York: The Free Press.

    Convergent

Convergent (thinking) that narrows all options to one solution, was coined by J. P. Guilford, psychologist known for his creativity research. In the midst of confusion, chaos, and especially conflict, the idea is to come to an understanding or to converge on a single solution or idea ("Convergent Thinking," 2001).

An example is a family trying to decide what to do for "family night," all with different ideas. Finally, after much conflict, they converge, deciding on one thing that all the family members will be able to participate in and hopefully enjoy.

    Dialectical Materialism

[Associated with German social thi nker Karl Marx (1818-1883)] This represents a Marxist worldview that adheres to a material basis of reality (vs. idealism) which is continually changing through a struggle of opposites. Because society contains elements that are in opposition, self-movement automatically occurs through thesis, antithesis, and synthesis. Therefor e, according to this doctrine, the conflict of opposing forces in society leads to growth, change, and development.

    Divergent

Divergent (thinking) has characteristics including fluency (ability to rapidly produce a large number of ideas or solutions to a problem); flexibility (capacity to consider a variety of approaches to a problem simultaneously); originality (tendency to produce ideas different from those of most other people); and elaboration (ability to think through the details of an idea and carry it out) ("Divergent Thinking," 2001).

For instance, in family mediation, the resolutions can be limitless with divergent thinking and constricting with thinking that "stays inside the box" like what might be decided by a judge/jury.

    Dysfunctional Family

Some dysfunctional families will be plagued by minor generalized problems throughout their existence; others will have trouble at specific periods. Most episodic family difficulties may be related to: (1) inability to cope adequately with the tasks of the current family phase; (2) need to move on to a new family phase; (3) stress of traumatic, excessive, and out-of-order even ts in the family developmental cycle; (4) all three problems (Glick, Clarkin, & Kessler, 1974).

Major family tasks are to provide for basic physical requirements, develop working marital/couple/family coalition, and rear offspring. In the dysfunctional family, these tasks are either not handled or handled differently/less adaptively than in health y families (Glick, 1974).

Glick, I. D., Clarkin, J. F., & Kessler, D. R. (1974). Marital and family therapy (3rd ed.). Orlando, FL: Grune & Stratton.

    Economic Determinism

[Associated with German social thinker Karl Marx (1818-1883)].     Escalation

Escalation is a process by which conflict intensifies between parties in disagreement. A provocative action promotes a perception of insult in the second party, who then responds disproportionately to the in itial action. The first party, in turn responds more intensely to the second party’s response, which promotes further amplification of the conflict.

Rubin, Pruitt, and Kim (1994) use a conflict-spiral model of escalation to predict behavior patterns of contending parties. According to the model, conflict predictably changes fr om light to heavy tactics, from specific to general issues, from modest to large commitments of resources, and from few to many participants.

Rubin, J. Z., Pruitt, D. G., & Kim, S. H. (1994). Social conflict: Escalation, stalemate, and settlement. New York: McGraw Hill.

    Functional Interdependence

Population tends to be knit together through an interwoven set of symbiotic and commensal relationships. Symbiotic relationships are those formed on the basis of their complementary differences. This is functional interdependence. Commensal relationships are those formed on the basis of supplemental similarities—such as ethnicity. Such solidarity is also a form of interdependence.

    Negotiation

Negotiation is an exchange between two parties who both want something. Often it is a process of bargaining among contentious parties in order to end disputes. Mutual benefit exchange proposes negotiators first determine their own goals and also those of the other party. They then design a plan of action to achieve their outcome, rearrange their strategies in accordance with the other person’s goals, and clearly communicate that their interest is in each party attaining their needs. “The end result is negotiators who will not only walk away with the win, but leave their partners winning, too” (Caliper Human Resources, p. 4).

    Partisan Perceptions

Partisan Perceptions is used by Michael Watkins (2002) to describe the exaggeration of differences between the parties. “When conflicts become embittered, the parties begin to gather and interpret information about each other in profoundly biased ways. Their perceptions get distorted in three ways. First, partisans assume that they themselves see things objectively, whereas their opponents have extreme and distorted views. Second, partisans tend to misjudge the other side’s motivations, underestimating the situa tional pressure their counterparts face. Third, … partisans consistently overestimate the distance between themselves and the other side.” (pp. 170-171).

Watkins, M. (2002). Breakthrough business negotiation: A toolbox for managers (pp. 159-188). Somerset, NJ: John Wiley & Sons.

    Power

Power is the capability to control people’s behavior with or without their consent. It determines who gets what and how much. The ways in which power is distributed is a critical element of social relationships. Sociological concepts of authority, legitimacy, coercion, and manipulation deal with power distribution and use. Authority is the form of power recognized as rightfully possessed. Legitimacy refers to t he generally held belief that a social institution (and its power) is valid and justified. Coercion speaks to use of force (or threat) to obtain a goal. Manipulation is secretive exercise of power unknown to those influenced.

Kloby, J. (1997). Inequality, power, and development: The t ask of political sociology. Boston: E. J. Brill Academic Publishers.

    Sociations

[Associated with Georg Simmel (1858-1918)] Sociation transforms an aggregation of isolated individuals into social forms through reciprocal interactions. These social forms are the foundation blocks of society and involve the dualism of both harmony and con flict. However varied the interests and purposes that give rise to specific associations, the social forms of interaction in which they are expressed may be identical. For example, both war and commerce involve cooperation. Conversely, identical interests and purposes may come together in different forms. For instance, aggressive intents may be fulfilled in various forms of conflict ranging from gang warfare to courtroom battles.

Levine, D. N. (Ed.). (1985). Georg Simmel on individuality and social forms: Selected writings. Chicago: University of Chicago Press.

    Structure of Dominance

Pratto and Sidanius ba se their theory on the observation that all human societies with surplus wealth are group-based social hierarchies, in which there is a dominant group at the top and one or more subordinate groups at the bottom. The dominant group is characterized by possession and control over a disproportionately large share of the material and symbolic goods people desire.
