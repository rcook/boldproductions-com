One of the major concepts in the ecology theory. This process that appears in solving problems, achieving goals, or participating in everyday activities involves the following steps:

  • recognition of the need for the decision;

    Total Human Ecosystem

This concept, which has both theoretical and practical implications for landscape ecology, has been formalized in terms of a hierarchical systems approach that is rooted in general systems theory and its recent insights into organized complexity. These insights include information theory and cybernetics, catastrophe theory and the new synthetic theories on coevolution, and new holistic and transdisciplinary insights into the dynamic self-organization and coevolution in nature and human societies. According to the conventional conception of ecological systems, natural ecosystems are the highest organi zational level of the ecological hierarchy—above organisms, populations, and communities. This conception reflects the still-dominant perception of a hierarchical order of nature, in which humans are merely external disturbance factors on ecosystems, creating only their own social hierarchies.

Naveh, Z. (2000). The total human ecosystem: Integrating ecology and economics. BioScience, 50(4), 357-61.

    Sustainable Development

Sustainable development requires that economic, ecological, and social goals be pursued with equal vigor. Economic g rowth must conserve the natural basis of life and help to even out large differences in standards of living at the same time. If the conservation of resources hinders economic growth in such a way that basic social needs are not met, then it too does not lead to the goal of sustainable development.

Roberts, M. (1996). What is sustainable development? Chemical Week, 158, 30-31.

    Carrying Capacity

A concept originally developed by ecologists with reference to the number of animals of a given species that can be supported without injury to the h abitat. When referring to humans, however, considering only density is not enough as individuals can differ greatly in the intensity of their impact on the environment. An American citizen, for example, may consume 100 or more times as much energy and resources as a citizen of a developing country. In other words, affluence reduces the number of people that can be supported by a given resource base. Thus, ecologists have to be concerned not only with density but also with per capita demands.

Barrett, G. W., & Odum, E. P. (2002). The twenty-first century: The world at carrying capacity. BioScience, 50(4), 363-8.

    Nature Evolving

A set of beliefs that is inherently "evolutionary and adaptive" and that draws insights and examples from a wide range of environmental and social systems. Preceding this concept are four alternative beliefs about causality, stability, and change that address problems of environmental management and sustainable development. Nature Cornucopian is a belief in exponential growth where resources are never scarce as hu mans always invent replacements or substitutes. Nature Anarchic states the world is inevitably followed by decrease and is fundamentally unstable. Nature Balanced views the world growing logistically seeking better ways to navigate upcoming transitions and maintain a sustained plateau. Nature Resilient believes in a world of cycles in which events and processes are discontinuous but always seek reorganization for renewal.

Holling, C. S. (1996). A theory of surprise. Environment, 38, 8.

    Family Ecology

"Family Ecology is a prismatic label tha t emphasizes and is represented by four major tenets:

(1) A philosophical orientation that follows closely the framework of three levels of world views as proposed by Altman and Rogoff (1987): the interactional; the organismic; and the transactional world views.

(2) A methodological pluralism for investigating fami ly phenomena with both qualitative and quantitative methods that capture the dynamic interactions of families and contexts.

(3) The promotion and synthesis of an integrated curriculum on family phenomena based on knowledge and conceptual issues from complementary disciplines (i.e., psychology, sociology, biology, anthropology, consumer studies, history, ethics, architecture, design, geography, urban planning, law, and political science).

(4) The active promotion of policy and intervention programs based on the synthesis of the stated tenets mentioned above."

Wright, S., & Herrin, D. (1988). Family ecology: An approach to the interdisciplinary complexity of the study of famil y phenomena. Family Science Review, 1(4), 253-282.

    Human Ecology

"Human Ecology is the study of our m icro (i.e., physiological, domestic) and macro (i.e., cultural, environmental, global) homes specifically through the relationships among humans and our inner environments, other humans, and our environments. It is an eclectic discipline that includes the synthesis of knowledge from other disciplines. It reflects a holistic paradigm that acknowledges the interconnectedness and interaction of the micro and macro realms of individuals, communities and our environments. Its approach is holistic, equalitarian, co-operative and one of shared power. It seeks to facilitate balance, integration, transformation, enlightenment and symbiosis among humans and our environments. One aspect of it seeks to increase "quality of life" by facilitating personal and global self-actualization or individuation (Sobon, 1993)."

    Nested Environments

Urie Bronfenbrenner (as cited in Boss, 1993, p. 423) described an individual's environmen t as "a set of nested structures each inside the next, like a set of Russian dolls." This resulted in ecological theory being widely represented by a diagram of the four levels of environments: micro, meso, exo, and macrosystems. 

  • Microsystem - environment closest to an individual. Individual has direct interaction here with family, friends, peers, workplace and so on.
  • Mesosystem - interactions of micro environments. One's experiences in one microsystem can effect another microsystem. 
  • Exosystem - an outer level that operates indirectly on the microsystem. Influence from another system not occupied by the individual. 
  • Macrosystem - oute rmost level, the broader culture in which the individual lives. Includes influences of customs, laws, social values and so on.

Boss, P. G., Doherty, W. J., LaRossa, R., Schumm, W., & Steinmetz, S. K. (Eds.). (1993). Sourcebook of family theories and methods: A contextual approach. New York: Plenum Pre ss.

    Bidirectional Relations

Lemer and Busch-Rossnagel (as cited in Wright & Herrin, 1988) define bi-directional relations as the reciprocal activity of an a ctive organism and the active context that results in changes to both. "Just as the context changes the individual, the individual changes the context. As such, by acting to change sources of their own development, by being a product and a producer of their contexts, individuals effect their own development."

Wright, S., & Herrin, D. (1988). Family ecology: An approach to the interdiscipl inary complexity of the study of family phenomena. Family Science Review, 1(4), 253-282.

    OIK - OEKOLOGIE -OIKONOMIKOS

The Greek root Oik means house or living place. This is the root word for both ecology and economics. The former proposed by Ernest Haeckel in 1873 as the science that studies the interrelationships between organisms of life and the environment. Ellen Richards and others attempted to define a new science named "home oekology" but the term economics was accepted instead. 

Boss, P. G., Doherty, W. J., LaRossa, R., Schumm, W., & Steinmetz, S. K. (Eds.). (1993). Sourcebook of family theories and methods: A contextual approach. New York: Plenum Press.

    Home Economics

The American Home Economics Association proposed the following definition of Home Economics in 1902. It implies an ecological perspective that is concerned with studying the relationships between individuals and their environments. 

"Home Economics in its most comprehensive sense is the study of the laws, conditions, principl es and ideals which are concerned on the one hand with man's immediate physical environment and on the other hand with his nature as a social being and is the study specially of the relation between these two factors" (Boss, 1993, p. 420).

Boss, P. G., Doherty, W. J., LaRossa, R., Schumm, W., & Steinmetz, S. K. (Eds.). (1993). Sourcebook of family theorie s and methods: A contextual approach. New York: Plenum Press.
    Adaptation

The process of the family interaction with itself an d its environment. This process involves change in environment and structure. This process leads to better integration of the system.
    Human Betterment

First used by Kenneth Boulding, who calls it the goal of an individual. He further identifies four virtues that are needed to reach human betterment. These virtues are: economic adequacy, justice, freedom, and peacefulness.
    Interdependence

This concept indicates that one part of the system supports another and cannot be understood separately from one another. For example child cannot be understood without looking at his/her family. In other words, description of one family member does not equal the description of the whole family.
    Family Ecosystem

Consists of a family system and its interaction with its environment. This surrounding environment can be broken in: human-built, social-cultural, and natural physical-biological environments.
    Household

People who share the same living space. It is important to make a distinction between household and family. People may live in the same household but be not a family.
