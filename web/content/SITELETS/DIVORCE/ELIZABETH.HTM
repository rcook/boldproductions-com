  Mediation and Domestic Violence
    Mediation in divorce disputes started in the 1970's to minimize the negative effects associated with both the adversarial nature of litigation and an increasing caseload (Guinta, & Amatea, 2001). The state of Texas has made mediation mandatory in order for a divorce to be granted. This raises many questions about the use of mediation to settle divorce cases in marriages that have experienced domestic violence (i.e. battering). 

In recent studies, outcome evaluations have put the results of successful mediated agreements in divorcing couples from 50 to 80% of the cases to 40 to 70% depending on the study while also reporting that as many as 60% of the couples were inappropriate for mediation even when chosen by the divorcing couple (Cohen, & Luxenberg, 1999). These researchers identified seven typologies of couple dynamics and found that only two out of the seven are actually suited to mediation with the other five types showing less and less benefit with increasing risk of violence. Imbrogno and Imbrogno (2000) state that the use of mediation in family violence cases reflect and reinforce the stereotypic and discriminatory position that domestic violence is a problem of a private, not a public sector. Such values , in fact, divert these types of cases away from adjudication moving this social situation out of the public attention and scrutiny and thereby stifling development of law that might address domestic violence. 

Presser & Gaarder (2000) state that while mediation is compatible with the feminist value of empowerment this "supposed" empowerment is actually lost with the ideology of mediation where persons participate in t he creation of their own problems which reflects a victim-precipitation theories that blame women for their abuse and center the conflict between blaming women and recognizing them as active subjects.

Another selling point of mediation to the public has been its claim to be more cost-effective. However, Imbrogno & Imbrogno (2000) claim that the money saved from choosing to settle in mediation is deceiving when compared with long-term financial results of adjudication for women. Ellis (2000), however, finds that divorce mediation is safer and fairer than adjudication for separating women due in part to the increasing likelihood of violence related to the "giving" of sworn affidavits that the rules of civil procedure mandate. Current research about the risk of violence include the finding that the threat of postseparation male partner violence toward separated female partners decreases the risk of nonlethal violence but increase s the risk of homicide and that participation in mediation further lowers the risk of nonlethal violence (Ellis). However, clinically depression, extremely jealousy, possessiveness, and dependency in a male partner significantly increase the risk of lethal violence and great care must be given to protect the female (Ellis).

All in all, court-mandated mediation may not be a forward step in divorce settlement and may in fact be a backward step especially in the presence of physical and/or emotional battering.
