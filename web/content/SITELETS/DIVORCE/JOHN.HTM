  Is There Alimony in Texas?
    Is there alimony in Texas? The answer is a qualified yes: Texas now has a v ery limited form of "Spousal Maintenance." In order to adequately answer this question about alimony, it is important to first explain the nature of marital property, both in Texas and in other states. 

Texas is one of only seven "Community Property" states in the nation. In Texas, at the moment of marriage three "estates" are created: (1) the "separate property estate" of the husband, (2) the "separate property estate" of the wife, and the "community property estate" of the marriage. Property acquired before marriage is considered the separate property of the spouse who acquired the property before marriage. However, Texas has a "Community Property Presumption," which means that all property acquire d during marriage, and even everything on hand at the time of divorce, is presumed to be community property. This means that upon divorce, all property acquired during marriage is presumed to belong to the community estate, even if the deed or title to the property is placed in the name of only one of the parties. 

The burden of proof is on the spous e seeking to prove that certain property is, in deed, separate property of a spouse, and certain rules have evolved to establish that a particular piece of property is his or her separate property. But because the Texas courts are given great latitude to divide the community estate in a manner that is fair and equitable to both spouses, Texas does not have alimony as it is known in ot her states. In fact, the term alimony is used only twice in the Texas Family Code, and only in reference to the resources available to a spouse for the purposes of paying child support. 

By contrast, the great majority of states in America are "Separate Property" states. That means that upon divorce, property acquired during marriage is considered the separate property of the spouse in whose name the property was acquired. In the traditional sense, alimony is the payment one spouse makes to the other spouse after divorce to compensate the receiving spouse for the value of community advantage or funds that benefited the other spouse's separate estate. However, the Texas scheme for dividing community property is not always just and fair to a spouse who has devoted her energies to raising children while the other spouse advanced his career. In 1995, the Texas legislature passed "Spousal Maintenance" legislation that offers a limited form of alimony designed to remedy this problem. The legislation was further modified, effective September 1, 2001. The spousal maintenance laws are foun d in Chapter Eight of the Texas Family Code, and they do not discriminate according to the gender of the parties. 

Under the Texas statutory scheme, there is no entitlement to spousal maintenance, but a trial court has discretion to award spousal maintenance only when the party seeking maintenance meets specific eligibility requirements. First, spousa l maintenance can be ordered if the spouse from whom maintenance is requested was found to have committed an act of family violence within two years of the start of a divorce or while the suit is pending, or the parties must have been married for at least ten years. When a divorce is sought in a marriage lasting ten years or more, a party is eligible to seek spousal maintenance only i f the party lacks sufficient property to meet minimum reasonable needs and the party cannot support himself or herself due to: (1) an incapacitating physical or mental disability, (2) a child in the home with a disability requiring substantial care and supervision, or (3) the lack of adequate earning ability. 

Absent the agree ment of the parties, a court may normally only order spousal maintenance for up to three years. There is also a presumption that maintenance is not warranted unless the spouse seeking payment has been diligent in seeking suitable employment or is developing skills necessary to become self-supporting. This presumption does not apply to a spouse who is unable to seek employment due to a n incapacitating physical or mental disability. Moreover, the court may order spousal maintenance for a period longer than three years if the recipient spouse has an incapacitating physical or mental disability. When a disability is found, the court may order periodic review of the maintenance order to determine whether the disability is continuing. Furthermore, when spousal maintenance is ordered by the court over a party's objection, either party may move to modify the amount of continued spousal maintenance if there is a material change in circumstances of either party affected by the maintenance order. 

Although the Texas spousal maintenance statute is very limited, the parties to a divorce may agree t o an alimony award that exceeds the statutory provisions. In such cases, the parties may elect to bind themselves contractually to continued alimony payments for an extended period. This most often happens in cases where the community property is not easy to divide, and one party waives his or her interest in property in exchange for a stream of income. 

Whether court-ordered or contractual, "spousal maintenance" in Texas may be treated as alimony for income tax purposes as long as: 1) the payments are made in cash, 2) the payee is a spouse or former spouse of the payor, 3) the payments are made under a divorce or separation order, 4) the payor and payee are not members of the same household, and 5) the pa yments will cease upon the death of the payee. When these requirements are met, the party paying alimony is allowed to deduct the alimony payments from his or her income, and the party receiving alimony is required to include the alimony payments as income on his or her tax return.
