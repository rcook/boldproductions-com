  Divorce: Family Economics
    Stressful economic conditions that result from divided family income may occur as a consequence of divorce. Divorce creates a large number of changes within the family that may undermine the ability to cope because of the condensed time period in which they occur (Wang & Amato, 2000). 

Wang and Amato (2000) found that divorced men experienced fewer economic hardships than women. Lorenz and Simons (1997) suggested that divorce may act as primary stressor for women, leaving them susceptible to a collection of stressful life events, including economic hardship. The 2000 U.S. Census reported that almost 19% of females over the age of 15 where either married with an absent spouse, separated, or divorced, compared to barely 15% of the males (U.S. Census Bureau, 2001). Single mothe rs increased by 7 million between 1970 and 2000. Single fathers also increased, but only by 1.6 million (U.S. Census Bureau, 2000). 

Income has been positively associated with divorce adjustment and appraisals of life satisfaction. However, declines in living standards have not proven to be a predictor of divorce adjustment, except for the unemployed (Wang & Amato, 2000). Amato (2000) found downward economic mobility and economic consequences to be more problematic for women than for men. As a result of more interrupted work histories, work-family conflict, and employment and wage discrimination, women were in more danger of an economic crisis (Amato, 2000). To compound the precarious situation that divorced women find themselves in economically, children in the household worsened a woman's economic prospects, by hindering the ability to hold gainful employment or remarry (Wang & Amato, 2000). 

More than one million children experience divorce each year in the United States (Amato, 2000). Although children in the household worsen a woman's economic prospects, children were also associated with positive life appraisals. Furthermore, although financially difficult to care for, children may be a source of continued satisfaction for the parent (Wang & Amato, 2000). According to the 1996 Census, 28% of children in the U.S. live in a mother-only household, compare d to 4% of children who live in a father-only household. More children lived with neither parent (5%) than lived with fathers (Coney & Mackey, 1998). 

In many divorced families, child support from the noncustodial parent is illusive or uncertain; even when present, most child support payments are below the cost of actually raising a child (Folk & Graham, 1992). Enforcement of child support payments has become a symptom of a deeper social pr oblem. Nonetheless, even if enforced, child support could not be expected to lift a female-headed household into middle class (Bogolub, 1994). The division of income between two households results in a reduction of benefit to the children (Furstenberg & Kiernan, 2001). Attainment of children from divorced single-mother families is substantially lower than attainment of children to single-mother families as a result of death of a spouse or children from a biological-two parent family (Biblarz & Gotta iner, 2000). Some scholars imply problems for children in divorce situations, while Amato found other scholars "…argue that adults find fulfillment, and children develop successfully, in a variety of family structures." (p. 1270). Coney and Mackey (1998) related a Danish proverb that states, " A rich child often sits in a poor mother's lap."

A growing body of literature questions whether research on divorce adequately studies family functioning over time . Predivorce information is often omitted from studies of potential divorce effects (Furstenberg & Kiernan, 2001). Marital dissolution is an uncoupling process (Amato, 2000), where a period of predivorce precedes the actual dissolution (Furstenberg & Kiernan, 2001). Furstenberg and Kiernan (2001) found that while there may be significant disadvantages to growing up in a single parent family there are also significant disadvantages and differences that predate the actual dissolution of marriage. Whil e significant economic hardship may face women and their custodial children, many economic problems were actually present before the divorce. Behaviors labeled as effects of divorce may be attributable to differences prior to parental separation and are often confused with the consequences (Furstenberg & Kiernan, 2001). Economic stress and economic hardship, although a component of divorce for many, may actually be contextual phenomena if divorce is viewed not as an end or a beginning, but as a process that lies somewhere in the middle of a life event continuum that has the potential to effect families in a variety of different ways.
