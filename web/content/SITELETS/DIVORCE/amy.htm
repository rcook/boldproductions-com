  Attachment Theory and its Relevance to Divorce
    Attachment theory is most notably associated with the infant-caregiver relationship (Todorski, 1995). Predicated upon the formation of an infant's first human relationship, how can attachment theory provide insight into the process of divorce that inversely poses the termination of a significant adu lt relationship? This paper will seek to address the salient aspects of attachment theory as they relate to divorce.

The first connection to be drawn between attachment theory and divorce is the common issue of loss and separation. Attachment theory was originally formulated in order to understand animal and human reactions to two major life stressors: loss and separation (Mikulincer & Florian, 1998). Specific evidence is accounted with an entir e volume from Bowlby's trilogy "Attachment and Loss," which was devoted to this topic (Bakermans-Kranenburg & van IJjzendoorn, 1997).

The theme of loss is pervasive among divorce literature and many divorce researchers have sought alliance with theories and models pertaining to the stages of grief (Bakermans-Kranenburg & van IJjzendoorn, 1997). Other than death, Bakermans-Kranenburg and van IJjzendoorn (1997) regarded divorce as the most rad ical form of relationship dissolution and spoke at length of the parallels between the experiences of loss through divorce and those through death. Walsh, Jacob, and Simons (1995) addressed the multiple layers of loss that can compound the difficulty and adjustment to divorce including the loss of the marriage, the loss of family, and the loss of friendship networks. Kitson (1982) gave credence to the application of attachment theory to divorce with the statement, "Attachment theory provides the fullest exp lanation for the anomalous situation in divorce in which an individual is apparently grieving over the loss of someone of whom he or she is often simultaneously glad to be rid" (p. 379).

The construct of separation and the consequential distress was of profound interest to Bowlby (1973). Mikulincer and Florian (1998) noted that the original empirical test of attachment theory, the Strange Situation study, assesses the quality of an emotional bond by the response of a child to two types of distress: maternal separation and the presence of a stranger. Based upon the observed reactions in a series of eight episodes of separation and reunion, three attachment patterns emerged - secure, avoidant, and anxious-ambivalent (Berk, 1998). These styles have been empirically tested and found to remain prevalent throughout the life course (Klohnen & Bera, 1998; Waters, Merrick, Treboux, Crowell, & Albersheim, 2000). Bowlby (1979) succinctly summarized the e nduring influence of the early attachment style with the comment that attachment behaviors "characterize human beings from the cradle to the grave" (p. 129). Other studies have quantified the enduring power of the attachment style and its ascendancy in adulthood (Collins, 1996; Kirkpatrick & Davis, 1994; Kobak & Hazan, 1991). 

The second connection between attachment theory and divorce is an individual's response to stress. Amato (2000) depicted divorce as a process that catalytically sets into motion numerous events that people experience as stressful. Attachment theory highlights individual differences in the way people regulate inner distress and relate to others (Mikulincer & Orback, 1995). Synthesizing the research in attachment literature, there seems to be general differences between the individual attachment styles and their habitual responses to stress (Mikulincer & Florian, 1995; 1998; Mikulincer, Florian, & Weller, 1993). Across a gamut of life events, Mikulincer and Florian (1998) concluded that adult attachment styles seem to influence how well individuals adapt to stress in their daily lives. The wide range of life events included issues related to life and death, physical and emotional threats, and personal and interpersonal losses. Cohen, Finzi, and Avi-Yonah (1999) found individuals with different attachment patterns experience the separation of divorce differently, have different coping strategies, and deal dif ferently with their ex-spouses in the tasks of parenting after the divorce.

The third association between attachment theory and divorce was made in the late 1970s when writers on divorce saw the connection between attachment theory and divorce and adopted the metaphor between the infant's attachment to its mother and the relationship between estranged or ex-spouses (Cohen, Finzi, & Avi-Yonah, 1999). Strong, DeVault, Sayad, and Yarber (2001) note d the core elements of love appear to be the same for children as they are for adults: the need to feel emotionally safe and secure. Finzi, Cohen, and Ram (2000) suggested that attachment theory can provide insight into the dynamics of the divorce transition. Individuals with different patterns of attachment will experience and deal differently with the divorce crisis and the interaction of the attachment patterns of the two spouses will produce distinctive divorce dynamics (Finzi, Cohen, & Ram, 2000). An example of this is supported by the research performed by Ceglian and Gardner (1999) whose findings indicated that multiple marriers are more likely to be avoidantly attached.

From the perspective of professional application, attachment theory may uncover important psychological underpinnings of the divorce dynamics (Cohen, Finzi, & Avi-Yonah, 1999). Given the complexity of human nature and the conflictive attributes of divorce, understanding what drives a couple's divorce behavior could be useful for both therapeutic and legal agents who are called upon to intervene in the process of divorce (Finzi, Cohen, & Ram, 2000). Cohen, Finzi, and Avi-Yonah (1999) summarized the applicability of attachment theory to divorce with the statement, "While it is recognized that attachment patterns are only one of the many possible aspects of personality that affect behavior, attachment theory does seem to be particularly applicable to understanding respon ses to separation" (p. 185).
