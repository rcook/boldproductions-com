  Divorce: Children Can Be Better Off
    The divorce rate today in the United States is generally known as fifty percent. Numerous studies have focused their attention on the effects of parental divorce and separation on children (Furstenberg & Teitler, 1994; Gately & Schwebel, 1992; Hazelton, Lancee, & O'Neil, 1998; Hetherington & Stanel y-Hagan, 1999). Most of the results indicate negative outcomes in short-term and/or long-term effects for children in divorced families. However, is this really the case? Many of the investigations are from clinical samples and, therefore, the results can be misleading (Gately & Schwebel, 1992; Hetherington & Stanely-Hagan, 1999). Research findings have shown that when non-clinical samples are included in the divorce-adjustment studies, positive effects increase. 
< br> When parental conflict, family violence, or neglect occurs in a two-parent, or so-called "intact" family, children can feel caught in the middle, develop anti-social behavior, become depressed, or experience emotional disturbance. Kot and Shoemaker (1999) and Linker, Stolberg, and Green (1999) reported that regardless of parent marital status, children with higher interparent hostility display more behavioral problems and poorer emotional adjustment than children with lower leve l of hostility. Also, Amato and Keith (1991) conducted a meta-analysis of eight studies comparing high-conflict intact families, low-conflict intact families, and divorced families. The finding showed the level of parental conflict had more effect on child adjustment than marital status.

Thus, at least some children adjust better if the conflict ends with divorce rather than being extended into the coparental relationship. Divorce can be an escape from the distress and help resolve extreme parental conflict or family dysfunction. Marital conflict usually declines after divorce when conflict is the primary cause of marital distress. Children who move from a conflicting, abusive, or neglecting family situation to a more harmonious, less stressful one show diminished problems following divorce (Amato, 2000; Amato, Loomis, & Booth, 1995). 

From the review of various studies in the divorc e-adjustment literature, Gately and Schwebel (1992) identified four areas of favorable outcomes of children in divorce families: "maturity, self-esteem, empathy, and androgyny" (p. 66). In the single parent family, the parent-child relationship is easily shifted to be a partnership. Children are expected to share more family responsibilities such as chores and childcare, and also to participate in family decision-making. Such involvement enhances maturity when children become more independent, responsible, and aware of the possibility of adult caretakers' being vulnerable.

Children in divorced families may develop higher self-esteem through the experience of effective coping with changed familial situation, being in charge of new responsibilities, accomplishing new duties, and so on (Gately & Schwebel, 1992). Amato and Keith (1991) reported a finding from several studies that a number of adolescents in divorced families, after being asked to share more household responsibilities, showed higher levels of self-esteem and a sense of responsibility than their counterparts in intact families.

Because they go through a stressful process, learn to cope with parental pressure and changed family circumstance, and witness their parents' strengths and weaknesses, children in divorced families may develop a better sense of empathy and sensitivity. Gately and Schwebel (1992) suggested that these children are more emotionally supportive and are willing to show concern for the well being of family members and people outside the family. They also exhibit an increased level of appreciation and understanding of others' feelings and role-taking skills.

Children usually live with one parent after the parental divorce, which means not only one parent is absent from the household but also the custodial parent is taking over the other parental role. Observin g two individual gender-role models become one helps children shift away from their stereotypes of gender-role perspective and behavior and toward androgyny (Gately & Schwebel, 1992). In addition, children may form nontraditional attitudes and thinking through parental encouragement or engaging in nontraditional activities and family responsibilities.

Can children be better off after parental divorce? The answer is positive. Finally, from a divo rced child's voice,

"I believe that the whole experience changed me as a person and I feel certain that if my parents had stayed together and as a result nothing in my life had significantly changed then I would not know or understand much of what I know now. As Henry Thoreau said 'Things do not change, we change', and you must remember that not all change is bad" (Divorce Online Limited, 2000).
