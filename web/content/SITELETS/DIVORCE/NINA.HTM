  Talking to Children about Divorce
    "In the United States, one in every two marriages ends in divorce, and two-thirds of these families include minor children. As a result, every year approximately one million new kids become children of divorce" (Neuman, 1998). How do parents tell their children that they are getting a divorce? What has been effective? What has research found? 

One thing research has found is that the most important factors that shape long term adjustments are (1) the amount of parental adjustment children are exposed to and (2) the quality of parenting or childrearing competence they receive. How the children deal with the pain and anxiety of a divorce will depend as much upon the parent's view of the event as it will on the actual words parents use to talk with their c hildren. The undercurrent of the parent's message, communicated by nonverbal and verbal cues, will speak volumes about what the divorce means and what the future may hold for them (Schneider & Zuckerberg, 1996).

Also, research has found that almost all children are very upset by the initial breakup (Teyber, 2001). However, the impact varies depending upon the gender and developmental level of the child. Children will feel the need to revisit the is sues surrounding the divorce as they grow older and enter different phases of their own lives. Their perspective will change with age and their developmental level (Schneider & Zuckerberg, 1996, p. 23). 
Talking to children prior to the divorce can help children begin to deal with some of their mixed emotions. This can help eliminate some of the confusion from children and allow them to cope more effectively. In the Sandcastles Survey, Gary Neuman (1998), randomly se lected over one thousand workbooks from children ages eight to seventeen. These workbooks contained opinions, thoughts, and emotions about their parents' divorce. One major pattern he found was that many children were not told about the divorce until it actually happened. Further, over 20 percent of the children ages eleven through seventeen were never "told" and they just figured it out. 

Lane Hartill (2001) discussed the findings of a survey of 10,088 students during a two-year period. During that time, 8% had a divorce in their family. This study provided the strongest evidence yet that many of the problems observed among children with divorced parents begin prior to parental separation.

Gary Neuman (1998) and other health professionals discussed the importance of parents' communicating openly and honestly with their children once they have decided on a separation or divorce. When children a sk questions about the divorce, parents should give truthful answers. Of course, parents may need to add more information as the children grow older or their own understanding of what happened becomes clearer. Parents should avoid having to withdraw a statement as a lie. The erosion of trust and security caused by such behavior can be very harmful to children (Lewis & Sammons, 2001).

Children should be allowed to express their fears, express their wo rries, and express their hurt so that they have an outlet. They are likely harboring all kinds of feelings, and they're afraid to speak up because the environment around them is very tense (Hartill, 2001). It is also important for parents to acknowledge their own sadness and regrets about the divorce. Parents and children alike need to grieve the loss of the family as they have known it (Teyber, 2001, p.29). Finally, it is always important for both parents to communicate clearly and frequently that each of them loves their children very much.
