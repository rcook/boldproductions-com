  Prevent Divorce: Can We? Should We?
    "Researchers say that they can - and some states feel they should reduce the likelihood of divorce by altering the course of bad marriage in the making." 

— Russo, F. (1997, p. 28)

A few decades ago the divorce was rate was very high but it has been dropping since the early 1980s (Goldstein, 1999). Still, divorce affects more than two million adults and one million children yearly (Norton & Miller, 1992). In 1998, the annual divorce rate was 4.2 per 1000 population, resulting in 1,135,000 divorces (National Center for Health Statistics, 2001). Can we prevent the majority of these cases? Alternatively, should we prevent them at all (Clulow, 1996)? The government can do this by a ltering the course of bad marriages in the making by measuring the strengths and weaknesses of the relationship (Russo, 1997). Many of the reasons women initiate divorce depend on the divorce laws. Where there is a presumption of shared custody with the husband, the percentage of women who initiate divorces is much lower (Brinig & Allen, 2000). Strong marital bonds can prevent divorce (Mintle, 2001). Researchers at the University of Washington are trying to answer the complex question and are making hea dway. Couples who have a child together have a slightly decreased risk of divorce compared to couples without children, but the decreased risk is far less than it used to be when parents with marital problems were more likely to stay together "for the sake of the children" (Heaton, 1990; Waite & Lillard, 1991). 

Stone (1998) lists few proven ways to prevent divorce. Research from the University of Maryland and the National Center for Health Stat istics finds that engagement in premarital sex is connected with a " considerable high risk" of divorce. Practicing abstinence would actually help in multiple other ways, too. The social science evidence shows couples who cohabitate prior to marriage have significantly higher rates (more than 46%) of divorce and the act of cohabitation itself generates attitudes in people that are more conducive to divorce (DeMaris & Rao, 1992). Stone also believes that avoiding such trial marriages would be a first way to reduce divorce dramatically. 

Separation does not solve the problem totally. Following divorce, the children involved are better off in stepfamilies than in single parent families (Booth & Dunn, 1994; Cherlin, 1992; McLanahan & Sandefur, 1994). When parents don't get along, children are at an advantage when their parents divorce than if they stay together (Andrew, Cherlin, Lindsay & McRae, 1998). Amato and Booth (1997) suggest contra dictory findings. Marital unhappiness and discord have negative impact; so does the fact of going through a divorce. It was only the children in very high conflict homes who benefited from the conflict removal that divorce may bring. Mismanaged conflict predicts both marital stressors and negative effects for children (Goldberg, 1993; Gottman, 1994). Research has shown that conflict can be managed, however. For example, training through the Connections curriculum improved participants' conflict resolution s kills. Also, graduates of the curriculum became less likely to see divorce as a good option for troubled marriages, and were more likely to take advantage of pre-marital and post-marital programs to build better marriages (Gardner, 2001). It implies either of the choices, to separate or to continue to live in disagreement, is a challenge for the family. 

With the costs of divorce now estimated to be in the billions annually, a modest investment in d ivorce reduction makes sense to many. The U. S. military is strongly encouraging soon-to-be married enlistees to attend premarital counseling and also is urging pastors to use systematic premarital education for all engaged couples. In Florida, marriage law went into effect which, in part, offers a reduction in marriage license fees for those who take a marriage preparation course or counseling (Marriage Preparation and Preservation Act, 1998). In Maryland and Michigan, proposed laws prescribe a delay in gr anting marriage licenses to couples until they attend marriage skills classes. Alaska and Kansas are considering reducing the fee for a license as an inducement to take such classes. A bill in Missouri for an outright mandate failed in committee in early 1997. Clulow (1996) suggests a client-centered approach in addressing marital problems. Unless people can be encouraged to access information about support services earlier, when marriages first get into difficulty, the focus on saving marriages will be rel evant to relatively very few. Clulow (2000) reflects that the thrust of the Family Law Act 1996 is to prevent marital breakdown and also differentiate between 'good' and 'bad' divorces. Peterson (1996) believed that preventing unhappy, destructive marriages is much cheaper - in dollars and in human misery than attempts to clean up the toxic waste that follows them. 

McManus (1997) described programs and organizations in the United States designed to prevent divorce and helped couples to stay together. Such programs include community marriage policy (as seen in Modesto, California), marriage preparation in the Catholic church, mentoring from older couples, marriage encounter, marriage ministry, step-family support groups, etc. Olsen, Fournier, and Druckman (1989) developed the program called PREPARE as a counseling tool for engaged couples, and later, ENRICH for married couples. Lori Garden developed the program, PAIRS, while Sherod Miller developed Co uple Communication Program (Coalition for Marriage, Family and Couples Education, 2000). 

Gardner and Howlett (2001) emphasized the need for primary prevention focused at the couple level and highlighted a variety of new high school marriage curricula. Pre-marital education programs improve relationship adjustment, problem-solving skills, decrease disagreements, and negative emotions, and thus promote marital quality and stability (Markman & Hah lweg, 1993; Markman, Renick, Floyd, Stanley & Clements, 1993; Renick, Blumberg & Markman, 1992). Such programs may cut the divorce rate by half (Markman, Floyd, Stanley & Storaasli, 1988; Markman, et al., 1993) and lead to happier and better functioning children (Markman, et al., 1988). Some of the programs in the high school and middle school curricula currently are called "The Art of Loving Well," "Building Relationships: Skills for a Lifetime," "Connections: Relationships and Marriage," "Soci al Emotional Intelligence," "Pairs for Peers," and " Partners." Sayers, Kohn, and Heavey (1998) reviewed a number of marriage preparation programs and found that skill-based programs can prevent marital dysfunctions. 

Alarmed by the societal costs of marital distress and divorce, policy makers in many states (Alabama, Alaska, Georgia, Florida, Illinois, Indiana, Louisiana, Michigan, Texas, Utah, and Wisconsin) and countries (Australia, England, Norw ay and United States) have recently presented legislation geared toward decreasing the divorce rate and its many negative outcomes. As research results would suggest, policy makers would be wise to focus efforts at the primary prevention level as opposed to focusing solely on creating barriers to divorce.
