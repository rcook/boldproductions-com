  Can Religion Help?
An Exploration of the Relationships Between Religious Beliefs and the Adaptability of Divorced People
    Carl Jung believed that humans have a desire to feel connected to something larger than themselves (as cited in Clift, 1982, p. 83). Divorce can be a time of feeling disconnected, as well as a time of healing and reconnecting. For many humans, their religion, spirituality, and/or relationship with God are the source of support that helps them heal and grow after a divorce. Kudlac (1991) contended, "Religion and religious beliefs are, for many people, a primary response to life, a reason for and focus of existence, a means of making sense of the world" (p. 277). A recent poll found that ninety-five percent of Americans believe in God and approximately sixty-five percent report that religion answers problems (Gallup, 2001, paragraph 12). Furthermore, Haug (1988) contended, "Opinion polls show that in spite of our society's progressive secularization during the 20th century, religions and spirituality continue to play a large part in people's lives" (p. 18).

Religion often plays a major role in problem solving (Pargament, Kennell, Hathaway , Grevengoed, Newman, & Jones, 1988). A topic that is not adequately addressed in current research is whether religion or spirituality contributes support to divorced people going through a life change (Maton, 1989; Nathanson, 1995; & Sheridan, Bullis, Adcock, Berlin, & Miller, 1992). Nathanson (1995) contended that in her research she found very little literature on divorce and spirituality. She stated, "It appears that whenever spirituality or religion had been studied in tandem with trauma, t he crisis has generally been death or illness, not divorce" (Nathanson, 1995, p. 180).

In a study regarding the effects of divorce on women's spirituality, eighty-five percent of the women surveyed reported that their spirituality became stronger after divorce (Nathanson, 1995). The researcher concluded "divorce may be a turning point for spiritual change, the crisis pushing one toward integration at many different levels" (Nath anson, 1995, p. 187). 

In studying the link between religious participation and personal well-being, Ellison (1991) found that religion could augment a person's idea of well-being. Two ways religion can enhance the aspects of well-being are by providing support and social integration and by establishing a "personal relationship with a divine other" (Ellison, 1991, p. 80). 

Divorce can be a stressful time for many people. In this essay, there has been an attempt in establishing the possible influence religion, spirituality, and church has on men and women going through divorce. The limited research found on this topic suggests that religion does play a part in the lives of people dealing with divorce.

"Besides being supportive and accepting, what else can the church do to support the divorced population?" Here are a few examples of what some churches do. Some offer a church service for the divorcing couple, their families, and friends. The service can be a time when the couple can offer to God their pain and ask for God's healing. Such a service can provide a forum for healing. Spong (1984) called this "A Service for the Recogni tion of the End of a Marriage" (paragraph 1). He continued, "because the man and the woman remained committed Christians, the church which had been a central focus of their marriage somehow also had to be part of their separation" (Spong, 1984, paragraph 4). Some churches offer local support groups as well as church recognized support groups nationwide. 1 Websites are also a resource that can be beneficial in supporting divorced people. 2 

The relationship among divorce, religion, and spirituality is a topic that has not received much attention through research. There may be various reasons why there is a dearth of research, especially, in this area. Perhaps the twenty-first century will provide new research on the topic of spirituality, religion, and divorce.
