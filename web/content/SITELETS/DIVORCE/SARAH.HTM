  Effects of D ivorce on Women and Men
   

There is no doubt that the emotional, financial, and psychological effects of divor ce have a substantial impact on both women and men, and the family and friends with whom they have shared their lives. Although children may be most affected by divorce, primarily due to increased conflict, it may be helpful to understand, based on published research, the typical effects of divorce on women and men.

The consequences of divorce are economically harder on women than men. Statistically, women initiate divorce twice as often as men, 90% of divorced mothers have custody of their children, 60% of people under poverty guidelines are divorce d women and children, and 65% of divorced mothers receive no child support (O’Connell-Corcoran, n.d.). Women experiencing separation or divorce typically undergo marked declines in family income and in measures of economic well-being. In contrast, men’s family incomes decline more modestly, and men often experience increases in standard of living measures (Smock, Gupta, & Manning, 1999).

After divorce, women experience less stress and better adjustment than do men. In a ten-year follow-up of research participants, Wallerstein (1986) found that more women than men appeared to have notably improved the quality of t heir lives during the post-separation decade. In her study, women had acquired greater self-confidence and pride in their abilities to take care of themselves and their children.

Divorce leads to a wider range of psychological growth among women, but significantly less so among men. Bevvino (2000) found that women reported significantly more positive consequences of divorce than did men, typically in the areas of self-growth and a sense of independence. In her study, only men responded to the category of “another relationship” as a positive consequence of their divorce. Interestingly, men tended to remarry more quickly than women.

One of the best predictors of successful post-divorce adjustment is social support. Duran-Aydintug (1998) found that both men and women rely on their friends for emotional support more than on any other sources, with parents following friends as a source of supp ort. Another review found that women received more social support from their families and friends, whereas men received more support from their lovers and social networks (Burrell, 2002). Jordan ’s research revealed that, particularly for men, l iving alone was the main predictive factor of who would find coping with marital separation difficult (1988).

As for physical health, men seem to suffer the most. Habits reinforced by daily routines have been shown to be disrupted by divorce. This may occur because divorce seems to change the traditional role elements of men and women. More meals were skipped and subjects ate out more often than they had when they were married (Huddleston & Hawkings, 1991). For men, marriage in itself may be a source of social support. Lee’s review of the research revealed that this support may be a key in the maintenance of healthy beh aviors. In her review it was found that married men had more servings of vegetables each week, less fried food, and were less likely to smoke (2001).

Men are usually confronted with greater emotional adjustment problems than women. Zeiss (1980) found in his study of sex differences in divorce adjustment that men seemed to have more difficulty moving away from the past marriage, and they experienced less improvement in their general adjustment than women. For example, on the Divorce Survey Adjustment Scale, women seemed happier than men to be out of their former marriages. They initiate divorce more often and they had more ne gative feelings toward their ex-husbands than men had toward their ex-wives.

Women’s better adjustment may be due, in part, to the fact that women are more likely to notice marital problems and to feel relief when such problems end. In her dissertation, Bevvino (2000) found that only women identified the categories of “physical, emotional, or sexual abuse” whereas only men chose the category of “unknown” to identify the cause of their divorce.

In general, divorce is associated with increased depression for both women and men. People experience the loss of a partner, hopes and dreams, and lifestyles. No two people share the exact same experience, nor do they react the same way to the same event. As the above research reveals, however, there are patterns that can be helpful for the counselor, family life educator, or just a friend to know when assisting men and women to cope with divorce.
