  Caregiving
    Life expectancy and the aging of our population have increased dramatically. The majority of caregivin g involves the elderly with dementia, Alzheimer’s disease, children with chronic diseases, and the mentally ill, especially the Schizophrenic. However, the decrease in accidental deaths has also increased the number of individuals with limitations in mobility and functional activity (Biegel & Schulz, 1999). With the growth of the disabled population comes the increased number of caregivers in our society. 

Caregiving is a role that is not always ant icipated and involves a significant amount of time and energy with tasks that are often uncomfortable and unpleasant (Biegel & Schulz, 1999). Each situation has different issues and challenges. To be a caregiver for a 38-year-old quadriplegic husband holds distinct and complex issues unlike the previously stated situations. Role elimination and additions revolve around every aspect of family life. Issues of providing income, participation in children’s lives, household tasks, nurturing a marital relatio nship and future security for the family is impacted by the disability.

Boundary ambiguity, not knowing who is in or who is out of the family (Boss, 2002), becomes a long-term issue. Life satisfaction is diminished after disability as spinal cord injuries affect most major life activities and demand profound changes to cope (Chase, Cornille, & English, 2000). Family members' perceptions of the situati on will be key to managing the family stress.
