  Family Perceptions: Yours, Mine and Ours
    Families have individual and sh ared perceptions to stressful events. For example, family members perceive the birth of a child differently. 

The new mother may feel overjoyed by the birth of her child yet feel overwhelmed by the sudden responsibilities she faces. Her older child may perceive the new baby as a threat to the previously formed family relationships, while being excited at the new arrival. The family as a whole may celebrate the birth while adjusting to their new structur e. 

Pauline Boss (2002) defines family perception “as the group’s unified view of a particular stressor event or situation” (p. 23). Individual perceptions must be acknowledged in order to define the overall family perception. It is helpful when the family can have a general understanding of the impact of the stressor on the family unit. However, this is not always achieved. With the addition of a new baby some mothers who experience post partum dep ression can move past the transition while others do not. A family in this situation may justify the mother’s depression as “just having the blues.” This may lead to a family whose view is not unified. 

In order to understand family stress, family member should be aware of the “yours, mine and ours” perceptions of stressful events. 

Reference

Boss, P. (2002). Family stress management: A contextual approach (2nd ed.). Thousand Oaks, CA: Sage.
