  Stress Pile Up!
    Author Pauline Boss (2002) states that family stress increases when "we are being made witness to events over which we have no control" (p.149). These external circumstances often add to an already difficult situation. 

After fourteen months of numerous medical treatments, a quadriplegic father still suffered from severe muscle spasms. His medical doctors had exhausted all but one option that would ease his spasms. That last option was experimental surgery. Eleven quadriplegics in the U.S. had received positive outcomes from such surgery. If the me dical team accepted him as a candidate, he would be the twelfth quadriplegic to use an implanted medicine pump to control his muscle spasms.

But during this time the quadriplegic's former employer changed insurance providers. The quadriplegic and his family were dropped from the insurance plan. The much-needed surgery would cost $30,000 and now he had no medical insurance.

The loss of insurance was be yond the control of the quadriplegic, his family, and his medical doctors. The surgery that could improve the quality of life for the quadriplegic and his family now seemed like a dream. An already difficult, stressful situation left the family feeling violated, confused and desperate. 

The family's ability to manage stress can be complicated by the "constraints imposed by such external forces" (p.149), leaving the family with feelings of anger and bewilderment.

Reference

Boss, P. (2002). Family stress management: A contextual approach (2nd ed.). Thousand Oaks, CA: Sage.
